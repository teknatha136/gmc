04 BA — Common Misconceptions

"Business Analyst" sounds like many things

And most of them are not what a software BA does.

❌ "BA = Finance / Business Intelligence analyst"

Someone who reads financial reports, builds dashboards, analyses revenue trends.

Reality: A software BA rarely touches money numbers. They analyse what a product should do, not how the business is performing.
❌ "BA = Project Manager"

Tracks deadlines, runs standups, updates the Gantt chart, chases people.

Reality: BA worries about what we build. PM worries about when we ship it. Different crafts, often different people.
❌ "BA = Data Analyst / Data Scientist"

Writes SQL, trains models, finds patterns in data.

Reality: BA may read data schemas and look at analytics, but their output is specs — not insights or models.
❌ "BA = Excellent English speaker who translates meetings"

Sits in meetings, writes minutes, forwards emails.

Reality: Writing is tooling, not the job. The job is understanding and defining the software.
05 QA — Common Misconceptions

"Quality Assurance" gets caricatured a lot

Let's dismantle the stereotypes before they stick.

❌ "QA is the person who just clicks buttons"

Logs into the app, clicks around, files a bug when something looks off.

Reality: QAs design tests — figuring out what to check and why. Clicking is the last 10% of the job, and even that is often automated now.
❌ "QA is the last line of defence before release"

Everything is fine until QA catches it.

Reality: Quality is built in, not tested in. QAs start working the day a requirement is written — not the day code lands.
❌ "QA is the enemy of developers"

Trying to find bugs and make devs look bad.

Reality: Good QAs and good devs are allies. They share one goal: a product users don't complain about.
❌ "QA is a junior role you grow out of"

A starter position on the way to becoming a developer.

Reality: QA is its own career, with its own senior and principal tracks — especially automation, performance, security QA.

Part One

The Business

The person who makes sure the team builds the right thing.

01
07 BA · The 10-second definition

A BA discovers what users need, negotiates what the team will build, and writes it down clearly enough that developers — and AI — can build it.

They live between two worlds: the world of users and stakeholders (messy, uncertain, political) and the world of engineering (precise, logical, deadline-driven). Their job is translation.

08 BA · A typical day

What a BA actually does, hour by hour

☀️ Morning

  • 09:00Stand-up with the dev + QA team. Clarify questions raised yesterday.
  • 09:30Meet a stakeholder (product owner, customer, support lead). Ask: "what problem are we trying to solve this quarter?"
  • 10:30Pull recent support tickets + usage analytics. Look for patterns.
  • 11:00Write a draft user story for a new feature. Send to dev for feasibility check.

🌙 Afternoon

  • 13:00Workshop with Design: walk through wireframes, flag edge cases and missing states.
  • 14:30Update the spec document. Add acceptance criteria. Ping QA for a review.
  • 15:30Attend a UAT (User Acceptance Testing) session with a customer. Take notes.
  • 16:30File backlog items for the next sprint. Write a short release note for the feature going live tomorrow.

Notice: they talk to people, write documents, and review drafts. They don't write code, but they understand the product deeply enough to spec it.

09 BA · Artifacts they produce

A BA is measured by the quality of these documents

User Story
Short · Sprint-level
"As a shopkeeper, I want to save billers as favourites so I don't re-type account numbers." One feature, one story.
Acceptance Criteria
Testable · Given/When/Then
The pass/fail rules for a story. "Given I've paid a biller, when I tap ⭐, then it appears in Favourites."
SRS / Scope Document
Long-form · Project-level
A Software Requirements Specification. Describes the whole product or module. Often 20–60 pages.
Process Flow (BPMN)
Diagram · Visual
Boxes + arrows showing how work moves through the system, with swim lanes per actor.
Wireframe
Low-fi sketch · Communication
A rough picture of the UI so everyone agrees where buttons go. Not a design — a conversation.
Backlog Item
Jira / Linear ticket
A single card with everything a developer needs to pick it up: description, AC, priority, links.
10 BA · Skills that matter

What makes a BA good — and what makes one great

The craft

  • Listening — hearing what stakeholders mean, not just what they say
  • Asking why — 5 times if needed
  • Writing clearly — short sentences, precise verbs
  • Reading critically — spotting gaps in specs before they hit dev
  • Drawing to think — wireframes, flows, not art

The edge (great BAs)

  • Systems thinking — seeing how one change affects three things
  • Saying no — protecting scope against stakeholder wishes
  • Data literacy — reading funnels, ERDs, API contracts
  • Negotiation — trading off time, scope, quality openly
  • Empathy for devs & QAs — respecting their time and constraints
11 BA · Career shape

Where BAs come from — and where they go

Common paths in

  • From customer support — knows users' pain viscerally
  • From domain work (banking, healthcare, telecom)
  • From QA — already reads specs, tightly trained on edge cases
  • From Dev — tired of coding, loves product
  • Fresh graduates with strong writing + analytical skills

Senior paths

  • Senior / Lead BA — owns a product area
  • Product Owner / Product Manager — owns the "why" too
  • Solution Architect — pairs with engineering leads
  • Consultant — parachutes into client projects to spec them out
  • Head of Product / CPO — some BAs rise to the top of product orgs

Part Two

Quality
Assurance

The person who makes sure the team builds the thing right.

02
13 QA · The 10-second definition

A QA designs tests from a spec, runs them manually or automated, reports what fails clearly, and argues for quality when schedule pressure grows.

Their superpower is distrust — the productive kind. A good QA assumes everything is broken until proven otherwise, and their tools prove it one scenario at a time.

14 QA · A typical day

What a QA actually does, hour by hour

☀️ Morning

  • 09:00Stand-up with dev + BA. Check overnight automated test results — flag any new failures.
  • 09:30Read a new user story the BA just drafted. Write acceptance-criteria questions back ("what happens if the user has 10 already?").
  • 10:30Design test cases for a story entering dev tomorrow — happy path, 3 edge cases, negative flows.
  • 11:30Pull a build from a feature branch. Run exploratory tests — no script, just poke around.

🌙 Afternoon

  • 13:00File a bug report from the morning's exploratory session. Attach screenshots, steps, severity.
  • 14:00Write a Playwright automation test for a regression found last week — so it never escapes again.
  • 15:00Pair with dev to reproduce a tricky bug from production.
  • 16:00Run the regression suite before the release. Give thumbs-up or file blocker.

Notice: reading specs happens before any clicking. The clicking itself is half manual, half automation code.

15 QA · Artifacts they produce

A QA is measured by the quality of these

Test Case
Structured · Repeatable
Precondition → Steps → Expected result. One per scenario. Written once, run many times.
Bug Report
Actionable · Unambiguous
Title, steps, expected vs actual, environment, severity, priority, attachment. A dev should reproduce it on the first read.
Test Plan
Strategy · Release-level
"For this release, what do we test, how, with what coverage, and when do we stop?"
Automation Script
Code · Checked into git
Playwright / Cypress / Selenium code. Runs on every commit. Catches regressions without human time.
Traceability Matrix
Coverage proof
A mapping from every requirement → the tests that cover it. Proves no AC was forgotten.
Release Sign-off
Go / no-go call
A short, honest report: what's tested, what's flaky, what's broken. Basis for release decisions.
16 QA · Skills that matter

What separates a solid QA from a great one

The craft

  • Destructive curiosity — "what if I do the wrong thing?"
  • Reading specs — same skill as BA, opposite direction
  • Test design techniques — boundary, equivalence, decision tables
  • Clear writing — a bug report that lands first time
  • Patience — regression, retest, retest again

The edge (great QAs)

  • Coding — at minimum, JavaScript/TypeScript for Playwright
  • API & DB fluency — test the back-end, not just the UI
  • Automation architecture — tests that don't flake or rot
  • Security & performance instincts — NFRs covered routinely
  • Courage to push back — "this shouldn't ship yet" and explain why
17 QA · Career shape

Where QAs come from — and where they go

Common paths in

  • From support / user-ops — already sees what breaks
  • From dev — some engineers love the quality craft
  • From BA — strong spec-reading, now learning testing
  • Fresh grads with strong attention to detail + basic scripting

Senior paths

  • SDET — Software Dev Engineer in Test (strong coder)
  • Automation Lead — owns the test pyramid + CI
  • Performance / Security QA — deep specialists
  • QA Manager / Head of Quality — culture + strategy
  • DevOps / SRE — quality concerns → reliability engineering

Part Four

AI Changes
Both Roles

Neither is replaced. Both are leveraged.

04
22 What AI changes

A BA writes fewer words. A QA runs fewer clicks.

In both cases, AI amplifies the craft. It doesn't do the craft.

📋 BA in the AI era
What gets easier
  • Drafting user stories from a rough description
  • Generating acceptance criteria variations
  • Turning a meeting transcript into a spec draft
  • Cross-checking a spec against itself for contradictions
What gets harder (higher bar)
  • Spec clarity — AI now writes code from it, so ambiguity is immediately expensive
  • Review — catching what AI got wrong in generated drafts
  • Judgment — knowing which stories matter and which are noise
🔍 QA in the AI era
What gets easier
  • Generating baseline test cases from a spec
  • Writing Playwright automation scripts faster
  • Summarising bug logs and error traces
  • Suggesting edge cases you might have missed
What gets harder (higher bar)
  • Test design intuition — what to test, not how to type it
  • Exploratory testing — the creative part AI is worst at
  • Reviewing AI-generated tests — many will be wrong or trivial

🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ BA makes sure we build the right thing — discovers needs, writes specs
  • ✅ QA makes sure we build the thing right — designs and runs tests, protects release quality
  • ✅ Both read specs critically; both write testable AC; both protect users
