Part 1

Why we start here

In your 1-on-1s with the training team, many of you said versions of: "I thought BA was financial analysis" or "I thought QA was just clicking buttons." That's not a failure on your part — the job titles are genuinely misleading. "Business Analyst" could mean five different things in five different industries. "Quality Assurance" sounds like the last step in a factory line.

In software, both mean something specific. Today's class makes that specificity concrete. By the end, you should be able to describe both roles to a friend in one sentence each — and name three things each role actually produces in a typical work week.

The big idea today

A BA makes sure the team builds the right thing. A QA makes sure the team builds the thing right. Two different jobs, two different crafts, one shared goal: software that works for the users who paid for it.

Part 2

Misconceptions worth clearing

Before we describe what BAs and QAs do, it's worth explicitly naming what they don't. These misconceptions come up often enough that if we skip them, they'll keep coloring everything else in your head.

BA — four common confusions

"BA = Finance / Business Intelligence analyst"

Reads financial reports, builds revenue dashboards, analyses money trends.

Reality: A software BA rarely touches money numbers. Their "analysis" is of what the product should do, not how the business is performing.
"BA = Project Manager"

Tracks deadlines, runs standups, chases people.

Reality: BA cares about what gets built; PM cares about when. Different crafts, often different people.
"BA = Data Analyst"

Writes SQL, explores data, finds insights.

Reality: A BA may read schemas and look at usage analytics to inform decisions, but their output is a spec — not an insight report.
"BA = meeting-minute writer"

Attends meetings, types up notes, forwards emails.

Reality: Writing is tooling. The job is understanding and defining the software. Notes without judgment are secretarial, not analytical.

QA — four common confusions

"QA is the button-clicker"

Clicks around, logs bugs when something looks off.

Reality: QAs design tests — deciding what to check and why. Clicking is the last 10% of the job, often automated.
"QA is the last line of defence"

Everything is fine until QA catches it.

Reality: Quality is built in, not tested in. QAs start the day a requirement is drafted — not the day code lands.
"QA is the enemy of developers"

Trying to make devs look bad.

Reality: Good QAs and devs are allies. One shared goal: a product users don't complain about.
"QA is a junior step to becoming a developer"

A stepping-stone you grow out of.

Reality: QA has its own senior and principal tracks — automation, performance, security — with comparable pay to development.
Part 3 · The BA

Who the BA is

A BA discovers what users need, negotiates what the team will build, and writes it down clearly enough that developers — and AI — can build it.

They live between two worlds. On one side are users and stakeholders: people with problems, preferences, politics, and opinions that often contradict each other. On the other side are engineers: people whose work demands precision, unambiguous input, and stable ground. A BA's job is to translate faithfully between these worlds — turning messy, contradictory human desires into clear, testable requirements the team can act on.

This is genuinely hard. It's not enough to listen and transcribe. You have to listen, interpret, challenge, negotiate, and write — all in a way that respects both sides of the conversation.

Part 4 · The BA

A day in the life

Here's what a reasonably typical BA day looks like on a product team. Times are illustrative — the point is the kind of work, not the schedule.

☀️ Morning
  • 09:00Stand-up with dev + QA. Clarify any questions raised yesterday.
  • 09:30Meet a stakeholder — a product owner, customer, or support lead. Ask "what problem are we solving this quarter?"
  • 10:30Pull recent support tickets and usage analytics. Look for patterns you can use as evidence.
  • 11:00Draft a user story for a new feature. Send it to a developer for a feasibility check.
🌙 Afternoon
  • 13:00Workshop with Design — walk through wireframes, flag edge cases and missing states.
  • 14:30Update the spec document. Add acceptance criteria. Ping QA for a review.
  • 15:30Sit in on a UAT session with a customer. Observe + take notes.
  • 16:30File backlog items for the next sprint. Write a short release note for the feature shipping tomorrow.

Notice the shape: conversations + writing + reviewing drafts. A BA doesn't write code, but they understand the product deeply enough to spec it — and to spot when what a stakeholder asked for doesn't actually solve the real problem.

Part 5 · The BA

The artifacts a BA produces

A BA is measured by the quality of the documents they produce — not by meeting attendance or word count. Here are the six you'll see most often. Later classes cover each in depth.

User Story

"As a <who>, I want <what> so <why>." One feature, one story, fits on a card.

Acceptance Criteria

Given / When / Then. Pass-fail rules that anchor both dev and QA work.

SRS / Scope Document

Long-form. Describes a whole product or module. 20–60 pages is normal.

Process Flow (BPMN)

Boxes and arrows. Swim lanes per actor. Shows how work moves through the system.

Wireframe

Low-fi UI sketch. Not visual design — a tool for agreeing where things go.

Backlog Item

Jira or Linear ticket. A single card with everything a dev needs to pick it up.

A BA's "code" is documents

If you imagine a developer's output as lines of code that ship, a BA's output is paragraphs of specification that ship. The quality standards are just as high — precision matters, and ambiguity has real costs downstream.

Part 6 · The BA

Skills & career shape

The craft

  • Listening — hearing what stakeholders mean, not just what they say.
  • Asking why — five times if you need to. The first answer is rarely the real one.
  • Writing clearly — short sentences. Precise verbs. No hedging.
  • Reading critically — spotting gaps in specs before they reach dev. (This is our next class.)
  • Drawing to think — wireframes and flows are thinking tools, not art.

The edge — what makes a great BA

  • Systems thinking — seeing how a change in one screen affects three others.
  • Saying no — protecting scope from stakeholder wishlists.
  • Data literacy — reading ERDs, API contracts, usage funnels.
  • Negotiation — trading off time, scope, and quality openly and honestly.
  • Empathy for dev & QA — respecting their time and constraints.

Where BAs come from

  • From customer support — they already know user pain.
  • From domain work — banking, healthcare, telecom backgrounds bring deep context.
  • From QA — already trained in reading specs and spotting edge cases.
  • From Dev — engineers who fall in love with product work.
  • Fresh grads with strong writing and analytical skills.

Where BAs go

  • Senior / Lead BA — owns a whole product area.
  • Product Owner / Product Manager — owns the "why" in addition to the "what".
  • Solution Architect — pairs tightly with engineering leads.
  • Consultant — drops into client projects to spec them out.
  • Head of Product / CPO — some BAs climb to the top of product orgs.
Part 7 · The QA

Who the QA is

A QA designs tests from a spec, runs them manually or automated, reports what fails clearly, and argues for quality when schedule pressure grows.

Their superpower is productive distrust. A good QA walks into every feature assuming something is broken and needs their tools to prove otherwise — one scenario at a time. That's not cynicism; it's discipline. Software is complicated, humans make mistakes, and every unchecked assumption is a future bug.

If a BA's job is making sure we build the right thing, a QA's job is making sure we build the thing right. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

Part 8 · The QA

A day in the life

☀️ Morning
  • 09:00Stand-up with dev + BA. Check overnight automation results — flag any new failures.
  • 09:30Read a new user story the BA drafted. Write back acceptance-criteria questions ("what happens if there are 10 already?").
  • 10:30Design test cases for a story entering dev tomorrow — happy path, three edge cases, a couple of negative flows.
  • 11:30Pull a build from a feature branch. Run exploratory tests — no script, just curiosity.
🌙 Afternoon
  • 13:00File a bug report from the morning's exploratory session. Attach screenshots, clean steps, severity.
  • 14:00Write a Playwright automation test for a regression found last week — so it can't escape again.
  • 15:00Pair with a developer to reproduce a tricky bug from production.
  • 16:00Run the regression suite before release. Give a thumbs-up or file a blocker.

Notice the shape: reading specs → designing tests → running tests → writing up findings. The clicking part happens, but it's half of the day at most — and increasingly, it's automated.

Part 9 · The QA

The artifacts a QA produces

Like a BA, a QA is measured by the quality of what they produce. Six staples:

Test Case

Precondition → Steps → Expected result. One per scenario. Written once, run many times.

Bug Report

Title, steps, expected vs actual, environment, severity, priority. A dev should reproduce it on first read.

Test Plan

"For this release — what do we test, how, with what coverage, and when do we stop?"

Automation Script

Playwright / Cypress / Selenium code. Runs on every commit. Catches regressions cheaply.

Traceability Matrix

Requirement → tests that cover it. Proves nothing was forgotten.

Release Sign-off

"Here's what's tested, what's flaky, what's broken." Basis for go/no-go calls.

Part 10 · The QA

Skills & career shape

The craft

  • Destructive curiosity — "what if I do the wrong thing?"
  • Reading specs — same skill as a BA, used in the opposite direction.
  • Test design techniques — boundary values, equivalence partitioning, decision tables, state transitions.
  • Clear writing — a bug report that lands the first time.
  • Patience — regression, retest, retest again.

The edge — what makes a great QA

  • Coding — at minimum, JavaScript/TypeScript for Playwright. Senior QAs code comfortably.
  • API & DB fluency — testing the back-end, not just the visible UI.
  • Automation architecture — tests that don't flake or rot over time.
  • Security & performance instincts — covering non-functional angles as a matter of routine.
  • Courage to push back — "this shouldn't ship yet" and a calm explanation why.

Where QAs come from

  • From support / user-ops — already sees what breaks in the wild.
  • From dev — some engineers love the quality craft.
  • From BA — strong spec-reading turned toward verification.
  • Fresh grads with attention to detail and basic scripting ability.

Where QAs go

  • SDET — Software Dev Engineer in Test. Strong coder in a QA title.
  • Automation Lead — owns the test pyramid and CI strategy.
  • Performance / Security QA — deep specialists.
  • QA Manager / Head of Quality — culture, strategy, team.
  • DevOps / SRE — quality concerns extended into reliability engineering.
Part 11

Where BA and QA meet

Every feature passes through a chain of hands. Each handoff is a translation, and every translation is a chance for meaning to be lost. BA sits near the start; QA sits near the end.

👤 Stakeholder"We need a thing."
🧭 PM"Do we, and why now?"
📋 BASpec · AC · wireframes
🎨 DesignerVisual & flow
💻 DeveloperBuilds from spec
🔍 QAVerifies against AC
🚀 ReleaseUsers

Despite being far apart in the chain, BA and QA share a lot:

  • Both read specs critically and spot gaps before they become bugs.
  • Both write testable acceptance criteria — BAs author them, QAs extend them into full test cases.
  • Both protect the user — BA by making sure we build the right thing, QA by making sure it works.
  • Both have to say "not yet" when things aren't ready — and say it with evidence.

The AC bridge

The single strongest overlap is acceptance criteria. A well-written AC from a BA becomes the first draft of a QA's test case. That handoff is why Classes 4 and onwards in this combined block will spend serious time on AC quality.

Part 12

How AI changes both roles

AI hasn't replaced either role. It has amplified them — and raised the bar for what competent work looks like. In both cases, the typing part gets faster and the judgment part gets more valuable.

📋 BA in the AI era
What gets easier
  • Drafting user stories from a rough description.
  • Generating variations of acceptance criteria.
  • Turning a meeting transcript into a spec draft.
  • Cross-checking a long spec for internal contradictions.
What gets harder (higher bar)
  • Spec clarity — AI generates code from it, so ambiguity is immediately expensive.
  • Review — catching what AI got wrong in drafts it produced.
  • Judgment — knowing which stories matter and which are noise.
🔍 QA in the AI era
What gets easier
  • Generating baseline test cases from a spec.
  • Writing Playwright automation scripts faster.
  • Summarising bug logs and error traces.
  • Suggesting edge cases you might miss.
What gets harder (higher bar)
  • Test design intuition — deciding what to test, not how to type it.
  • Exploratory testing — the creative part AI is worst at.
  • Reviewing AI-generated tests — many will be wrong, trivial, or duplicate existing coverage.

The shift

In both roles, typing speed matters less and thinking quality matters more. The people who thrive will be the ones who can judge AI output — not the ones who are fastest at prompting it.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • A BA makes sure we build the right thing — discovers user needs, writes and negotiates specs.
  • A QA makes sure we build the thing right — designs and runs tests, protects release quality.
  • Both roles start earlier in a feature's life than most people assume.
  • Both roles are measured by the quality of their artifacts, not hours or clicks.
  • Both roles share spec-reading, AC writing, and user-protection as core skills.
  • AI amplifies both — typing matters less, judgment matters more.
  • The big one: BA is not finance. QA is not clicking. Both are thinking jobs.
