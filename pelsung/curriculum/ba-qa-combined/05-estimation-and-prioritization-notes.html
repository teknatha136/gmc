Part 1

Why this matters

There is always more to build than you have time for. Always. Every project, every sprint, every company. The interesting question isn't "can we do it all?" — the answer is always no. It's "what should we do first, and how long will it take?"

That is prioritization and estimation. They aren't the same skill — one tells you which features win, the other tells you how big each one is — but they're always paired, because one without the other is useless.

BA + QA angle

BAs run the prioritization conversation with stakeholders. QAs contribute effort estimates for testing, and push back when a feature's scope grows without its quality bar growing too. Both need to speak these languages fluently.

Part 2

MoSCoW · the simplest prioritization method

Every feature goes into one of four buckets. The capital letters spell it: Must, Should, Could, Won't. The little o's are padding to make the word pronounceable.

M
Must-have

Without it, the release fails. Unshippable without this.

S
Should-have

Important but release can ship without it. Pain but not fatal.

C
Could-have

Nice if there's budget. Nobody will complain if it's missing.

W
Won't-have

Explicitly not in this release. Say it out loud — prevents scope creep.

Why Won't-have matters

Most MoSCoW lists have M, S, C. The "W" column is the one teams skip — and then spend the next three sprints arguing about whether those features were "supposed to be in there." Write them down. Get stakeholder agreement. Put a date on when they'll be reconsidered.

The honesty test

A real MoSCoW list has at most ~60% of features marked Must-have. If everything is a Must, you haven't prioritized — you've just labelled. Go back and force yourself to pick.

When to use MoSCoW

  • Stakeholder conversations — easy to say out loud.
  • Quick kickoff workshops — sticky notes on a whiteboard.
  • Release planning — slotting work into a fixed window.
  • Not for comparing two similar features head-to-head — both might be "Should", and you need a tie-breaker.
Part 3

RICE · when you need numbers

RICE is what you reach for when MoSCoW runs out of resolution. It's a score, not a label — so you can rank features that are all "Must" or all "Should".

RICE Score  =  (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort

What each letter means

  • Reach — how many people are affected per time window. "12,000 users per month."
  • Impact — how big the effect is per person. A common scale: 0.25 (tiny) · 0.5 (small) · 1 (medium) · 2 (high) · 3 (massive).
  • Confidence — how sure you are of the first three numbers. A common scale: 50% (gut feel) · 80% (some data) · 100% (hard data).
  • Effort — how many person-months of work. "0.5" = two weeks of one engineer.

Example · four features competing for one sprint

FeatureReachImpactConfidenceEffortScore
Biller favourites8,0002100%116,000
Bank statement PDF export3,0001100%1.52,000
Dark mode12,0000.580%22,400
Split bill with friends2,000350%31,000

Biller favourites wins — high reach, proven impact, well-known solution (high confidence), low effort. Notice how "split bill" sounded flashy but scored last: reach was narrow, and the low confidence penalised the optimistic impact score.

The virtue of RICE

It doesn't tell you the truth. It forces a transparent conversation. Everyone can see why a feature ranked where it did — reach, impact, confidence, effort. When people disagree, they disagree on specific numbers, not on gut preference. That's always a productive argument.

When RICE breaks down

  • Strategic/foundational work — "replatform the payments service" scores badly on RICE because reach feels abstract. Use judgment, not the formula.
  • Compliance / legal — must-do regardless of score. RICE doesn't apply to "we must encrypt all PII by law."
  • Small teams with small decision surfaces — RICE is overkill for a 5-feature sprint. Use MoSCoW.
Part 4

MVP thinking

MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product. Both words matter equally:

  • Minimum — the smallest scope you can build.
  • Viable — it actually works for the core use case. People can use it and you can learn from it.

Most teams accidentally drop one of the two words. Dropping "Minimum" gives you a bloated first release. Dropping "Viable" gives you a broken, half-baked thing that teaches you nothing because no one could use it.

The one-sentence test

If you can't describe your MVP in one sentence, it's not an MVP — it's a wish list. Cut scope until it fits in a sentence.

Example · three sizings of the same feature

  • ❌ Too minimum: "Users can 'save' — but favourites never actually show up yet." (Ships. Teaches nothing. Annoys users.)
  • ✅ Right-sized MVP: "Save a biller. See it on home. Tap to pre-fill. That's it." (Ships in 2 weeks. You learn if users want it.)
  • 😬 Too maximum: "Save, categorise, share, auto-pay, export, notify family…" (Six months of work. Most of it unused.)

MVP isn't about being cheap or lazy. It's about being honest — shipping the smallest thing that lets real users give you real signal, so you can invest the next round of effort where it actually matters.

Part 5

Estimation · relative sizing

Humans are famously bad at absolute estimates. "How many hours will this take?" is the wrong question — our answers are off by 2× to 4× more often than they aren't. But humans are good at comparisons: "is this bigger or smaller than that other task?" is much easier to answer accurately.

That's why modern teams estimate in relative sizes, not hours.

Story points — a Fibonacci-like scale

1
tiny
2
small
3
sm-med
5
medium
8
large
13
very large
20+
break up

The gaps widen as size grows, reflecting the reality that big tasks are always more uncertain than small ones. A task that feels like "20 points" should almost always be broken into smaller pieces before being committed to.

T-shirt sizing — for stakeholders

Story points are great in a dev standup, but stakeholders tune out the moment you say "13 points." T-shirt sizes (XS · S · M · L · XL · XXL) are a warmer language for outside-the-team conversations. Roughly:

  • XS — a few hours
  • S — a day or two
  • M — about a week
  • L — two to three weeks
  • XL — a month or more
  • XXL — unknown; needs a spike (short investigation) before it can even be estimated

Planning poker

When a team estimates together, avoid anchor bias: have everyone write their estimate privately, reveal simultaneously, and discuss the outliers. Whoever said "3" and whoever said "13" both know something interesting about the task — and the conversation between them is where the real estimate emerges.

Part 6

Six common estimation traps

If you've ever been off by 2× or more on an estimate, it was almost certainly one of these:

🎯 Anchor bias

The first person to say a number sets everyone else's expectation. "I think two days" — now nobody will say seven, because they'll feel like an outlier.

Fix: write estimates privately first, reveal simultaneously. Planning poker does this by construction.
😎 Happy-path blindness

You estimated the main flow and forgot: error handling, edge cases, code review, QA, bug fixes, deployment, documentation.

Fix: when estimating, explicitly ask "does this number include testing, review, and documentation?" If not, add 30–50%.
🌫️ Dependency blindness

"It's 3 days" — yes, but it depends on an API that doesn't exist yet, a design that hasn't been reviewed, and a decision nobody has made.

Fix: list dependencies before estimating. If a dependency isn't ready, the task is unestimable until it is.
🧠 Optimism bias

You estimated assuming everything goes right. Nothing ever goes 100% right.

Fix: compare to your last 5 similar tasks. Use your actual velocity, not your hope.
💢 Pressure to please

Stakeholder says "we need this by Friday." Team says "sure, Friday." It arrives three Fridays later.

Fix: separate estimate from commitment. Estimates are honest; commitments are negotiated. If the business needs Friday, something else has to give.
📦 Single-number fallacy

"It'll take 5 days." Treated as truth. Reality is a range, not a point.

Fix: estimate ranges ("3 to 7 days, most likely 5") or confidence intervals. Ranges keep the honesty about uncertainty.
Part 7 · Hands-on

Exercise · 6 features, 1 sprint

You have 10 story points of dev capacity for this sprint. Prioritize (MoSCoW) + estimate (story points) and pick the features that fit.

  1. Login with phone + MPIN — required to use the app at all. Well-understood pattern.
  2. Biller favourites — high user demand. Uses existing biller data model. Small UI addition.
  3. PDF export of bank statement — enterprise users ask for it. Needs a new PDF library. Moderate new code.
  4. Dark mode — widespread user request. Requires rethinking every colour in the app.
  5. Support for Dzongkha in notes field — currently broken — Dzongkha characters corrupt on save. Legal/compliance concern.
  6. In-app chat with customer support — support team would love it. No integration exists with current helpdesk. Large effort.

A reasonable answer

Nobody's answer is "the" right answer here — but a defensible one looks roughly like this:

  • Login — Must, 3 pts. Foundational.
  • Dzongkha fix — Must, 2 pts. Compliance; currently broken.
  • Biller favourites — Should, 3 pts. Highest bang-for-buck per RICE.
  • PDF export — Could, 5 pts. Useful but can wait.
  • Dark mode — Could, 8 pts. Popular but expensive; not essential.
  • In-app chat — Won't (this sprint), 13+ pts. Too big; needs its own sprint or platform work first.

Sprint fit: Login (3) + Dzongkha (2) + Favourites (3) = 8 of 10 points. Leaves buffer — which is wise, because (see traps above) single-number estimates are always optimistic.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • There is always more to build than time. Prioritization is a permanent activity.
  • MoSCoW — simplest method, great for stakeholder conversations. Four buckets: Must, Should, Could, Won't.
  • RICE — numerical scoring for head-to-head feature comparisons. (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort.
  • MVP — the smallest viable thing. One sentence or it's a wish list.
  • Estimate in relative sizes — story points with devs, t-shirts with stakeholders.
  • Watch for traps: anchoring, happy-path, dependencies, optimism, pressure, single-number.
  • Separate estimate from commitment — they're not the same conversation.

If everything is a Must, you haven't prioritized — you've labelled.
