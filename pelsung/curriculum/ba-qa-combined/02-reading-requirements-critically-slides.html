03 Hook
🎤 Quick challenge

Read this requirement for 10 seconds.
How many questions would you ask before coding it?

"The system should allow users to upload documents.
The upload should be fast and secure.
Users should be notified once it's done."

If your honest answer is "ten or more," you already have the instinct we're going to sharpen today.

04 Why this matters

A bug caught early is 100× cheaper than one caught late

This is the Boehm curve, named after the researcher who first measured it. The cost of fixing a requirements-level mistake grows by roughly 10× at each stage it goes undetected.

1×

5×

10×

A question asked at the spec stage costs 1 minute. The same issue discovered in production may cost a release, a customer, and a weekend of firefighting.

05 Who reads requirements critically?

Reading specs critically is the shared skill

📋 BA lens
Reads to improve
  • Is the problem clearly stated? Is it worth solving?
  • Are the users named? Do we know them?
  • Are assumptions written down or hiding?
  • Is the scope explicit — including what's out?
  • Does it contradict another spec in the same product?
  • Are there gaps stakeholders didn't think to mention?
🔍 QA lens
Reads to test
  • Can I write a pass/fail test for each statement?
  • Are edge cases defined — or only the happy path?
  • Are non-functional expectations named (performance, security…)?
  • What happens when inputs are invalid?
  • Is the error behaviour specified?
  • Can two testers reading this arrive at the same verdict?

Same artefact, two lenses. BAs read to improve the spec; QAs read to test against it. Both find the same problems — often first.

The Seven Sins
of Requirements

How written specs fail. And what to ask when they do.

02
07 The 7 Sins

Every bad spec commits one or more of these

Sin 01
🫥 Ambiguity

Words that sound specific but aren't: "fast", "user-friendly", "as needed", "several".

Sin 02
🧊 Unstated assumptions

Things the writer thinks are obvious. They aren't obvious to anyone else.

Sin 03
♊ Contradictions

Section 2.1 says X, Section 4.3 says not-X. Both somehow survived review.

Sin 04
👤 Missing actors

"The system notifies the user." Which system? Which user? Via what channel?

Sin 05
⚖️ Untestable statements

"Should be intuitive." No QA can verify this yes/no.

Sin 06
🛡️ Missing NFRs

No mention of performance, security, accessibility, reliability, scalability.

Sin 07
♾️ No out-of-scope

The spec only lists what to do. Nothing says what not to do. Scope creep lives here.

Your job
🔎 Hunt all seven

A clean spec commits none. A shippable spec commits at most one — and names it as a known risk.

08 Sin 01 · Ambiguity

Words that sound specific — but aren't

❌ Sin spotted
"The search should return results quickly and show relevant matches."
Questions to ask:
  • "Quickly" — under 200ms? 2 seconds? 10 seconds on a slow connection?
  • "Relevant" — by whose judgment? Exact match, fuzzy, or semantic?
  • What does the user see while waiting? Empty screen? Loader? Skeleton?
  • What if there are 0 matches — is that still "working"?
✅ Fixed version
"The search returns results within 500ms on 4G. Matches include product name (exact or fuzzy, one-character typo tolerated) and SKU (exact only). Below 500ms a skeleton loader is shown; above 5s an error with retry."

Ambiguity is comfortable for the writer and expensive for everyone else.

09 Sin 02 · Unstated assumptions

What the writer thinks is obvious — but isn't

❌ Sin spotted
"When the user logs in, they see their dashboard."
Hidden assumptions:
  • That there is a dashboard. (Which one? Customized or generic?)
  • That "user" means a single type. (What about admin? Deactivated accounts? First login?)
  • That login always succeeds. (What if it's wrong password? Locked account? 2FA?)
  • That "see" means a full page load. (Mobile? Dark mode? Empty state if no data?)

Technique: read it out loud

Reading a sentence out loud exposes assumptions. If you have to add words in your head to make it make sense — those added words are the assumptions. Write them down and ask the author whether they're correct.

10 Sin 03 · Contradictions

Two sections of the same spec disagreeing

Section 2.1 — Registration Users must register with a valid email address. Phone number is optional.
Section 4.3 — Login Users log in with their phone number and OTP. Email login is not supported.
💡 Your job
  • Spot it. Most contradictions survive because nobody reads the whole spec in one sitting.
  • Flag it. Ask which is correct — not "fix it" — because often both sections have a partial story.
  • Don't assume. Both could be wrong. The real answer might be "phone for login, email for password reset."

Long specs hide contradictions like driftwood hides rocks. A BA's re-read and a QA's coverage map are the two things that find them.

11 Sin 04 · Missing actors

Passive voice hides the "who"

❌ Sin spotted
"The approval is sent for review. When approved, the document becomes active."
Questions to ask:
  • Sent by whom? Automatically? Or does a human trigger it?
  • Sent to whom? A specific role? A queue? A distribution list?
  • Who has the power to approve? One reviewer, or quorum of N?
  • "Becomes active" — does it do so instantly, or on next login, or after a cron job?
✅ Fixed — active voice
"When the author clicks Submit, the system notifies all users with role Reviewer in the same department by email. Once any one Reviewer clicks Approve, the system marks the document active within 5 seconds."
12 Sin 05 · Untestable statements

If a QA can't verify yes/no — it's not a requirement

❌ Sin spotted
"The app must be intuitive. Navigation should feel natural. The experience must be delightful."
The test that exposes it: imagine two QAs testing this feature independently. Do they arrive at the same verdict? If the answer is "maybe, depending on taste," you don't have a requirement — you have a mood.
✅ Fixed — measurable
"80% of first-time users complete the sign-up flow without abandoning. The average user can reach the dashboard in 3 taps or fewer from app launch. Core actions (send money, pay bill, view balance) are reachable within 2 taps from the home screen."

Numbers, thresholds, counts — these turn opinions into requirements.

13 Sin 06 · Missing NFRs

The spec says what to build — but says nothing about how well

❌ Sin spotted
"Users can upload their government ID to verify their account."
What's missing:
  • 🏎️ Performance — max file size? upload timeout on 2G?
  • 🔒 Security — encrypted at rest? who can view it? retention period?
  • ♿ Accessibility — screen reader flow? keyboard-only upload?
  • 🔁 Reliability — what happens if upload fails halfway?
  • 📈 Scalability — supports 10,000 uploads/hour during a KYC campaign?

Tomorrow's class (Class 03 — Functional vs Non-Functional) is dedicated to this whole category. For today — just notice when NFRs aren't mentioned at all. Silence is the sin.

14 Sin 07 · No out-of-scope

Every spec needs a "what we're NOT doing" section

Because if you don't say it, someone will assume it's in. Especially stakeholders.

❌ Sin spotted
Spec covers: "Users can save billers as favourites. Favourites show on home. Tap to pre-fill."
What's not said — but will be assumed by somebody:
  • Can I share favourites with family? (Someone's grandma will ask.)
  • Can I categorise them (utilities, school, rent)?
  • Can I set auto-pay from a favourite?
  • Does this work on the old mobile app version?
✅ Fixed — explicit out-of-scope
Out of scope (v1): • Sharing favourites between users • Categorisation / custom tags • Auto-pay from a favourite • Support in the legacy mBoB v4 app

A good out-of-scope list is a deadline-saving device.

How to Annotate
a Spec

A repeatable technique BAs and QAs use.

03
16 The 3-pass annotation

Read the spec three times — for different things each time

Pass 1 · Comprehension

  • Read it end-to-end without stopping
  • Goal: understand the shape of the feature
  • No annotations yet — just absorbing

Pass 2 · Hunt the 7 sins

  • Red — ambiguity, untestable, contradictions
  • Yellow — assumptions, missing actors, missing NFRs
  • Blue — questions for the author

Pass 3 · Testability

Go through every statement and ask: could I write a pass/fail test for this? If the answer is no, it's not a requirement yet — add it to your question list.

The output of the three passes: a clean question list you send back to the author, not just scribbled margins.

Part Four · Hands-on

The Leave-Request
Spec

Find as many sins as you can. Pair up. 15 minutes.

04
18 The spec

Annotate this leave-request requirement

Feature: Online Leave Request 1. Purpose Employees should be able to submit leave requests online. The system will handle the approval process. The solution should be simple and easy to use. 2. Requirements 2.1 Employees can submit a leave request at any time. 2.2 The manager is notified when a leave is submitted. 2.3 The manager approves or rejects the request quickly. 2.4 Once approved, the leave is added to the employee's record. 2.5 The system should be secure. 2.6 Leave balance should be checked automatically. 2.7 Employees can cancel their leave request. If already approved, the manager handles it. 2.8 The system supports all types of leave. 2.9 Performance should be good even when many users are online. 2.10 Employees will see their leave history on the dashboard. 3. Notifications All relevant parties will be notified as appropriate.

Your task: find at least 10 problems — sins, gaps, contradictions, missing NFRs. Write a numbered question list you'd send back to the author.

19 How to work

Pair work · 15 min · then we debrief

Minutes 0–10 · Work in pairs

  • Pair up: one BA-track + one QA-track if possible
  • Read through once together (Pass 1)
  • Hunt the 7 sins (Pass 2)
  • Check testability (Pass 3)
  • Write a numbered question list on paper or laptop

Minutes 10–15 · Compare

  • Swap lists with a nearby pair
  • What did they find that you missed? And vice versa?
  • Combine into a single stronger list
  • Volunteer pairs will share their top 3 findings in debrief

Tip for pairs

If one of you reads the spec to the other out loud, the hidden assumptions and ambiguities tend to fall out within the first pass. Try it.

20 Debrief · the minimum you should have found

A strong question list looks something like this

  1. "Simple and easy to use" (§1) — untestable. What's the target time to submit a leave? How many taps?
  2. "At any time" (§2.1) — literally 24/7 including during a server deploy? Or weekdays 9–5?
  3. "The manager" (§2.2, §2.3) — which manager? Direct line? Skip-level? What if manager is on leave too?
  4. "Approves quickly" (§2.3) — ambiguous. Is there an SLA — 24 hours? Auto-approve after 3 days?
  5. "The system should be secure" (§2.5) — NFR with no detail. Encryption at rest? RBAC? Audit log?
  6. "Leave balance checked automatically" (§2.6) — what if balance is insufficient? Blocked? Warning? Convert to unpaid?
  7. "Manager handles it" (§2.7) — missing actor and flow. What does "handles it" mean — does the employee know?
  8. "All types of leave" (§2.8) — which types exist? Sick, annual, casual, bereavement, maternity, unpaid?
  9. "Performance should be good" (§2.9) — untestable NFR. How many concurrent users? What response time?
  10. "Leave history on the dashboard" (§2.10) — conflicts with no mention of dashboard elsewhere. Does it exist?
  11. "All relevant parties notified as appropriate" (§3) — missing actors, missing channels, missing triggers.
  12. Missing entirely: accessibility, mobile support, Dzongkha text, error handling, edge case of half-day leave, holidays clashing with leave, what happens to the request when an employee resigns.

If you found 10 of these 12 — excellent. If you found 6, you're on the way. If you found 3, today's class paid for itself.
