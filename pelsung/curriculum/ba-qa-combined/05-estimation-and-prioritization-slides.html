Part One

MoSCoW

The simplest prioritization method. Four buckets. Works for stakeholders.

01
05 The four buckets

Every feature goes into one of four categories

The capital letters spell MoSCoW — the little o's are just padding to make it pronounceable.

M
Must-have

Without it, the release fails. Literally unshippable without this.

Login. Payment confirmation. Legal compliance.
S
Should-have

Important but release can ship without it. Pain but not fatal.

Password reset. Transaction history filter. Nice error messages.
C
Could-have

Would be nice. Add if there's budget. No one will complain if it's missing.

Dark mode. Bulk actions. Extra sort options.
W
Won't-have

Explicitly not in this release. Important to say out loud — prevents scope creep.

Offline mode (v2). Admin analytics (v3). Sharing (later).

The honesty test

A real MoSCoW list has at most 60% of features marked Must-have. If everything is a Must, you haven't prioritized — you've labelled.

Part Two

RICE

When you need numbers, not labels.

02
07 The formula

A score = (impact × reach × confidence) ÷ effort

R
Reach
people/month
×
I
Impact
0.25 · 0.5 · 1 · 2 · 3
×
C
Confidence
50% · 80% · 100%
÷
E
Effort
person-months

What each means

  • Reach — how many people does this affect per month?
  • Impact — how big is the effect per person? (tiny = 0.25 ... massive = 3)
  • Confidence — how sure are you of the first three? (50% = guess, 100% = we have data)
  • Effort — how many person-months to build?

Why it's useful

  • Forces stakeholders to be honest about small reach features
  • Penalises expensive features unless impact justifies it
  • Confidence column exposes assumption-driven prioritization
  • Scores are relative — useful for comparing, not absolute truth

Part Three

MVP Thinking

The smallest useful thing you can ship.

03
10 MVP is not a broken product

MVP = Minimum Viable Product

Minimum — the least you can build. Viable — it actually works for the core use case. One of those words often gets dropped.

❌ Too minimum

You shipped a broken, unfinished thing. Users bounce. You learn nothing.

"Users can 'save' — but favourites never actually show up yet."
✅ Right-sized MVP

Smallest scope that lets real users complete the core action and give signal back.

"Save a biller. See it on home. Tap to pre-fill. That's it."
😬 Too maximum

You built everything. Took 6 months. Half of it wasn't used.

"Save, categorise, share, auto-pay, export to Excel, notify family…"

The one-sentence test

If you can't describe your MVP in one sentence, it's not an MVP — it's a wish list. Cut until it fits in a sentence.

Part Five

Estimation Traps

The five ways estimates go wrong.

05
14 Common estimation traps

If you've ever been off by 2× or more, it was probably one of these

🎯 Anchor bias

First person to say a number sets everyone else's expectation. "I think 2 days" — now nobody will say 7.

Fix: write estimates privately first, reveal simultaneously (planning poker).
😎 Happy-path blindness

You estimated the main flow and forgot: error handling, edge cases, code review, QA, bug fixes, deployment.

Fix: when estimating, ask "does this include testing, review, and documentation?"
🌫️ Dependency blindness

"It's 3 days" — yes, but it depends on an API that doesn't exist yet + a design that hasn't been reviewed.

Fix: list dependencies explicitly. Can't start without them — can't estimate either.
🧠 Optimism bias

You estimated assuming everything goes right. Nothing ever goes 100% right.

Fix: look at your last 5 similar tasks. Use your actual velocity, not your hope.
💢 Pressure to please

Stakeholder says "we need this by Friday." Team says "sure, Friday." It arrives three Fridays later.

Fix: separate "estimate" from "commitment." Estimates are honest; commitments are negotiated.
📦 Single-number fallacy

"It'll take 5 days" — treated as truth. Reality is a range, not a point.

Fix: estimate ranges ("3 to 7 days") or confidence intervals.
