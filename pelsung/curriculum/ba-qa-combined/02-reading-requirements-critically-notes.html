Part 1

The 20-second challenge

Read this requirement quickly, and count how many questions you'd need answered before a developer could start coding it:

The system should allow users to upload documents. The upload should be fast and secure. Users should be notified once it's done.

If your honest answer is "ten or more", congratulations — you already have the instinct this class sharpens. If it's "maybe two or three", read on. The aim today is to see what looks fine on the surface but isn't.

The big idea

Reading requirements critically is not a gentle skim for meaning — it is an active hunt for ambiguity, gaps, and contradictions. The earlier you find them, the cheaper they are to fix.

Part 2

Why bad requirements are so expensive

In the 1980s, Barry Boehm (a software economist) measured the cost of fixing the same defect depending on where in the SDLC it was discovered. His findings have been re-validated many times since. The curve is steep:

The Boehm cost curve

The cost of fixing a defect grows by roughly 10× at each stage it goes undetected.

Stage caughtRelative cost to fix
Requirements review1×
Design review5×
During development10×
During QA / testing50×
After release (production)100×+

A question you ask during spec review costs one minute. The same question, unanswered, can translate into a week of rework when it surfaces in development, a missed release when it surfaces in QA, or a production incident when it surfaces after launch.

This is why the instinct to read requirements critically is the single most leveraged skill in the BA and QA toolkit.

Part 3

Two lenses on one spec

A BA and a QA read the same document differently — not because the document is different, but because their purpose is different. BAs read to improve the spec; QAs read to test against it. Both arrive at roughly the same list of issues, often from opposite directions.

📋 The BA lens · reads to improve
Intent: make the spec clearer and more complete
  • Is the problem clearly stated? Is it worth solving?
  • Are the users named? Do we understand them?
  • Are hidden assumptions written down?
  • Is scope explicit — including what's out?
  • Does it contradict other specs in the same product?
  • What gaps did stakeholders not think to mention?
🔍 The QA lens · reads to test
Intent: write pass/fail test cases from it
  • Can I write a pass/fail test for each statement?
  • Are edge cases defined — not just happy path?
  • Are non-functional angles named (performance, security…)?
  • What happens with invalid inputs?
  • Is the error behaviour specified?
  • Would two testers reading this agree on the verdict?
Part 4

The seven sins of written requirements

Almost every bad spec commits one or more of these seven sins. Learn to name them explicitly and your feedback becomes precise instead of vague.

Sin 01 · Ambiguity

Words that sound specific but aren't. "Fast", "user-friendly", "as needed", "several", "robust".

"The search should return results quickly and show relevant matches."
Questions to ask:
  • "Quickly" — under 200ms? 2 seconds? 10 seconds on a slow connection?
  • "Relevant" — by whose judgment? Exact match, fuzzy, or semantic?
  • What does the user see while waiting? What about zero matches?
"Search returns results within 500ms on 4G. Matches include product name (fuzzy, one-character typo tolerated) and SKU (exact only). Below 500ms shows a skeleton loader; above 5s shows an error with retry."

Sin 02 · Unstated assumptions

Things the writer thinks are obvious. They aren't. The author wrote the spec after long conversations; everyone else is reading it cold.

"When the user logs in, they see their dashboard."
Hidden assumptions:
  • A dashboard exists. (Which one? Customized or generic?)
  • "User" means a single type. (Admin? Deactivated? First login?)
  • Login always succeeds. (Wrong password? Locked? 2FA?)
  • "See" means a full page. (Mobile? Dark mode? Empty state if no data?)

Technique · read it out loud

Reading a sentence out loud exposes assumptions. If you have to add words in your head to make it make sense — those added words are the assumptions. Write them down.

Sin 03 · Contradictions

Two sections of the same document disagreeing. Specs get long; reviewers read in chunks; contradictions survive unnoticed.

Section 2.1 — Registration: Users must register with a valid email address. Phone is optional. Section 4.3 — Login: Users log in with their phone number and OTP. Email login is not supported.
Your job when you spot it:
  • Flag it rather than fix it — you don't know which side is correct.
  • Don't assume both halves are right. Sometimes the real answer is neither. ("Phone for login, email for password reset.")
  • Contradictions are often hiding a real design decision nobody has made yet.

Sin 04 · Missing actors

Passive voice hides the "who". "The email is sent" — by whom? To whom?

"The approval is sent for review. When approved, the document becomes active."
Questions to ask:
  • Sent by whom — system automatically, or a human trigger?
  • Sent to whom — a role, a queue, a distribution list?
  • Who has the authority to approve — one person, or quorum?
  • "Becomes active" — instantly, on next login, or after a batch job?
"When the author clicks Submit, the system emails all users with role Reviewer in the same department. When any Reviewer clicks Approve, the system marks the document active within 5 seconds."

Sin 05 · Untestable statements

If a QA can't verify yes or no — it is not a requirement. It's a mood.

"The app must be intuitive. Navigation should feel natural. The experience must be delightful."
The test that exposes it: imagine two QAs testing independently. Do they arrive at the same verdict? If the honest answer is "depends on taste", you don't have a requirement.
"80% of first-time users complete sign-up without abandoning. Average user reaches dashboard in ≤3 taps from app launch. Core actions (send money, pay bill, view balance) are reachable in ≤2 taps from home."

Sin 06 · Missing non-functional requirements

The spec says what to build, but nothing about how well.

"Users can upload their government ID to verify their account."
What's missing:
  • 🏎️ Performance — max file size? timeout on slow networks?
  • 🔒 Security — encrypted at rest? who can view? retention period?
  • ♿ Accessibility — screen reader flow? keyboard-only upload?
  • 🔁 Reliability — what if upload fails halfway?
  • 📈 Scalability — supports 10,000 uploads/hour during a KYC campaign?

Class 03 goes deep on non-functional requirements. For now, just notice when they're silent. Silence is the sin.

Sin 07 · No "out of scope"

If the spec doesn't say what's not being built, stakeholders will assume their favourite feature is in.

Spec covers: "Users can save billers as favourites. Favourites show on home. Tap to pre-fill."
What's not said — but will be assumed by somebody:
  • Can I share favourites with family?
  • Can I categorise them (utilities, school, rent)?
  • Can I set auto-pay from a favourite?
  • Does this work in the legacy app version?
Out of scope (v1): • Sharing favourites between users • Categorisation / custom tags • Auto-pay from a favourite • Support in the legacy mBoB v4 app

A good out-of-scope list is a deadline-saving device.

Part 5

The three-pass annotation technique

BAs and QAs don't read a spec once and pronounce judgment. They read it three times — each pass has a different goal. This is a repeatable technique you can start using today.

Pass 1 · Comprehension

Read it end-to-end without stopping. No annotations. Goal is to understand the shape of the feature — what it roughly is, who it's for, what changes because of it. Resist the urge to mark things up on the first read.

Pass 2 · Hunt the seven sins

Go through sentence by sentence with three colours:

  • Red — ambiguity, untestable statements, contradictions
  • Yellow — unstated assumptions, missing actors, missing NFRs
  • Blue — questions to ask the author

Don't rewrite anything yet. Just mark.

Pass 3 · Testability

For every statement that looks like a requirement, ask: could I write a pass/fail test for this? If not, add it to your question list. This pass is where the QA lens really earns its keep.

The output — a clean question list

The result of three passes is not a messy marked-up PDF. It's a numbered list of specific questions you send back to the author. Something they can read in five minutes and respond to. That's the artifact that actually moves the spec forward.

Part 6 · Hands-on

Exercise · the leave-request spec

In class, you annotated this deliberately flawed spec. You can repeat the exercise at home on your own or with a classmate. Aim to find at least 10 problems — more is better.

Feature: Online Leave Request 1. Purpose Employees should be able to submit leave requests online. The system will handle the approval process. The solution should be simple and easy to use. 2. Requirements 2.1 Employees can submit a leave request at any time. 2.2 The manager is notified when a leave is submitted. 2.3 The manager approves or rejects the request quickly. 2.4 Once approved, the leave is added to the employee's record. 2.5 The system should be secure. 2.6 Leave balance should be checked automatically. 2.7 Employees can cancel their leave request. If already approved, the manager handles it. 2.8 The system supports all types of leave. 2.9 Performance should be good even when many users are online. 2.10 Employees will see their leave history on the dashboard. 3. Notifications All relevant parties will be notified as appropriate.
Part 7

Exercise reference answers

Your list does not need to match this word for word — the goal is to find the categories of problems. Twelve representative issues:

  1. "Simple and easy to use" (§1) — untestable (Sin 05). What is the target time to submit a leave? How many taps?
  2. "At any time" (§2.1) — hidden assumption (Sin 02). Literally 24/7 including server maintenance windows? Or weekdays 9–5?
  3. "The manager" (§2.2, §2.3) — missing actor (Sin 04). Which manager — direct line, skip-level, delegated? What if manager is on leave?
  4. "Approves quickly" (§2.3) — ambiguity (Sin 01). Is there an SLA — 24 hours? Auto-approve after 3 days?
  5. "The system should be secure" (§2.5) — missing NFR detail (Sin 06). Encryption at rest? RBAC? Audit logging? Who can view leave records?
  6. "Leave balance checked automatically" (§2.6) — missing flow (Sin 02). What happens when balance is insufficient — blocked? warning? convert to unpaid?
  7. "Manager handles it" (§2.7) — missing actor + missing flow (Sin 04). What does "handles it" mean — does the employee get told?
  8. "All types of leave" (§2.8) — ambiguity (Sin 01). Which types — sick, annual, casual, bereavement, maternity, unpaid? Do they all behave the same?
  9. "Performance should be good" (§2.9) — untestable + missing NFR (Sins 05, 06). How many concurrent users? What response time?
  10. "Leave history on the dashboard" (§2.10) — contradiction (Sin 03). No dashboard mentioned anywhere else. Does one exist?
  11. "All relevant parties notified as appropriate" (§3) — missing actors, missing channels, missing triggers (Sins 04, 01). This whole sentence is essentially zero-content.
  12. Missing entirely (Sin 06, 07): accessibility, mobile support, Dzongkha support, error handling, half-day leave, holiday overlap, what happens to the request when an employee resigns.

If you found 10 of these 12 — excellent; you have the instinct. If you found 6, you're well on the way. If you found 3, today's class has already paid for itself.

Part 8

How to practise on your own

This skill gets sharper with use. Two ways to keep practising:

  1. Audit a real spec you find online. Government tender documents and product "help" pages are a rich source of requirements-like writing. Pick one, mark up the 7 sins, and write a question list as if you'd been handed it to implement.
  2. Re-read your own writing. After writing any document longer than a page, put it down for a day. Come back and read it through the 7-sins lens. You'll be surprised how many sins your own writing commits when you're not defending it in real time.

A useful habit

Before agreeing to a piece of work — any piece of work, at any company — ask yourself: can I write a pass/fail test for what I'm being asked to do? If the answer is no, your first job is to get the answer to yes, not to start working.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • Defects get 100× more expensive the later they're caught — reading specs critically is the highest-leverage BA/QA skill.
  • BAs read to improve the spec; QAs read to test against it — same skill, two lenses.
  • The seven sins: ambiguity, unstated assumptions, contradictions, missing actors, untestable statements, missing NFRs, no out-of-scope.
  • The three-pass annotation: comprehension → hunt the sins → test testability.
  • The output is a numbered question list, not scribbled margins.
  • A question asked at spec time costs a minute. A production bug costs a weekend.
