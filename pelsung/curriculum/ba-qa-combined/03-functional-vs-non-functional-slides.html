Part One

FR vs NFR

What vs how well.

01
06 Definitions

Two kinds of requirements. Both must be written.

FR
Functional Requirement

Describes what the system does — features, behaviours, actions, outputs. Observable from outside.

"When the user clicks Submit, the system creates a leave request in status Pending."
NFR
Non-Functional Requirement

Describes how well the system must do it — quality attributes. Often measured in numbers, thresholds, or rules.

"The leave request must appear in the manager's inbox within 2 seconds of submission, 99% of the time."

One way to remember: FR = the verb. NFR = the adverb.

07 Side by side

Same feature — FR and NFR voices

FR · Login

The system allows users to log in using a registered phone number and MPIN.

NFR · Login

99% of login attempts complete within 2 seconds on a 4G connection.

FR · Bill payment

Users can pay registered billers by selecting a biller and entering an amount.

NFR · Bill payment

The system supports 10,000 concurrent bill-payment transactions during peak hours.

FR · Document upload

Users can upload their government ID (PDF or JPG) as part of KYC.

NFR · Document upload

Uploaded IDs are encrypted at rest (AES-256) and deleted after 7 years per regulatory policy.

Notice how each pair covers the same feature — but from two directions.

08 Why both matter

FRs get a product built. NFRs decide whether users keep using it.

Without NFRs

  • Everything "works" in a demo on good hardware
  • Real users on slow networks or old phones hate it
  • Security review fails at the 11th hour
  • Accessibility audits add weeks of rework
  • App crashes under real load

With NFRs

  • Performance targets shape architecture from day one
  • Security is designed in — not bolted on
  • Accessibility is a gate, not an afterthought
  • Load testing has concrete targets
  • QA has something measurable to sign off on

FRs answer "did we build the thing?" NFRs answer "is the thing any good?"

11 NFR 01 · Performance

🏎️ Speed and responsiveness under realistic conditions

What BAs and QAs ask

  • What's the target response time for key actions?
  • What about slow networks (3G, 2G, roaming)?
  • What's the p95 — i.e. 95 out of 100 users' experience?
  • Are there time-boxed operations (report generation, file upload)?
  • How does performance degrade under load?

Concrete examples

  • Home screen load: < 2s on 4G, < 4s on 3G, p95.
  • Send-money confirmation: < 3s end-to-end, p95.
  • Biller list: < 1s after home screen loads, p99.
  • PDF receipt generation: < 5s, never more than 10s.
  • Search typing lag: < 150ms per keystroke.

"Fast" is a feeling. "500ms p95" is a requirement.

12 NFR 02 · Security

🔒 Who's allowed to do what — and is the data safe?

Questions to ask

  • Authentication — how do users prove who they are?
  • Authorization — what's each role allowed to see/do?
  • Data at rest — what's stored encrypted?
  • Data in transit — TLS? certificate pinning?
  • Audit — who viewed or changed what?
  • Session — timeout, logout, token rotation?

Concrete examples

  • Passwords hashed with bcrypt (cost factor ≥ 12); never stored plain.
  • All network traffic over TLS 1.3; no downgrade.
  • Government IDs encrypted with AES-256 at rest; keys in HSM.
  • Admin actions logged to an append-only audit table.
  • Session expires after 15 min idle; re-auth required for transactions > Nu. 50,000.
  • Role-based access: Teller ≠ Branch Manager ≠ Auditor.
13 NFR 03 · Reliability

🔁 How often does it work, and how does it fail?

The two halves

Availability — what % of the time is the service up?
Fault tolerance — when things do break, do they fail gracefully or catastrophically?

  • What happens on network loss mid-transaction?
  • What's shown during planned maintenance?
  • How do we retry a failed operation safely?
  • Where is offline support required vs nice-to-have?

Concrete examples

  • Service availability: 99.9% monthly (~43 min downtime/month).
  • On network loss during transfer, show clear "not confirmed" state — never ambiguous.
  • Retry automatically up to 3× with exponential backoff.
  • Offline: viewing balance + past transactions OK; new payments disabled.
  • Planned maintenance: in-app banner 24h in advance.
14 NFR 04 · Usability

🎨 How easy is it for real users to succeed?

Questions to ask

  • What's the success rate for first-time users?
  • How many taps/clicks to complete core actions?
  • Are errors clear, recoverable, in user language?
  • Is the learning curve acceptable for the target user?
  • Does it work for low-literacy users where relevant?

Concrete examples

  • 80% of first-time users complete account setup without help.
  • Send Money is reachable in ≤2 taps from home.
  • Error messages written in plain language with a clear next action ("Try again" / "Contact support").
  • Standard primary actions on the right side of the screen (thumb-zone on Android).
  • Critical destructive actions require confirmation.

Usability turns into NFRs when you put numbers on it. "Intuitive" does not.

15 NFR 05 · Accessibility

♿ Can people with disabilities use your software?

The four dimensions (WCAG)

  • Perceivable — can users perceive the content? (alt text, captions, contrast)
  • Operable — can they use it? (keyboard, no time-trap, readable fonts)
  • Understandable — do they understand it? (plain language, consistent nav)
  • Robust — does it work with assistive tech? (screen readers, voice control)

Concrete examples

  • All interactive elements keyboard-reachable and focus-visible.
  • Text contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 (body) / 3:1 (large text).
  • All images with meaning have alt text; decorative images marked empty-alt.
  • Form errors linked to the field — not just a summary at the top.
  • Target WCAG 2.1 Level AA across the product.

Also a legal bar in many jurisdictions. And good for everyone — not just people with disabilities.

16 NFR 06 · Scalability

📈 What happens at 10× today's usage?

Questions to ask

  • What's normal load? What's peak?
  • What's the growth assumption over 12 months?
  • Which operations are bursty (pay day, month end)?
  • How does database growth affect cost?
  • Where are the bottlenecks likely to appear first?

Concrete examples

  • System supports 10,000 concurrent users during pay-day peak (1st of the month).
  • Database handles 5M transactions/month, growing to 25M in year 2.
  • No single request exceeds 500ms p95 under peak load.
  • Horizontal scaling plan documented; auto-scaling triggers on 70% CPU.
  • Reports older than 2 years archived to cheaper storage.

FURPS+

An industry-standard NFR checklist.

03
18 FURPS+

The classic checklist

Developed at HP in the 1980s. Still used. The "+" at the end accounts for everything the original letters forgot.

F
Functionality
Features & capabilities
U
Usability
Ease of use & human factors
R
Reliability
Availability & fault tolerance
P
Performance
Speed, throughput, resource use
S
Supportability
Maintainability & deployability

The "+" — extra concerns

Design constraints (tech stack mandated), Implementation (language, coding standards), Interface (external APIs to integrate with), Physical (hardware / deployment target), Compliance (GDPR, PCI-DSS, local banking regs), Localization (languages, currency, date formats).

Accessibility often gets classed under Usability or the "+" — but in modern practice it deserves its own column.

Part Four

Writing NFRs
That Are Testable

Numbers, not adjectives.

04
20 Bad vs good NFRs

Every NFR must be measurable by someone outside the team

❌ "The app should be fast."
✅ "Home screen loads within 2 seconds on a 4G connection, at p95 (95 out of 100 users)."
❌ "The app must be secure."
✅ "All API traffic uses TLS 1.3. Passwords stored bcrypt-hashed with cost factor ≥ 12.
   Government IDs encrypted at rest with AES-256. Pen test passes with zero Critical findings."
❌ "The system must handle many users."
✅ "System supports 10,000 concurrent active users with <3s p95 response time
   for core operations (login, balance, send money)."
21 Template for a testable NFR

A testable NFR has four parts

What must be true · measured by · under what conditions · with what tolerance

Example: "[what] Home screen loads [measured by] time from tap to interactive [conditions] on a 4G connection in Gelephu, with a cold app start [tolerance] within 2 seconds at p95."

Common trap:

Writing NFRs only as absolute maxima — "must be under 2 seconds." Better: percentile-based — "2s at p95, 4s at p99". This lets the team handle outliers sensibly.

23 Roles in NFR discipline

Two roles · one shared output

📋 BA · elicit and write
  • Ask stakeholders for implicit NFRs ("we can't be slower than the competitor app")
  • Surface compliance & regulatory constraints early
  • Write NFRs in a testable form (with numbers)
  • Balance NFRs against scope — not every app needs 99.99% uptime
  • Document assumptions so the team knows what was agreed
🔍 QA · challenge and verify
  • Challenge vague NFRs ("what exactly does 'secure' mean here?")
  • Design tests that measure each NFR objectively
  • Catch silent NFRs missing from the spec
  • Automate NFR checks where possible (perf, a11y, security scans)
  • Report NFR regressions clearly, not just functional bugs

The most common NFR failure

Not that NFRs are wrong — that they are silent. Specs describe features in loving detail and skip quality attributes entirely. When in doubt, write it down. A vague NFR you can revise is infinitely better than an invisible NFR you discover in production.
