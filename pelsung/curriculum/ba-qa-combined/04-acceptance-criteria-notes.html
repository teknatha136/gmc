Part 1

Why AC matters

Imagine giving this user story to two different developers on the same day:

"As a user who has forgotten their password, I want to reset it online so I can log back in without contacting support."

They both say "done" a week later. They've built two very different things.

  • Developer A — email → reset link → new password. Thirty minutes of work. Nothing about expiry. Nothing about what happens if the user re-enters their old password.
  • Developer B — phone + OTP → password rules → auto-login + email confirmation. Two hours of work. No support for users abroad without a phone.

Both "finished" the story. Neither is objectively wrong. They each filled ambiguity with different assumptions — and neither matches what a manager, stakeholder, or QA expected. That's the cost of a story without AC.

The big idea today

Acceptance criteria close the gap between "what we asked for" and "what got built." They're the specific, testable conditions that the whole team — BA, Dev, QA, stakeholder — agrees on before coding starts.

Part 2

What AC actually is

Acceptance criteria are the specific, testable conditions a user story must meet for everyone to agree it's "done".

Each AC is a pass/fail statement. Either the system does a specific thing when a specific trigger happens, or it doesn't. If a QA can't answer "yes" or "no" after testing the AC, the AC isn't written well enough yet.

Story vs AC

User story = what we want and why. "Save favourite billers so users don't re-type details."
Acceptance criteria = how we'll know we got it right. "Given the user completed a payment, when they tap ⭐ Save, then the biller appears in Favourites within 1 second."

One story, multiple AC. AC can't stand alone — they always belong to a story.

Part 3

The shared artifact · three readers

AC is unusual because three different roles depend on the same sentences for their jobs. That's what makes it the single highest-leverage document on the team.

📋 The BA

Writes AC. Uses them to clarify scope with stakeholders and to ensure nothing was assumed silently. If there's no AC, the story isn't ready.

💻 The Developer

Builds to AC. They're the "definition of done" from the implementer's side — no guessing required, the behaviour is specified.

🔍 The QA

Verifies AC. Each AC becomes at least one test case. QA signs off by walking every AC and confirming pass/fail.

Nowhere else in software do three roles depend on the same document for their job. That's the leverage — and that's why bad AC ripples through the whole team while good AC makes everyone faster.

Part 4

The Given/When/Then format

The industry-standard format for AC, used everywhere from small startups to regulated banking teams:

Given   the starting situation  

When   the user does a specific action  

Then   a specific thing happens  

  • Given — the context or precondition. What's true before the user acts?
  • When — the triggering action or event. What does the user do?
  • Then — the expected, observable outcome. What must happen as a result?

Why this shape works

  • Forces you to specify state before action. Many bugs come from unstated preconditions.
  • Makes the cause → effect explicit. No more "it should kind of do X."
  • Maps cleanly to a test case — precondition → step → expected result.
  • Reads well in plain English even to a non-technical stakeholder.

A filled-in example

For the story "Save a biller as a favourite":

Given the user has just completed a bill payment successfully,

When they tap the "⭐ Save as favourite" button on the receipt screen,

Then the biller appears in their Favourites list on the Bill Payments home within 1 second.

Notice how this is:

  • Specific — one action, one outcome, no room for misreading.
  • Testable — a QA can verify yes/no.
  • Observable — about behaviour ("appears in list"), not implementation ("adds a row to favourites_table").
  • Bounded — "within 1 second" quantifies the expectation.
Part 5

One story · multiple AC

A real story needs several AC to cover the behaviour properly. A good rule of thumb: 3 to 7 AC per story.

  • Fewer than 3 = probably missing edge cases.
  • More than 7 = the story is too big; split it.

Shape of a well-covered story: one or two happy paths + one inverse (undo/remove) + two or three edges. Here's the favourites story, fully covered:

AC 1 · Happy path · save

Given the user completed a payment, when they tap ⭐ Save, then the biller appears in Favourites within 1 second.

AC 2 · Use the favourite

Given the user has a saved favourite, when they tap it on the home screen, then the payment form is pre-filled with biller and last amount; MPIN is still required to confirm.

AC 3 · Remove a favourite

Given a saved favourite, when the user long-presses it and taps "Remove", then the favourite disappears immediately and no payment is triggered.

AC 4 · Edge · limit reached

Given the user already has 10 saved favourites, when they tap ⭐ Save, then the system shows a message "You've reached the limit of 10. Remove one to save this." and no favourite is added.

AC 5 · Edge · offline

Given the user is offline, when they tap ⭐ Save, then the save is queued locally and shows "Will sync when online"; it appears in the live list once the connection returns.

Five AC. One happy path · one companion (use the favourite) · one inverse (remove) · two edges (limit + offline). That's a well-scoped story.

Part 6

AC vs test case · close cousins

AC becomes test cases when QA prepares to verify — but they're not identical.

AspectAcceptance CriterionTest Case
Written byBA (often with QA review)QA (derived from AC)
PurposeClarify what "done" meansVerify the built software
Level of detailBehaviour-level, ~3 linesStep-by-step, with data, environment, preconditions
RatioOne AC typically spawns 1–3 test casesCan be many
Lives inUser storyTest management tool (TestRail, Xray, etc.)
Changes whenStakeholder clarifies behaviourEnvironment or tooling changes

A QA reads the AC and asks "what specific inputs, environments, or variations would exercise this?" The test cases are the answer to that question.

Part 7

What good AC looks like · SCARCE

Good AC is SCARCE — a mnemonic for six properties every AC should have:

🎯 S · Specific

Anyone reading gets the same mental picture. No room for two interpretations.

"appears within 1 second" — not "appears quickly"
📏 C · Concrete

About behaviour you can observe, not implementation or intent.

"shows a red error label" — not "validates the input"
✅ A · Atomic

One criterion = one check. If the AC contains "and" in the Then, split it.

One AC for save; one AC for email confirmation
🔁 R · Repeatable

Running the same test twice should give the same result. No reliance on timing luck or mystery state.

Reset the user's state before the Given
🧪 C · Checkable

A QA can verify yes/no after executing the When. No judgement calls required.

Use exact wording, counts, timings — not feelings
⚖️ E · Equal-parties

BA, Dev, and QA all read the same AC and agree on what it means. No private knowledge needed.

If only the BA knows "we meant X" — the AC is bad

When you're reviewing AC — yours or someone else's — walk the SCARCE list. Anything that fails one property gets reworked before dev starts.

Part 8

Bad AC vs good AC

For the forgotten-password feature:

❌ AC: The app should handle forgotten passwords in a user-friendly way and let users reset quickly.

Fails every SCARCE property. Vague ("user-friendly"), no observable behaviour, not atomic, not repeatable, definitely not checkable. A QA can't sign off on this; a dev can't build it; a stakeholder can't argue about it.

✅ AC: Given the user taps "Forgot password" on the login screen, When they enter a registered phone number, Then the system sends an OTP within 10 seconds and navigates to the OTP entry screen.

Specific · concrete · atomic · repeatable · checkable. A QA can write a test; a dev can build it; a stakeholder can read it and spot if anything's wrong before coding starts.

Part 9

Six common AC mistakes

Every new BA makes some combination of these. Spotting them is the shortcut to writing better AC than most of your more experienced colleagues.

1. Only the happy path

You wrote the AC for when everything goes right. You wrote none for limits, errors, or weird states.

Fix: for every story, write at least one AC for a limit ("user already has 10"), one for an error ("payment fails"), and one for an edge ("user is offline").
2. Implementation leak

"Then insert a row into the favourites table." That's how, not what. It constrains the developer for no user benefit.

Fix: ask "what does the user see or experience?" Describe that, not the database or API.
3. Vague verbs

"The system handles errors gracefully." What does "handles" mean? To whom? In what form?

Fix: replace vague verbs with observable ones. "Shows", "displays", "returns", "navigates", "logs", "sends".
4. Mixing multiple behaviours

"When user X, then the system does A and B and C." Fails atomicity — and makes the AC hard to test partially.

Fix: split at every "and" in the Then. Three behaviours = three AC.
5. Missing context

Jumping straight to "When" without a "Given". Relies on a shared assumption about starting state — which often isn't actually shared.

Fix: always state the precondition, even if it feels obvious. "Given the user is logged in" is rarely wasted space.
6. Untestable adjectives

"Intuitive." "Fast." "Smooth." "Delightful." These are moods, not requirements. Two QAs will arrive at different verdicts.

Fix: turn them into numbers or concrete behaviours. "Within 2 seconds." "Within 3 taps." "No horizontal scroll on iPhone SE."
Part 10

AC lifecycle · who does what

AC isn't a document that gets written once and forgotten. It moves through the team and gets touched at several stages.

StepWho owns itWhat happens
1 · DraftBAWrites 3–7 AC in G/W/T form based on stakeholder conversations and the user story.
2 · Pre-dev reviewQA (BA participates)QA reads AC with a testing lens. Challenges vagueness, asks about edges, adds missing scenarios. Before dev starts.
3 · EstimateDevUses AC to scope the work. If any AC is unclear or enormous, kicks back to BA/QA before committing.
4 · BuildDevImplements until every AC passes. Writes unit/integration tests alongside where possible.
5 · VerifyQAWalks every AC on the built software. Passes/fails each one. Files bugs for fails.
6 · Sign-offBA (stakeholder accepts)Final check: does the software deliver the story, not just the AC? Then stakeholder accepts.

The highest-leverage habit on this list

Step 2 · QA reviews AC before dev starts. Teams that skip this step write AC, hand it to dev, and QA only sees the AC after the build lands. By then the AC's weaknesses cost a whole cycle of rework. Introduce pre-dev AC review and you'll catch a huge proportion of defects before code is written.

Part 11 · Hands-on

Exercise · password reset AC

In class, you wrote 4–6 AC for this story:

Story: As a user who has forgotten my password, I want to reset it online so that I can log back in without contacting support.

You were asked to cover the happy path and at least three edges: unregistered account, expired OTP, and re-using an old password.

A defensible reference answer

AC 1 · Happy path · request reset

Given the user is on the login screen and taps "Forgot password", when they enter a registered phone number and tap Submit, then the system sends a 6-digit OTP via SMS within 10 seconds and navigates to the OTP entry screen.

AC 2 · Happy path · complete reset

Given the user is on the OTP entry screen with a valid OTP, when they enter the OTP and a new password meeting the policy (≥ 8 chars, not one of the last 5 used), then the password is updated and they are navigated to a "Reset successful" screen with a "Log in" button.

AC 3 · Edge · unregistered account

Given the user enters a phone number not registered in the system, when they tap Submit, then the system shows the same message as on success ("If this number is registered, an OTP will arrive shortly") to avoid account enumeration, and takes no real action.

AC 4 · Edge · expired OTP

Given an OTP was issued more than 5 minutes ago, when the user enters it, then the system shows "This code has expired. Request a new one." and provides a "Resend OTP" button.

AC 5 · Edge · re-used password

Given the user enters a new password that matches any of their last 5 passwords, when they submit, then the system shows "Please choose a password you haven't used recently" and leaves the old password unchanged.

AC 6 · Edge · rate limit

Given the user has requested 3 OTPs for the same number in the last 10 minutes, when they try again, then the system shows "Too many attempts. Try again in 10 minutes." and does not send another OTP.

Your answer doesn't need to match word-for-word. If you wrote 4–6 AC covering the happy path plus at least three edges, and each AC is specific, observable, and testable — you're in great shape.

Notice · AC 3 is the most interesting one

Returning the same message for registered and unregistered phone numbers is a subtle but important security choice (prevents account enumeration). If you or your pair spotted that during the exercise — that's the kind of thinking a senior BA gets paid for.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • AC are the specific, testable conditions a user story must meet to be "done".
  • Given / When / Then is the industry-standard format.
  • One story = 3–7 AC — fewer means missing edges; more means the story is too big.
  • Good AC are SCARCE — Specific, Concrete, Atomic, Repeatable, Checkable, Equal-parties.
  • AC is the shared artifact — BA writes, QA reviews, Dev builds, QA verifies, BA signs off.
  • The single highest-leverage habit: QA reviews AC before dev starts.
  • Avoid the six mistakes: happy-path only, implementation leak, vague verbs, mixed behaviours, missing context, untestable adjectives.

Good AC is the difference between "I thought you meant…" and "we all agreed."
