Part 1

Why this distinction matters

Imagine you ship a payment app. Every feature in the spec works. You demo it to stakeholders and they sign off. A month after release, users are furious:

  • Login takes 12 seconds on a 3G connection.
  • A bug lets anyone view anyone else's balance.
  • Visually impaired users can't navigate the home screen.
  • The app crashes when more than 500 users try to pay bills at the same time.
  • It doesn't support Dzongkha characters in notes.

None of those are "feature bugs." Every function works. What failed are the non-functional requirements — the ones that describe not what the system does, but how well it has to do it.

The big idea

Requirements are an iceberg. FRs are the visible peak — features, buttons, flows. NFRs are the 80% under the water — invisible when they work, devastating when they don't. Every BA and QA has to develop an instinct for both.

Part 2

FR vs NFR · definitions

FR
Functional Requirement

Describes what the system does — features, behaviours, actions, outputs. Observable from outside: you can click a button, see the result, say "yes, that happened."

"When the user clicks Submit, the system creates a leave request in status Pending."
NFR
Non-Functional Requirement

Describes how well the system must do it — quality attributes. Often measured in numbers, thresholds, or rules. You verify them with tools or measurements, not just by looking.

"The leave request appears in the manager's inbox within 2 seconds of submission, 99% of the time."

One way to remember the distinction:

FR = the verb. NFR = the adverb.

A verb is what the system does. An adverb is how it does it. "Login" is the verb. "Quickly, securely, reliably, accessibly" are adverbs — each one is a whole category of NFR.

Part 3

Side-by-side examples

The easiest way to internalise the difference is to write both for the same feature:

FR · Login

The system allows users to log in using a registered phone number and MPIN.

NFR · Login

99% of login attempts complete within 2 seconds on a 4G connection.

FR · Bill payment

Users can pay registered billers by selecting a biller and entering an amount.

NFR · Bill payment

The system supports 10,000 concurrent bill-payment transactions during peak hours.

FR · Document upload

Users can upload their government ID (PDF or JPG) as part of KYC.

NFR · Document upload

Uploaded IDs are encrypted at rest (AES-256) and deleted after 7 years per regulatory policy.

Notice how each pair covers the same feature — but from two directions. If your spec only has left-column text, the right-column issues will eat you later.

Part 4 · NFR

Performance

🏎️

Speed & responsiveness under realistic conditions

Performance is how the software feels to real users on their real devices over their real network. It's the NFR users notice first and forgive the least.

What to ask
  • Target response time for key actions
  • Slow networks (3G, 2G, roaming)
  • p95 and p99 percentiles — not just averages
  • Time-boxed operations (reports, uploads)
  • How performance degrades under load
Concrete examples
  • Home screen: < 2s on 4G, p95
  • Send-money confirmation: < 3s end-to-end, p95
  • Biller list after home: < 1s, p99
  • PDF receipt: < 5s, max 10s
  • Search keystroke lag: < 150ms

"Fast" is a feeling. "500ms p95" is a requirement. Always pick the second one.

Part 5 · NFR

Security

🔒

Who can do what · is the data safe

Security NFRs cover authentication, authorization, and data protection (at rest and in transit). Weak security is invisible until the breach — at which point it's the only topic anyone talks about.

Questions to ask
  • Authentication — how do users prove identity?
  • Authorization — what can each role see/do?
  • Data at rest — what's stored encrypted?
  • Data in transit — TLS? cert pinning?
  • Audit — who viewed or changed what?
  • Session — timeout, logout, token rotation?
Concrete examples
  • Passwords bcrypt-hashed, cost ≥ 12; never plaintext
  • All traffic TLS 1.3; no downgrade
  • Government IDs encrypted at rest (AES-256)
  • Admin actions logged to append-only audit
  • Session expires 15 min idle; re-auth for transactions > Nu. 50,000
  • Role-based access: Teller ≠ Branch Manager ≠ Auditor
Part 6 · NFR

Reliability

🔁

How often it works · how it fails

Reliability has two halves. Availability is what percentage of the time the service is up. Fault tolerance is whether failure is graceful or catastrophic when the service (or its dependencies) goes wrong.

Questions to ask
  • What happens on network loss mid-transaction?
  • What's shown during planned maintenance?
  • How do we retry a failed operation safely?
  • Where is offline support required vs nice-to-have?
  • What does "degraded mode" look like?
Concrete examples
  • Service availability: 99.9% monthly (~43 min downtime)
  • On network loss mid-transfer — clear "not confirmed" state, never ambiguous
  • Retry automatically up to 3× with exponential backoff
  • Offline: view balance + past transactions OK; new payments disabled
  • Planned maintenance: in-app banner 24h in advance
Part 7 · NFR

Usability

🎨

How easy is it for real users to succeed

Usability NFRs anchor the "user experience" conversation in numbers. "Intuitive" is untestable. "80% of first-timers complete sign-up without help" is a requirement.

Questions to ask
  • Success rate for first-time users?
  • Number of taps/clicks to complete core actions?
  • Are errors clear, recoverable, in user language?
  • Is the learning curve acceptable for the target user?
  • Does it work for low-literacy users where relevant?
Concrete examples
  • 80% of first-time users complete account setup without help
  • Send Money reachable in ≤2 taps from home
  • Error messages in plain language with a clear next action
  • Primary actions on the right side of the screen (thumb-zone)
  • Destructive actions require confirmation
Part 8 · NFR

Accessibility

♿

Can people with disabilities use your software

WCAG 2.1 is the widely-used standard. It's organised around four dimensions — the POUR principles. Meeting WCAG 2.1 Level AA is a common target; in many jurisdictions, it's a legal requirement for public-facing services.

The four POUR dimensions
  • Perceivable — alt text, captions, contrast
  • Operable — keyboard, no time-trap, readable fonts
  • Understandable — plain language, consistent nav
  • Robust — works with screen readers, voice control
Concrete examples
  • All interactive elements keyboard-reachable and focus-visible
  • Text contrast ≥ 4.5:1 body / 3:1 large
  • All meaningful images have alt text; decorative images empty-alt
  • Form errors linked to their field, not just a top summary
  • Target: WCAG 2.1 Level AA across the product

Accessibility is good for everyone, not just people with disabilities. Keyboard navigation helps power users. High-contrast text helps tired eyes on a phone screen in sunlight. Captions help people in noisy environments.

Part 9 · NFR

Scalability

📈

What happens at 10× today's usage

Scalability is about growth — both expected (more users over time) and bursty (month-end, pay-day, KYC campaigns). It shapes architecture more than almost any other NFR.

Questions to ask
  • What's normal load? What's peak?
  • What's the growth assumption over 12 months?
  • Which operations are bursty (pay day, month end)?
  • How does database growth affect cost?
  • Where will the bottlenecks appear first?
Concrete examples
  • Support 10,000 concurrent users during pay-day peak
  • Database handles 5M transactions/month, growing to 25M in year 2
  • No single request exceeds 500ms p95 under peak load
  • Horizontal scaling plan documented; auto-scaling on 70% CPU
  • Reports older than 2 years archived to cheaper storage
Part 10

FURPS+ · the wider landscape

The six categories above cover most of what matters in user-facing software. But the full industry checklist is FURPS+, developed at HP in the 1980s and still in use:

F
Functionality
U
Usability
R
Reliability
P
Performance
S
Supportability

The "+" covers the rest

Design constraints (mandated tech stack), Implementation (language, coding standards), Interface (external APIs to integrate with), Physical (hardware / deployment target), Compliance (GDPR, PCI-DSS, local banking regulations), Localization (languages, currency, date formats).

Accessibility is sometimes classed under Usability, sometimes under the "+". In modern practice it increasingly has its own column — including in SELISE's own work.

Part 11

Writing NFRs that are testable

An NFR is only useful if someone outside the team can measure whether it's been met. The single biggest mistake is writing NFRs as adjectives ("fast", "secure", "scalable") instead of measurable statements.

Bad vs good

❌ "The app should be fast."
✅ "Home screen loads within 2 seconds on a 4G connection, at p95 (95 out of 100 users)."
❌ "The app must be secure."
✅ "All API traffic uses TLS 1.3. Passwords stored bcrypt-hashed with cost factor ≥ 12. Government IDs encrypted at rest with AES-256. Pen test passes with zero Critical findings."
❌ "The system must handle many users."
✅ "System supports 10,000 concurrent active users with <3s p95 response time for login, balance, and send-money."

The four-part template

What must be true

measured by which metric

under what conditions

with what tolerance

Example filled in: "[what] Home screen loads — [measured by] time from tap to interactive — [conditions] on a 4G connection in Gelephu, cold app start — [tolerance] within 2 seconds at p95."

Percentiles, not maxima

Write NFRs with p95 / p99 percentiles rather than absolute maxima. "Must be under 2 seconds" is rigid and brittle — the one outlier fails everything. "2s at p95, 4s at p99" tells the team what the typical user experience must be while leaving room for real-world noise.

Part 12

BA and QA collaboration on NFRs

NFRs are a two-person job. A BA elicits and writes them; a QA challenges and verifies them. Both are necessary for NFRs to actually hold the line on release.

📋 BA · elicit and write
  • Ask stakeholders about implicit NFRs ("we can't be slower than the competitor app")
  • Surface compliance and regulatory constraints early
  • Write NFRs in a testable form with numbers
  • Balance NFRs against scope — not every app needs 99.99% uptime
  • Document assumptions so the team knows what was agreed
🔍 QA · challenge and verify
  • Challenge vague NFRs ("what exactly does 'secure' mean here?")
  • Design tests that measure each NFR objectively
  • Catch silent NFRs missing from the spec
  • Automate NFR checks where possible (perf, a11y, security scans)
  • Report NFR regressions clearly, not just functional bugs

The most common NFR failure

Specs describe features in loving detail and skip quality attributes entirely. The sin is not bad NFRs — it's silent ones. When in doubt, write it down. A vague NFR you can revise is infinitely better than an invisible NFR you discover in production.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • FR = what the system does. NFR = how well it does it.
  • Requirements are an iceberg — NFRs are the 80% below the water.
  • The six core NFRs: Performance · Security · Reliability · Usability · Accessibility · Scalability.
  • FURPS+ is the industry checklist for the full landscape — use it as a mental coverage audit.
  • Testable NFRs have four parts: what · measured by · under what conditions · with what tolerance.
  • Use percentiles (p95, p99) rather than absolute maxima.
  • The most common NFR bug is silence — BAs and QAs must break that silence early.

FRs get a product built. NFRs decide whether users keep using it.
