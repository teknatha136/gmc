Part 1

What an SRS actually is (and isn't)

An SRS — Software Requirements Specification — is a formal document that captures every requirement for a software system. It's structured per an international standard (ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018, which supersedes IEEE 830-1998 and is still the reference text worldwide).

Think of the SRS as the contract between the customer (who wants the system) and the builder (who delivers it). In many industries, it literally is a contract — signed, dated, versioned, legally binding.

✅ An SRS is…

  • Complete — every requirement appears
  • Unambiguous — one interpretation only
  • Verifiable — each req has a test method
  • Traceable — each req links upward (business need) and downward (design · test case)
  • Consistent — no two reqs contradict each other
  • Ranked — priority / stability noted per req
  • Modifiable — structured so changes don't ripple

❌ An SRS is NOT…

  • A design document (HLD/LLD come later)
  • A project plan (that's a separate doc)
  • A user manual
  • Sales / marketing material
  • A back-of-the-napkin sketch — this is a 30–100+ page artifact
  • Code, pseudocode, or API signatures
  • Optional, in the industries that mandate it

The SRS mental model

A feature spec says "Here's what we're building in the next two weeks." An SRS says "Here is the complete behavioural specification of this system. If you build anything matching this document, you have delivered. If you build something not in this document, it's out of scope." The difference matters when millions of BTN, aircraft lives, or regulatory approvals are at stake.

Part 2

When to use an SRS — the 6 signals

An SRS is a heavyweight document. 30–100+ pages, multiple reviewers, formal sign-off, change-control process. You don't reach for one lightly. Reach for an SRS when one or more of these six signals are present:

SignalWhy it forces an SRSExample
Regulated industryAuditors, regulators, or certifiers require structured requirementsCore banking · e-health · civil aviation · defence · automotive safety
Government / enterprise RFPProcurement rules demand a standards-compliant specificationBidding for a national ID system · tax portal · ministry ERP
Formal contract with a customerScope disputes will be resolved by reading the SRSFixed-price delivery to a government ministry or large corporate
Safety-critical systemFailure costs lives, money, or reputation at scaleHospital monitoring · flight control · banking settlement
Long lifecycle (10+ years)The original team won't be around; the SRS is institutional memoryNational infrastructure · telecom backbone · military systems
Cross-organisation integrationMultiple parties need a shared, immutable referenceInter-bank settlement · cross-ministry data exchange

If none of these apply, you probably want a PRD or a Feature Spec — not an SRS.

Part 3

When an SRS would hurt you — 5 warning signs

Writing an SRS where one isn't needed is almost as dangerous as not writing one where it is. The signs you've picked the wrong tool:

Warning signWhat goes wrong
The product is still being discoveredYou'll "baseline" requirements that change two sprints later — every change becomes a 3-week review cycle
The team is 2–5 people building iterativelySRS overhead exceeds the implementation effort; nothing gets built
You ship to production weekly or fasterThe SRS can't keep up; it drifts from reality and becomes fiction
The customer is internal and in the same roomA shared Notion page + AC would give you 80% of the value for 10% of the cost
Your org has no change-control disciplineAn unmaintained SRS is worse than no SRS — people treat a lie as truth

The reality at SELISE

Most SELISE product work uses PRDs and feature specs, not SRSs. We teach the SRS because (a) some SELISE engagements are regulated (core banking, government systems), (b) clients and tenders sometimes demand it, and (c) once you understand the SRS, every smaller doc becomes easier to write — the SRS is the ancestor of all of them.

Part 4

The IEEE 29148 skeleton

The standard prescribes four top-level sections plus front matter and appendices. Here's the full skeleton you must cover (though within each section you can adapt headings to your domain):

§ 1

Introduction

Purpose · Scope · Definitions/Acronyms · References · Document overview. This is the orientation section — a reader should know in two pages what the system is and where to look for each type of detail.

§ 2

Overall Description

Big-picture context. Product perspective (where the system sits), product functions (summary of what it does), user characteristics (who uses it), constraints (non-negotiable limits), assumptions and dependencies, apportioning (what's deferred).

§ 3

Specific Requirements

The main section — often 50–70% of the document. External interface requirements (UI, hardware, software, communications), functional requirements, non-functional requirements (performance · safety · security · quality attributes · business rules), and any other requirements (database, I18n, compliance).

§ 4

Verification

How each requirement will be verified — Test, Demonstration, Inspection, or Analysis. This section is what auditors actually read. Every req in §3 must trace to at least one verification method here.

§ 5 · Appendices

Analysis models · Traceability matrix · Issues · Glossary

Context diagrams, use-case diagrams, data flow, state diagrams, ER models. The traceability matrix is required — it's how you prove completeness during audit.

Part 5

§1 Introduction — setting the stage

The Introduction is short but load-bearing. Five subsections:

§ 1.1 · Purpose

State why this SRS exists and who should read it

Example: "This SRS specifies the requirements for the Biller Favourites feature of the mBoB mobile banking platform. It is intended for the development, test, and audit teams at Bank of Bhutan, and for the QA Assurance office of the Royal Monetary Authority."

§ 1.2 · Scope

What the product will do — and will NOT do

Two paragraphs. Name the product officially, summarise capabilities, list scope boundaries explicitly. The "will NOT" half is as important as the "will" half — it protects against contract disputes.

§ 1.3 · Definitions, Acronyms, Abbreviations

A glossary of every domain term that a non-specialist reader would trip on

Bank examples: MPIN, UPI, NEFT, KYC, RTGS, biller aggregator, settlement window. If a reviewer has to Google it, it belongs here.

§ 1.4 · References

Numbered list of every document this SRS depends on or cites

Other SRSs (interfacing systems), standards, regulations, prior versions. Each reference gets a number; §3 and §4 cite references by number. Example: [1] ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018; [2] RMA Directive on Digital Payments, 2024; [3] mBoB Core Banking SRS v3.2.

§ 1.5 · Document Overview

A navigation map for the rest of the SRS

One short paragraph: what §2, §3, §4, and the appendices each contain. Feels redundant, saves readers enormous time.

Part 6

§2 Overall Description — the hard one

This is where first-time authors get stuck. §2 is the "big picture" section — non-specialists read it end-to-end, specialists skim it for context. Six subsections:

§ 2.1 · Product Perspective

Where this system sits relative to everything else

Is it standalone? A component of a bigger system? Replacing an older system? Always include a context diagram — one picture showing this system as a box, with arrows to every external actor (users, other systems, hardware). Then prose for each interface type.

┌─────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐ │ Customer │───────▶│ │───────▶ Payment Gateway │ (mBoB app) │ │ Biller Favs │ └─────────────┘ │ Service │───────▶ Core Banking Ledger │ │ ┌─────────────┐ │ │───────▶ Notification Bus │ Admin Tool │◀───────│ │ └─────────────┘ └──────────────┘ │ ▼ [Favourites DB]

Always enumerate six interface types: system interfaces, user interfaces, hardware interfaces, software interfaces, communication interfaces, memory/storage constraints.

§ 2.2 · Product Functions

Summary of the major functions — details go in §3

A bulleted list, not a full feature catalogue. If §3 is the microscope, §2.2 is the table of contents.

§ 2.3 · User Characteristics

Every class of user — expertise, frequency of use, privileges

This section DRIVES UX, training, help-system, and security requirements. A novice daily user and a power-user occasional admin need very different interfaces.

User Class Description Expertise Frequency Privilege --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- Retail Customer mBoB app end-user Novice Daily Own data only Branch Agent Assists customers Expert Daily Read + cust-on-behalf Ops Controller Monitors daily settlement Expert Daily Read-only, all data Compliance Auditor Post-hoc review Expert Quarterly Read-only + exports System Admin Configures billers Expert Weekly Full admin
§ 2.4 · Constraints

The non-negotiable rules the design must honour

Regulatory policies · hardware limits · interfaces to legacy · parallel-operation requirements · audit functions · safety · reliability · criticality · language/platform requirements. Every item here costs you design freedom; state them explicitly so reviewers see them.

§ 2.5 · Assumptions & Dependencies

What you're assuming is true — which if it changes, invalidates requirements

Assumptions: "The RMA regulation on transaction limits will not change before launch." Dependencies: "The Core Banking API v3.2 will be available in the UAT environment by date." If any assumption is broken, you must revisit the SRS.

§ 2.6 · Apportioning of Requirements

Which requirements are explicitly deferred to future versions

Similar to "Out of Scope" in a feature spec, but phrased as "these requirements exist, but their implementation is deferred to v2/v3". Maintains traceability without committing to a timeline.

Part 7

§3 Specific Requirements — the heart

§3 is the bulk of the document — often 50–70% of total length. Every requirement here must be uniquely identified, testable, traceable, prioritised. Structure:

§ 3.1 · External Interface Requirements

Four sub-sub-sections — one per interface type

  • 3.1.1 User Interfaces — screen layouts, UI standards, navigation, error messages
  • 3.1.2 Hardware Interfaces — supported devices, logical/physical characteristics, protocols
  • 3.1.3 Software Interfaces — external systems (name · version · source · purpose · interface definition)
  • 3.1.4 Communications Interfaces — protocols, synchronous vs async, encryption standards
§ 3.2 · Functional Requirements

The requirements themselves — numbered, atomic, testable

Each requirement is a structured block:

FR-01 · Save a biller as a favourite Description: A customer, having completed a bill payment, shall be able to save the paid biller as a favourite from the receipt screen with a single tap. Inputs: User tap event on 'Save as favourite' button. Current biller context (biller_id, alias default = biller name). Processing: Validate user has < 10 existing favourites. Validate biller not already a favourite for this user. Persist with timestamp. Outputs: UI confirmation toast within 1 s. New favourite visible on Bill Payments home on next render. Preconditions: User is authenticated; payment was successful; online. Postconditions: Favourite persisted; audit event emitted; cache invalidated. Error handling: Limit reached → display "Limit reached (10)" and abort save. Duplicate → display "Already saved" and abort save. Network failure → queue locally with retry. Priority: Must Traces to: BR-02 (Customer retention through convenience) Verifies via: TC-FR01-01 (happy path), TC-FR01-02 (limit), TC-FR01-03 (dup)

Repeat this block for every functional requirement. This is what makes an SRS long — but also what makes it useful during audit.

§ 3.3 · Non-Functional Requirements

Five sub-types — all testable in numbers

  • 3.3.1 Performance — throughput · response time · capacity · p95/p99 targets
  • 3.3.2 Safety — prevents loss, damage, harm. References IEC 61508, ISO 26262, IEC 62304 where applicable.
  • 3.3.3 Security — authN · authZ · encryption · audit · compliance (PCI-DSS, HIPAA, GDPR, SOC 2)
  • 3.3.4 Software Quality Attributes — reliability · availability · maintainability · portability · usability · accessibility
  • 3.3.5 Business Rules — operating principles that aren't features but constrain behaviour ("Only a manager can approve transfers > BTN 100k")
§ 3.5 · Other Requirements

Database · I18n · Legal / Regulatory · anything that doesn't fit above

Data retention policies, entity-relationship summaries, language/region support, statutes the system must comply with.

Part 8

Writing NFRs that a regulator will accept

NFRs are the single most common place where SRSs fail audit. The error pattern is always the same: adjectives instead of numbers. Every regulator rejects "system shall be fast". Every regulator accepts "system shall respond to 95% of biller-list reads in under 1.0 seconds over 4G".

❌ Bad (rejected by audit)

  • "The system shall be reliable."
  • "The UI shall be user-friendly."
  • "The system shall have high performance."
  • "Security shall be strong."
  • "Response time shall be acceptable."

✅ Good (accepted by audit)

  • "MTBF ≥ 720 hours measured over a rolling 90-day window."
  • "A novice customer shall complete task T-01 in ≤ 60 seconds with ≤ 1 error, measured on a sample of 30 users."
  • "p95 response time ≤ 1.0 s for reads; ≤ 2.0 s for writes; tested at 5 000 concurrent users."
  • "All customer PII at rest shall be encrypted using AES-256; all PII in transit shall use TLS 1.3 or higher."
  • "95% of transactions shall complete within the performance envelope defined in §3.3.1; measured hourly."

The NFR test

Read your NFR aloud. Can a tester set up an experiment that produces a yes/no answer? If yes, the NFR is good. If you'd need to ask "yes but how would we check?" the NFR isn't a requirement yet — it's an aspiration.

Part 9

§4 Verification — tracing every req to a test

§4 is what separates a real SRS from a glossy pretender. Every requirement in §3 must be linked here to at least one verification method. Four methods are recognised by the standard:

MethodCodeWhat it meansExample
TestTExecute the software and check outputs against expected valuesRun test case TC-01 · assert response matches spec
DemonstrationDObserve the software operating under representative conditionsWalk stakeholder through the UI flow on staging
InspectionIReview the product / design / code without executing itRead the implementation against the security requirement
AnalysisAUse models, simulations, or calculations rather than executionQueueing-theory model to validate throughput under peak load

The verification matrix is typically a table at the start of §4:

Req ID | Method | Acceptance Criterion | Test Artifact -----------+--------+------------------------------------------------+---------------- FR-01 | T | Favourite persists; visible within 1s | TC-FR01-01..03 FR-02 | T | Favourites list renders in correct order | TC-FR02-01 NFR-3.3.1 | A | p95 ≤ 1.0s at 5000 concurrent users | Perf report R-01 NFR-3.3.3 | I | Code review confirms TLS 1.3 in all endpoints | Review log REV-07 NFR-3.3.5 | D | Business-rule demo to compliance office | Demo minutes D-03

Traceability is the whole game

During audit, the regulator picks a requirement at random and asks: "Show me the evidence that this was met." If the trace exists — Req → Verification Method → Test Artifact → Result — you pass. If it doesn't, the audit fails regardless of whether the software actually works.

Part 10

A shortened worked SRS · mBoB Biller Favourites

To make the skeleton concrete, here's a shortened SRS for the same Biller Favourites feature we used in Class 01. A real SRS would be 40+ pages; this is a guided skeleton showing how each section lands.

§ 1 · Introduction
1.1 Purpose

This SRS specifies the requirements for the Biller Favourites feature of the mBoB mobile banking platform. It is intended for the development and test teams at Bank of Bhutan and for the QA Assurance office of the Royal Monetary Authority (RMA).

1.2 Scope

Will: Allow a retail mBoB customer to save up to 10 recurring billers as favourites, retrieve them from the Bill Payments home screen, and pre-fill a new payment with one tap. MPIN re-authentication is required to complete payment.

Will not: Allow favourite sharing between customers; support auto-pay; support bulk import/export; support legacy mBoB v4 devices.

1.3 Definitions

mBoB — Mobile Bank of Bhutan app. MPIN — Mobile Personal Identification Number. Biller — entity to whom a customer pays (utility, telco, govt). Favourite — a customer-saved biller reference.

1.4 References
[1] ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018
[2] RMA Directive on Digital Payments, Rev 3, 2024
[3] mBoB Core Banking SRS v3.2
[4] BoB Information Security Policy v2.1
1.5 Overview

§2 describes the feature in context. §3 specifies all external interfaces, functional, and non-functional requirements. §4 maps each requirement to its verification method. Appendices contain the context diagram, use-case diagrams, and the traceability matrix.

Part 11

Mapping an SRS to a Feature Spec and back

A feature spec is an SRS compressed to its essentials. Every section maps:

Feature Spec §SRS § (equivalent)Expansion in SRS
01 MetadataFront matter + §1.1Adds purpose statement, audience, readership
02 TL;DR§1.2 Scope (first paragraph)Becomes formal, contractual
03 Problem— (moves to a BRD)SRS assumes problem is settled; BRD holds the rationale
04 Goals & Non-goals§1.2 (will / will-not) + §2.6 (deferred)Non-goals become formal scope exclusions
05 Users & Personas§2.3 User CharacteristicsMore classes, more detail (expertise, frequency, privileges)
06 Functional Requirements§3.2Each FR gets structured block with inputs / processing / outputs / pre / post / errors
07 Acceptance Criteria§4 VerificationMoves from per-story to system-level with formal methods T/D/I/A
08 NFRs§3.3Split into Performance · Safety · Security · Quality · Business Rules
09 UX & UI§3.1.1Style-guide compliance, explicit UI standards
10 Data Model§3.5 (Database)Full ER diagram, retention policy, constraints
11 Dependencies / Risks§2.5 Assumptions & DependenciesStronger formal framing
12 Out of Scope§1.2 (will-not) + §2.6Scope exclusions vs deferred reqs — subtly different

Takeaway: if you can write a good feature spec, you can scaffold an SRS by expanding each section. If you can write an SRS, you can distill a feature spec by keeping the essence of each part.

Part 12

Sign-off, change control, baselining

An SRS is baselined — a frozen, version-numbered reference point, signed by all stakeholders. After baselining, every change requires formal change-control.

Lifecycle

Five states — similar to a spec but with formal gates

  • Draft — author is writing; sections may be incomplete.
  • Review — circulated to reviewers; change requests collected; version 0.x.
  • Baseline (version 1.0) — signed by all stakeholders; frozen.
  • Change-controlled (1.1, 1.2…) — every modification goes through a Change Control Board (CCB); impact analysis; re-approval.
  • Superseded / Archived — replaced by a later version or retired.
Sign-off table

Every baselined SRS has one

Role                        Name        Signature   Date
-----------------------------------------------------------
Author (BA)
Development Lead
QA Lead
Architect
Product Owner
Compliance / QA Assurance
Customer Representative
Regulator Liaison (if req)

Change control in practice

Each proposed change is logged as a Change Request (CR): description · rationale · impact (reqs affected · cost · schedule · risk). The CCB reviews weekly and either rejects, defers, or approves. Approved CRs trigger a minor version bump (1.1, 1.2) and a new review cycle. No one edits a baselined SRS without going through this.

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • An SRS is the formal, complete, auditable specification of a software system — structured per ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018.
  • Use one when the project is regulated, contracted, safety-critical, long-lifecycle, or RFP-driven. Avoid for iterative small teams.
  • The skeleton has four major parts: Introduction · Overall Description · Specific Requirements · Verification, plus appendices.
  • §2.1 Product Perspective always includes a context diagram. §2.3 User Characteristics drives UX and security.
  • §3 Specific Requirements must be numbered, atomic, testable, traceable, prioritised. NFRs get numbers, not adjectives.
  • §4 Verification maps every req to one of four methods: Test · Demonstration · Inspection · Analysis.
  • Baseline is frozen and signed; change-control through a CCB keeps the doc honest.
  • A feature spec is an SRS in miniature; every section has an SRS counterpart.

An SRS isn't longer than a feature spec because it's better. It's longer because the stakes require it.
