Part 1

What is a BRD (and what is it not)?

A BRD — Business Requirements Document — is the earliest artifact in the spec lifecycle. It answers "should we do this at all?" — before any PRD, SRS, or feature spec exists. It describes the business problem, the business-level solution expected, and the business case for funding it.

The BRD is written in business language only. No systems. No apps. No databases. No APIs. A reader with no software background must be able to read the BRD and understand what's being proposed and why.

✅ A BRD is…

  • Pre-solution — no specific technology named
  • Business-focused — problem, users, outcomes, cost-benefit
  • Stakeholder-ready — sponsors, leadership, compliance sign off
  • Solution-agnostic — could be solved by software, process change, or policy
  • The funding document — it gets budget approved

❌ A BRD is NOT…

  • A design document
  • A functional / technical specification
  • A project plan
  • A user manual
  • A place to say "we'll build a mobile app"

The BRD mental model

A BRD describes a capability the business needs. Not a feature. Not an app. A capability. "The business needs the ability to let retail customers reuse recurring payment details." — that's a BRD sentence. "The mBoB app should have a favourites screen." — that's a spec sentence, already a solution.

Part 2

Why BRDs exist — the problem they solve

Without a BRD, projects start with someone saying "let's build a mobile app" — which is a solution looking for a problem. Six months later the app ships but nobody uses it, because nobody checked whether the real problem was worth solving, or whether an app was the right shape of solution.

A BRD forces four uncomfortable questions before money is spent:

  1. Is this a real problem? — with evidence, not opinions.
  2. Who owns it? — named sponsor, not a committee.
  3. What does success look like? — a number, measurable, with a date.
  4. Is the business case positive? — benefits minus costs > 0, including the risk-adjusted version.

Rule of thumb

If leadership hasn't signed off a BRD, you're building on shifting sand. The next PRD, SRS, feature spec, and test plan all reference the BRD. If its foundations are wrong, everything downstream is wrong — you'll just find out later.

Each document has a different job. The BRD doesn't replace them; it's the ancestor.

Traceability goes all the way up

A functional requirement in an SRS (FR-01) traces up to a business requirement in the BRD (BR-02) which traces up to a business objective (BO-01). When someone asks "why are we building this?" — the answer is a chain of BRD rationale, not a developer's memory of a meeting.

Part 4

BRD structure — 16 sections

A well-formed BRD has 16 standard sections. You don't always write them in order, but they always appear, and reviewers expect to find each one in roughly this place.

§§ 01–04 · Setup

Metadata · Executive Summary · Context · Problem

Metadata (title · sponsor · reviewers · status · version), executive summary (2–3 paragraphs for a board member), business context and background (what triggered this), problem & opportunity (who is affected, how much it costs today, what happens if we do nothing).

§§ 05–07 · What's needed

Business Objectives · Business Requirements · Stakeholders

Objectives (numbered, measurable, time-bound). Business requirements (numbered capabilities: "the business needs the ability to…"). Stakeholders (name, interest, influence, engagement plan).

§§ 08–09 · How work flows today vs tomorrow

As-Is Process · To-Be Process

Describe current process with pain points marked. Describe future process with improvements marked. Delta is the value proposition.

§§ 10–12 · The context that limits us

Assumptions · Constraints · Risks

Assumptions (what must remain true). Constraints (budget · deadline · regulatory · tech). Risks (likelihood · impact · mitigation · owner).

§§ 13–14 · Making the case

Success Metrics · Cost-Benefit

How the business will know it worked (metric · baseline · target · timeframe · owner). Cost breakdown (build · run · change-mgmt). Benefit breakdown (quantified + qualitative). Payback / NPV if modelled.

§§ 15–16 · Closing

Out of Scope · Approvals

Explicit "we are not doing this" list. Sign-off table with named roles: sponsor, finance, compliance, business-unit head, programme management.

Part 5

Problem statement — the anti-solution rule

The single most common mistake in BRDs: naming a solution in the problem statement. If you catch yourself writing "we need a mobile app for X" — stop. Rewrite until the problem can be solved in multiple ways.

❌ Wrong (solution-laden)

  • "We need a mobile app for bill payments."
  • "Customers need a favourites feature."
  • "We should integrate with the Core Banking API."
  • "Build a chatbot for customer support."

✅ Right (problem only)

  • "Retail customers pay the same recurring bills each month and must currently re-enter a 12-digit account number each time, costing ≈36 minutes of friction per customer per year."
  • "Customer support receives 12 000 tickets/month for questions that are already documented, costing ~800 agent-hours/month."
  • "Compliance reporting takes 14 staff-days/month to compile manually, with a 3% error rate."

The problem-statement checklist

  • Who is affected? (Segment · size)
  • What concretely hurts? (Described in observable detail)
  • How much does it cost today? (Hours · money · tickets · churn · risk)
  • What's the evidence? (Analytics · research · interviews · incidents)
  • Why now? (What changed · what's the trigger)
  • What happens if we do nothing? (Baseline trajectory)
Part 6

Current process · as-is

Describe how the work gets done today. Number each step. Mark pain points explicitly — reviewers need to see where the friction lives.

1
Customer opens mBoB pain: login latency ~8s

Two-factor login, average wait 8 seconds on 4G.

2
Customer navigates to Bill Payments

Two taps from home.

3
Customer types 12-digit account number pain: ~38s · high error rate

Manual entry · 6% error rate on first try · often requires reference to a physical bill.

4
Customer enters amount

~5 seconds.

5
Customer enters MPIN and confirms

~8 seconds.

6
Payment complete · receipt shown pain: total ~65s per payment

For 62% of users paying 3+ same billers monthly = 36× friction per year.

Tip: for service or ops processes, use a swim-lane diagram showing who does what. For customer-facing processes, a step list with pain tags is usually enough.

Part 7

Future process · to-be

Now describe the capability you're proposing — still without naming a technology. Mark improvements.

1
Customer opens mBoB (unchanged)
2
Customer sees recurring billers on home improvement: 0 typing

Saved recurring billers are displayed for one-tap access.

3
Customer taps a saved biller 38s → 1s saved

Amount and account pre-filled.

4
Customer confirms with MPIN (unchanged for security)
5
Payment complete total ~20s vs 65s

≥70% reduction in payment-time for recurring billers.

Key rule: notice we didn't say "add a favourites screen" or "use local storage" — those are design choices. The BRD says "the business needs the ability to save and retrieve recurring billers with one tap". How that capability is delivered is for the PRD and spec to decide.

Part 8

Stakeholders · RACI · interest-influence

Every BRD has a stakeholder section. Two tools combine to make it useful:

Tool 1

Interest / Influence grid

A 2×2 that categorises stakeholders by how much they care and how much they can affect the outcome. Drives engagement strategy.

  • High interest · High influence — manage closely · weekly check-ins
  • High interest · Low influence — keep informed · fortnightly
  • Low interest · High influence — keep satisfied · key decision points only
  • Low interest · Low influence — monitor · monthly newsletter
Tool 2

RACI matrix (SELISE uses RCI — "A" dropped per Julian's adaptation)

Responsible · Accountable · Consulted · Informed. SELISE's RCI variant drops Accountable and requires Responsible to be exactly one person. Teach both; pick per engagement.

Approve business caseRCIC

Success metrics · how the business will know

Every BO (Business Objective) needs at least one metric. Good metrics are specific, measurable, time-bound, and instrumented — if you can't measure it today, plan how you will.

The "instrument before you launch" rule

If your metric requires data that isn't being collected today, build the measurement before the feature ships. Otherwise, launch day finds you unable to compare to baseline, and "did it work?" becomes a permanently open question.

Part 10

Cost-benefit & payback

A BRD without a business case is a wish list. The business case has three parts:

A · Costs

Build · Run · Change-management

  • Build: engineering effort (days × rate) · design · BA · QA · tooling · one-time licences
  • Run: ongoing hosting · support · licences · monitoring (per month, then annualised)
  • Change-management: training · comms · rollout coordination
B · Benefits

Quantified · Qualitative · Risk-reduction

  • Quantified revenue: uplift × customers × conversion × retention
  • Quantified cost savings: hours saved × rate × volume, or error-rate reduction × cost per error
  • Risk reduction: probability × magnitude of averted losses (e.g. avoided regulatory fine)
  • Qualitative: brand · staff morale · strategic optionality · competitive differentiation
C · Payback

When does cumulative benefit exceed cumulative cost?

  • Break-even month — single headline number
  • NPV over 3 years — if discount rate policy exists
  • Sensitivity — what if adoption is 50% of target? 25%? Still positive?
Part 11

Risks · Assumptions · Constraints

Three lists, each serving a different reader. All three go into the BRD.

Assumptions

What we're taking as true. If any of these change, revisit the BRD.

  • "Regulation X will not change before Q3."
  • "Vendor Y remains our partner of record."
  • "Team Z will have capacity starting <date>."

Constraints

Fixed, non-negotiable boundaries.

  • Budget: < BTN 3M total build
  • Timeline: live by <regulatory deadline>
  • Regulatory: RMA directive compliance
  • Tech estate: must sit inside existing mBoB stack
Risks

Each risk has an owner and a mitigation

RiskLikelihoodImpactMitigationOwner
Regulator requires additional audit controlsMHEarly engagement with RMA; design for auditability from day 1Compliance
Customer adoption below targetLMIn-app promotion · onboarding tooltips · branch staff trainingMarketing
Integration dependency delayedMMParallel-track workstream · feature-flag launchPM
Data migration corrupts existing recordsLHShadow DB · dry-run · rollback planDev Lead
Part 12

Approvals · sign-off · governance

A BRD without approvals is a draft. The sign-off table is the contract that unlocks funding.

Who signs off

Standard sign-off roster

  • Business Sponsor — the exec who owns the outcome
  • Finance — budget approval
  • Compliance / Legal — confirms no regulatory blockers
  • Head of affected business unit(s) — agrees to the process change
  • Programme Management — agrees to slot the work
  • Optional: Customer / end-user representative · Trade union (if ops change impacts staff) · External partner
Governance lifecycle

From draft to baselined

  • Draft (v0.1–0.5) — BA is writing; sections incomplete
  • Review (v0.5–0.9) — reviewers adding comments; multiple minor bumps
  • Approved (v1.0) — signed; baseline; funding released
  • Updated (v1.x) — material change after approval; goes back to sponsors for re-approval
  • Closed — initiative completed or cancelled; archived
Part 13

Worked example · mBoB Biller Favourites BRD

A compressed BRD for the same feature we've used throughout — this time framed pre-solution, with a sponsor signing off funding.

§ 2 · Executive Summary

Retail customers of mBoB paying recurring bills face significant friction — re-entering 12-digit account numbers on every payment, 36 times per year for the typical customer. This initiative proposes a capability that lets customers save and reuse recurring payees with a single tap, reducing payment time by ≥70% and reducing support-ticket load on "save billers" questions by >80%. Estimated build cost BTN 2.4M · break-even in month 9 post-launch.

§ 4 · Problem

62% of mBoB retail customers pay the same 3+ billers every month (Q1 2026 analytics). Each payment requires re-typing a 12-digit account number — a 38-second task with a 6% first-try error rate, resulting in ≈36 re-entries per active customer per year. Customer support receives 8% of its ticket volume on "can I save my biller?" questions — ≈9 600 tickets/month costing ≈800 agent-hours. Current NPS among recurring-payers is +22, trailing non-payers at +31.

§ 6 · Business Requirements (selected)
  • BR-01 — The business needs the ability for a retail customer to mark a recently-paid biller as recurring, so that future payments to that biller are faster.
  • BR-02 — The business needs the ability for a customer to retrieve their recurring billers from the bill-payments entry point with no more than one interaction, so that payment time drops.
  • BR-03 — The business needs to remain compliant with RMA Digital Payments Directive §7 on data residency, so that no regulatory breach occurs.
  • BR-04 — The business needs to enforce MPIN re-authentication on every payment regardless of origin, so that security is not compromised.
§ 13 · Success Metrics

Recurring-payment completion time ≤ 20 s p50 (from 65 s baseline) within 60 days. Weekly active use ≥ 40% of active customers within 60 days. Support tickets on "save billers" drop to <1% within 90 days. NPS for recurring-payer segment lifts from +22 to +30 within 120 days.

§ 14 · Cost-Benefit (summary)

Build: BTN 2.4M (80 dev-days · 20 QA-days · 12 design-days · 5 BA-days + tooling).
Run: BTN 220k/year (negligible marginal hosting · monitoring · support).
Quantified benefit: 800 agent-hours/month × BTN 250/hr = BTN 2.4M/year in support savings alone. Excludes customer-retention benefit from NPS lift.
Break-even: Month 9 post-launch (conservative scenario: 50% of target adoption).

Takeaways

Key points to carry forward

What we covered today

  • A BRD answers should we do this at all? — before any spec is written.
  • Written in business language only — no technology, no systems, no solutions named.
  • 16 sections: metadata · exec summary · context · problem · objectives · business reqs · stakeholders · as-is · to-be · assumptions · constraints · risks · metrics · cost-benefit · out of scope · approvals.
  • Problem statement must not name a solution. The test: could the pain be solved ≥2 ways?
  • Use as-is / to-be flows with pain tags and improvement tags. Delta = value.
  • Stakeholder engagement: Interest/Influence grid + RACI (or SELISE's RCI).
  • Every BO gets at least one measurable metric with a baseline, target, and timeframe.
  • A BRD without a signed approval table is a draft, not a BRD.
  • Traceability runs up from FR → BR → BO. That chain is how you answer "why are we building this?"

A good BRD makes every downstream doc easier. A bad BRD makes every downstream doc a fight.
