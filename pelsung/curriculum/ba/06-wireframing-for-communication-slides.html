03
Part One

Why BAs
Wireframe

To communicate intent — not to design.

01
04 The whole point

A picture beats a paragraph.

Imagine writing: "The leave request screen should show a list of past requests with status, plus a button to create a new one." Two devs read it. They build two very different screens.

📝 Words alone

Where is the button? Top? Bottom? Floating? What does "status" look like — text, a coloured dot, an icon? The dev guesses. The QA argues. The trainer redesigns. Two weeks lost.

📐 Words + a sketch

Everyone sees the same picture. They argue about the sketch in 5 minutes — not the built app in 5 weeks. That's why BAs wireframe.

You are not designing. You are communicating.

05
Part Two

Wireframe
vs Mockup
vs Prototype

Three points on one spectrum.

02
06 The fidelity spectrum

Low-fi → Hi-fi → Interactive

All three are fine. But each one costs more time than the last. Wireframes are where BAs live.

Low-fi · BA
📦 Wireframe

Boxes, lines, scribbled labels. No colour. No icons. Just structure and intent. Cheap to throw away.

Hi-fi · Designer
🎨 Mockup

Real colours, real fonts, real icons. Pixel-perfect. Looks like the final product. Designers do this, not BAs.

Interactive · Designer
▶️ Prototype

Mockups linked together. Click here, jump there. Feels like a real app, but no real code. Used for user testing.

Part Three

Tools

Including the one already in your hand.

03
08 Pick what fits

Four good tools. The pen is fine.

✏️ Paper + pen

Free. Fast. Throw it away without guilt. The most used wireframing tool in the world. If a stakeholder is sitting next to you, this is almost always the right answer.

📋 Whiteboard

Group sketching. Big enough that the whole team can stand around it. Take a phone photo when you're done — that's your "saved file".

🧰 Balsamiq

Looks hand-drawn on purpose. Stops people from arguing about colours and fonts. Drag-and-drop boxes, buttons, dropdowns. Easy to learn.

🎨 Figma (low-fi mode)

The industry standard. We'll show you the basics next — but as a BA you only need a tiny slice of Figma. Designers do the heavy lifting.

Rule of thumb

Start on paper. Always. Move to Figma only when the sketch is settled and you need to share it with people who aren't in the room.

09 Figma · the tool you'll see designers use

Figma · the tool you'll see designers use

What it is. An online design tool that runs in your browser. Like Google Docs, but for app screens.

What BAs need to know. Open the file. Find the frames (one per screen). Drop comments on anything that's wrong. That's it.

What BAs do NOT do. Master components. Build design systems. Pixel-align. Pick fonts. Choose colours. That's the Designer's job.

If you can sketch on paper, you can communicate. Figma is optional polish.

10
Part Four

Layout
Principles

Four ideas that cover most BA wireframes.

04
11 Four layout principles

Content priority · Navigation · Reading order · Whitespace

1. Content priority

Ask: "What is the ONE thing the user came here to see?" That goes biggest, top-most. Everything else is smaller.

mBoB-like example: account balance is huge. "Last 5 transactions" is smaller below it.

2. Navigation

Three patterns to know: top nav (websites), side nav (admin tools), bottom tabs (phone apps).

For phone apps in Bhutan (mBoB, BoB, Druk PNB) — almost always bottom tabs.

3. Reading order — Z and F

Eyes scan top-left to bottom-right. Z pattern for simple pages (landing). F pattern for content-heavy pages (lists, articles).

Put the most important thing where the eye lands first — never bottom-right.

4. Whitespace = readability

Empty space is not wasted space. It tells the eye where to rest. Cramped wireframes feel cramped in the final app too.

If your screen looks busy in grey boxes, it'll look chaotic in colour.

Part Five

Annotations

The BA's superpower.

05
13 Every box gets a number

A wireframe without annotations is just a doodle.

1

Header. Static. Always reads "New leave request" — does not change between users.

2

From date / To date. Tap opens a calendar picker. From-date cannot be in the past. To-date must be ≥ From-date.

3

Reason. Dropdown with fixed list: Sick, Personal, Bereavement, Other. "Other" reveals a free-text field below.

4

Submit. Disabled until both dates and reason are filled. On tap, submits and goes to the Confirmation screen.

Number every interactive element. Write the intent in plain words.

Part Six

Worked Example

Three screens for a leave request app.

06
15 Three screens · one user journey

List · New request · Confirmation

Screen 1 · List

1. Header. Static.

2. Tap a row to view details. Dot colour = status (green/blue/red).

3. Tap to go to Screen 2.

Screen 2 · New request

1. Calendar opens on tap. From ≤ To.

2. Dropdown: Sick / Personal / Bereavement / Other.

3. Disabled until 1 + 2 are filled. On tap → Screen 3.

Screen 3 · Confirmation

1. Green check confirms send. Echo dates + reason so the user can verify.

2. Tap to return to Screen 1. The new request appears at top of the list, status "Pending".

Three grey screens. A few annotations. The whole feature is now agreeable in 5 minutes.

Part Seven · Hands-on

Paper.
Pen.
25 minutes.

Wireframe 3 screens. Annotate them. Pin them up.

07
17 Pick one. Sketch three screens.

Pick ONE feature. Sketch three screens for it.

🏛️ Option A

GMC visitor registration

A new visitor arrives at GMC. They need to register, get a visitor pass, and see where to go next.

🍱 Option B

Pelsung canteen lunch ordering

A trainee picks today's lunch in the morning. Pays via mBoB. Gets a token to collect at noon.

🏫 Option C

Parent-teacher meeting booking

A parent books a 15-minute slot with their child's class teacher. Sees available slots and confirms one.

How to spend 25 minutes

  • 0–3 min — pick one option. Just pick.
  • 3–8 min — list the 3 screens you'll draw on a small note.
  • 8–22 min — sketch them. Keep them grey. Add numbers.
  • 22–25 min — write the annotation list next to each number.

Rules

  • No phones. No Figma. Paper only.
  • One screen per page.
  • Number every button, field, dropdown.
  • If you find yourself drawing icons or picking colours — stop. You're designing, not communicating.
  • Stuck on one screen? Move on. You can come back.
18 Pin them up · 10 min walk-through

Pin sketches on the wall. Walk through your annotations.

If you're sharing

  • Read your annotations out loud — point at each number.
  • Don't apologise for the drawing. Nobody's grading the lines.
  • Say what the user does, not what the system does. ("The user taps Submit" — not "the API returns 200").

If you're listening

  • Ask: "What happens if I tap [thing] but [field] is empty?"
  • Ask: "What's the most important thing on this screen? Is it the biggest?"
  • Ask: "Where do I go after I tap this?"

These three questions will catch most missing requirements before a single line of code is written.
