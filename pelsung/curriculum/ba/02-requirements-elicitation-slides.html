03 The gap
🤔 Why elicitation matters

People rarely tell you what they actually need.

What they SAY"We need a faster leave system."
What they WANTA way to apply for leave from their phone, even when they're in the field.
What they NEEDA leave request that the manager can approve in one tap and HR can see without phone calls.

Elicitation is the work of closing that gap. Skip it and you build the wrong thing — fast.

04 Garbage in → garbage out

Bad elicitation poisons everything that comes after.

😬 Wrong feature

A bank builds a fingerprint login. Turns out half their users share phones at home — fingerprint locks them out.

😞 Missed user

A leave app assumes everyone has email. Field officers in remote dzongkhags don't — they're left out.

💸 Costly rework

Three months of building, the launch demo flops, the team starts over. The mistake was made on day one.

A BA's first job

Before anyone writes a spec, draws a diagram, or builds a screen — the BA has to find out what's actually needed. That's elicitation.

06 The five at a glance

Five techniques. Different shapes. Different jobs.

🗣️
Interviews

One person at a time. You ask, they answer. You probe.

Best for: deep, sensitive, individual views.
👥
Workshops

A room full of stakeholders. Together. With a goal.

Best for: alignment when people disagree.
👀
Observation

You watch them do their work. You don't interrupt.

Best for: how things really happen, not how people think they happen.
📊
Surveys

The same questions to many people. Quick. Shallow.

Best for: many users, simple questions.
📄
Document Analysis

You read what's already written. Forms, manuals, reports.

Best for: starting from existing rules.

Real BA work mixes 2–3 of these. You almost never use just one.

07 Technique 1 · Interviews
🗣️

Interviews

A 30–60 minute one-on-one conversation with a stakeholder.
When it shines
  • Topic is sensitive (salary, complaints)
  • You need depth, not breadth
  • The person has rare knowledge
  • Different people may disagree privately
When to skip it
  • You need 100 opinions — use a survey
  • People need to argue it out together — use a workshop
  • You only need facts already in a document
Bhutan example: A bank wants to know why village customers stop using their mobile banking app after one month. You sit with five customers, one at a time, in their own homes. Each tells you something the others wouldn't say in a group — like "I share my phone with my husband and I don't want him to see my balance."
08 Technique 2 · Workshops
👥

Workshops

A facilitated 1–3 hour session with a group, with a clear goal.
When it shines
  • Several stakeholders disagree — get them in one room
  • You need a decision, not just opinions
  • You're mapping a process that crosses departments
  • Time is short and you need alignment fast
What can go wrong
  • The loud person dominates — quiet voices lost
  • No facilitator → it becomes a fight or a lecture
  • No goal → 2 hours of talking, no decision
  • Wrong people in the room → no power to decide
Bhutan example: Planning a school annual day. Principal, class teachers, parents' rep, cultural club, and IT support all want different things. You run a 2-hour workshop. By the end you have one agreed schedule, one budget, one owner per task — instead of six versions in six WhatsApp groups.
09 Technique 3 · Observation & shadowing
👀

Observation / Shadowing

You watch the person do their actual job. Quietly. You don't help, you don't lead.
When it shines
  • What people say they do ≠ what they actually do
  • Steps are hard to describe in words
  • Workarounds are hidden — the sticky note on the monitor, the "we just call Pema"
  • You're new to the domain
Watch out for
  • People perform when watched (the "observer effect")
  • One day isn't enough — slow seasons hide problems
  • You'll see things you don't understand — note them, ask later, don't interrupt
Bhutan example: A government office says "we approve leave requests in 2 days." You shadow a clerk for one morning. Reality: the form sits in a tray for a day waiting for the manager's signature, then gets walked across the corridor to HR. You learn more in 3 hours than from 3 interviews.
10 Techniques 4 & 5 · Surveys & document analysis
📊

Surveys

The same set of questions, asked of many people, usually online.
When it shines
  • You need many voices (50+) on a small set of questions
  • You want numbers — "how many", "how often"
  • People are spread out across geography
Limits
  • Shallow — no follow-up questions
  • Bad questions = bad data (people will tick anything)
  • Low response rates are normal — don't trust 5 replies out of 200
Example: Asking 200 mBoB users one short question — "what's the one feature you'd remove?" Useful as a signal. Not enough to design with.
📄

Document analysis

Reading what's already written down — forms, manuals, policies, old emails, complaint logs.
When it shines
  • The rules are in writing already (policy, regulation)
  • You need history — "how was it done before?"
  • Stakeholders are too busy to talk often
Limits
  • Documents are often out of date
  • Reality on the ground may not match what's written
  • Always confirm with one quick interview
Example: Designing a new wedding registration system for a dzongkhag office. Start by reading the current paper form — every field is already a clue about what data must be captured.

12 Open vs closed questions

Two kinds of questions. You need both — at the right moment.

🌿 Open questions

Can't be answered with yes/no. Invite a story.

YOU
"Walk me through how you ordered lunch last week."
YOU
"What's the most annoying part of applying for leave?"
YOU
"Tell me about a time the system failed you."
Use to: explore, discover, find pain points you didn't know existed.
🎯 Closed questions

Yes/no, or a short fact. Pin things down.

YOU
"Do you order lunch every day?"
YOU
"How many leaves did you take last year?"
YOU
"Is it the manager who approves, or HR?"
Use to: confirm, count, fix a number or a name.

Rule of thumb: start open, end closed. Open questions discover the territory; closed ones lock down the details.

13 Active listening

An interview isn't waiting for your turn to talk.

Three habits that turn an okay BA into a great one.

🔁 Paraphrase

Say back, in your own words, what you just heard. Lets the person correct you before you write the wrong thing in a spec.

"So what I'm hearing is — you stop using the app because it logs you out every time? Did I get that right?"
🪜 Ask follow-ups

The first answer is rarely the real one. Ask "why" or "tell me more" once or twice and the actual problem appears.

"You said it's slow. Slow how — slow to open, slow to load a page, or slow to respond to a tap?"
🤐 Be okay with silence

If you wait 4 seconds after they stop talking, most people add the most important thing they were holding back. Don't rush to fill it.

Count to four in your head before you ask the next question.
14 Common pitfalls

Four ways every new BA messes up an interview.

1. Leading questions

"Don't you think the new app is much better?" — you've already told them the answer you want. They'll just agree.

Fix: ask neutrally. "How do you feel about the new app, compared to the old one?"
2. Double-barrelled questions

"Is the app fast and easy to use?" — two questions in one. They'll answer one and you won't know which.

Fix: split it. "Is the app fast?" — wait — "Is it easy to use?"
3. Assuming you understood

You hear "online" and write down "website". They meant the WhatsApp group. Now you're building the wrong thing.

Fix: paraphrase back. "When you say online, do you mean a website, an app, or WhatsApp?"
4. Jumping to the solution

They mention slow lunch ordering and you're already saying "we'll build an app!" — you skipped the problem entirely.

Fix: first 30 minutes — only the problem. No solution words from your mouth.

16 The brief

Run a real elicitation interview — with the person next to you.

Scenario
"Pelsung campus lunch ordering app"

Imagine the campus canteen wants a small app: trainees pre-order lunch in the morning so the kitchen knows what to cook and there are no queues at noon. You don't know any details. You have to find them out.

🧑 Trainee A · plays the user
  • You're a Pelsung trainee who eats in the canteen daily
  • Make up real opinions: favourite dishes, pain points (queues, cold food, no veg option, paying in cash, etc.)
  • Don't volunteer everything — make the BA pull it out
  • Be honest about what you wouldn't use
📋 Trainee B · plays the BA
  • 10-minute interview · take written notes
  • Start with open questions, then close in
  • Use paraphrasing and follow-ups
  • Avoid the four pitfalls (leading, double-barrelled, assuming, jumping to solution)
Round 1
10 min · interview

A is user, B is BA. B takes notes.

Round 2
10 min · swap

B is now the user. A is the BA. New 10-minute interview.

Debrief
Share back

What surprised you? Which pain point did you almost miss? Did anyone catch a leading question?

17 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ People rarely tell you what they need — elicitation closes the say / want / need gap
  • ✅ Five techniques: interviews · workshops · observation · surveys · document analysis
  • ✅ No single technique is best — pick by the question you're trying to answer
  • ✅ Open questions to explore, closed questions to confirm
  • ✅ Active listening = paraphrase · follow up · be okay with silence
  • ✅ Watch for the four pitfalls: leading · double-barrelled · assuming · jumping to solutions
