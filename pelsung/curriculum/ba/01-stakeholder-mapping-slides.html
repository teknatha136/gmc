03 Hook
🎤 A familiar story

You are planning a Bhutanese wedding.

You think the job is simple — book the venue, order the food, send the invites.

Then your aunt calls: "You forgot to invite the village lama."
Your boss says: "I need three days off approved before you confirm the date."
The caterer asks: "Will the dzongkhag allow tents on that field?"

You didn't fail at planning. You failed at stakeholder mapping — finding everyone who can affect, or is affected by, the wedding, before you start.

04
Part One

Who is a
Stakeholder?

Wider than just "users".

01
05 The plain definition

A simple sentence to remember

Stakeholder

A stakeholder is anyone who can affect your project — or who will be affected by it — even if they never use it themselves.

Why "wider than users" matters

If you only think about users, you'll miss the people who approve, pay, block, or feel the side-effects. A new mBoB feature has users — but it also has Druk PNB's compliance officer, the Royal Monetary Authority, and the call-centre team who will get the complaint calls. All four are stakeholders. Forgetting any one of them is how projects go wrong late.

06 Four questions to find stakeholders

If you're stuck, ask these four questions

Each question pulls a different group out of the shadows.

1. Who decides?

Whose "yes" do you need before the project can move? Example: the GMC project lead, the school principal, the bank's CEO.

2. Who pays?

Whose budget funds the work? They will ask hard questions. Example: SELISE finance, the donor agency, the trainee's employer.

3. Who suffers?

Whose work gets harder, or whose job changes, because of the project? Example: the call-centre staff, the front-desk clerk, the village postman.

4. Who benefits?

Whose life gets easier? Not just end users — also their family, their community. Example: a farmer using mBoB; their wife who no longer travels to the bank.

07
Part Two

Finding
Stakeholders

Five practical techniques.

02
08 Five ways to identify stakeholders

Use all five — they each catch different people

1
Brainstorm by role, not by name

List the roles first, not specific people. "School principal", "front-desk clerk", "Tashi Cell tower technician". Names come after.

2
Scan the org chart

Walk up and down the chart of any organisation involved. Don't stop at managers — go to the team that does the actual work, and to the leader who signs the budget.

3
Ask the four framing questions

Who decides · who pays · who suffers · who benefits. Each pulls out a group you'd otherwise miss.

4
Review the project artifacts

Read the project brief, the existing process documents, the contract. Every name, role, or department mentioned is a stakeholder candidate.

5
Ask: who shows up when it goes wrong?

If the system breaks tonight, whose phone rings? That person is a stakeholder you forgot. (Often the IT team, the compliance officer, or a regulator.)

09 Worked example · GMC citizen services portal

A 60-second brainstorm for a real GMC project

Project: "GMC online citizen services portal" — apply for permits, pay fees, get notifications.

Stakeholders we'd map (10):
1. GMC residents — end users; apply for things online
2. GMC project leadership — sponsors; fund and approve scope
3. Government ministries — issue the permits/services behind the portal
4. Front-desk clerks — their daily work changes the most
5. Call-centre / helpdesk team — they handle every confused user
6. SELISE delivery team — BA, Dev, QA, Design building it
7. Druk PNB / payment partners — fee collection runs through them
8. Bhutan InfoComm regulator — data protection and digital ID rules
9. Village representatives — voice of citizens with low connectivity
10. Future GMC investors — a working portal signals readiness

Notice: only #1 actually clicks the portal. The other nine still shape whether the project succeeds.

Part Three

The Power /
Interest Grid

Knowing who they are isn't enough — you need a strategy for each.

03
11 Mendelow's grid · two simple questions

Two questions per stakeholder. Two answers each.

Aubrey Mendelow · 1991

For each stakeholder, ask just two questions:

1. How much POWER do they have over the project?
(Can they stop it, change it, or fund it?)

2. How much INTEREST do they have in the project?
(Do they care about the outcome? Will they be affected day-to-day?)

Each answer is just "high" or "low". Two questions × two answers = four groups. Each group needs a different way of working with you.

12 The four quadrants

One strategy per quadrant

HIGH Power LOW
High Power · Low Interest Keep Satisfied

They can derail the project but don't watch it daily. Brief them only on big decisions and risks. Don't drown them in detail.

High Power · High Interest Manage Closely

The key players. Talk to them often, involve them in trade-offs, get their buy-in early. They make or break delivery.

Low Power · Low Interest Monitor

Acknowledge they exist; check in occasionally. Don't spend time managing them — but don't forget them either.

Low Power · High Interest Keep Informed

They care a lot but can't change much. Send updates, listen to feedback, treat them as allies and an early-warning system.

LOW Interest HIGH

Part Four · Recap + link

RACI vs RCI

From Foundation 08 — the SELISE adaptation, and how it connects to your map.

04
15 Quick recap · then the link

RACI is the industry standard. SELISE uses RCI.

LetterRACIRCI (SELISE)What it means
R · Responsible✓✓The one person who actually does the work. Always exactly one.
A · Accountable✓dropped"Final answerable owner." Julian dropped it — no clean German equivalent, and R already implies ownership.
C · Consulted✓✓People whose input is needed before the work is finished.
I · Informed✓✓People who need to know the result, but don't shape it.
📍 Stakeholder map

Tells you WHO the people are, and how to engage each one (manage closely, keep informed, …).

"GMC residents are Low-Power High-Interest — keep them informed."
→
📋 RCI

Tells you, for a specific deliverable, WHAT each one does — Responsible, Consulted, or Informed.

"For the user-permission spec: BA = R, GMC lead = C, residents = I."

First map the people. Then, deliverable by deliverable, decide their role with RCI.

Part Five · Hands-on

Build a
Stakeholder Map

Pairs · 15 minutes · brainstorm + grid + strategy.

05
17 Exercise · the brief

In pairs · 15 minutes · pick one project

Pick ONE
Option A — GMC online citizen services portal

A one-stop website for GMC residents to apply for permits, pay fees, and get notifications.

Option B — Druk PNB launches a new mobile app

A redesigned banking app with bill pay, savings goals, and Tashi Cell top-ups.

Step 1 · 5 min
Brainstorm 8–10 stakeholders

Use the four framing questions: who decides, pays, suffers, benefits? Roles, not names.

Step 2 · 5 min
Place each on the grid

For each one, decide: high or low power? High or low interest? Drop them into a quadrant.

Step 3 · 5 min
Pick the strategy

Write the engagement strategy next to each name (manage closely · keep satisfied · keep informed · monitor).

Two volunteer pairs share their map in the debrief. Disagreements are normal — say why you placed someone where you did.

18 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ A stakeholder is anyone who can affect the project, or be affected by it — wider than just users
  • ✅ Find them with five techniques: brainstorm by role, scan the org chart, ask the four framing questions, review artifacts, "who shows up when it breaks"
  • ✅ The Power / Interest Grid sorts them into four quadrants, each with one strategy
  • ✅ Manage Closely · Keep Satisfied · Keep Informed · Monitor — match the strategy to the quadrant
  • ✅ Stakeholder map answers WHO; RCI answers WHAT each one does on a deliverable
  • ✅ Placements shift over time — re-map at every phase change

Find the people first. Then decide how to work with each one.
