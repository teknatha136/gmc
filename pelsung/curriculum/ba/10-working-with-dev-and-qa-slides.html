Part One

The Daily Loop

A BA sits in the middle. Stories flow out. Bugs flow in. Both flows fail in known ways.

01
04 Why this class exists

You wrote a clear story. Why is the team still confused?

In your day-to-day, two flows happen over and over:

📤 Stories go out

You hand a story to a developer. They start asking questions. Some you can answer. Some you didn't think about. Some don't have an answer yet.

📥 Bugs come in

QA tests the build and files bug reports. Some are critical. Some are not. You help decide what to fix, what to defer, and what wasn't really a bug — it was a missing AC.

Today is about how to handle both flows well — so the team moves faster, not slower.

06
Part Two

Clarifying
Questions

When a developer asks "what should happen if…?" — that's a gift, not an interruption.

02
07 Three rules when a dev asks

A question from Dev = a hole in your spec. Treat it that way.

1
Don't dismiss it

If a smart developer is confused, the story is unclear. The question is real. Saying "just use your judgement" pushes a decision onto someone who can't make it for the business.

"Just do whatever" → you'll get whatever.
2
Update the story · don't answer in chat

Answering on WhatsApp solves it for one developer, today. Tomorrow QA will hit the same gap and won't see your message. Add the answer to the story or the AC where everyone can find it.

Chat reply = lost in 24 hours. Updated AC = forever.
3
Tribal knowledge is the enemy

"Tribal knowledge" = stuff that lives only in someone's head. New joiners can't learn it. If only you know how leave approval works, the team breaks when you're sick.

Write it down. Always. Even the obvious things.

A clarifying question is a free QA review before the code is even written. Welcome it.

Part Three

Spec Ambiguity
Workshop

Three real AC. Three real questions. Three fixes.

03
09 Ambiguity · examples 1 & 2

Read the AC · spot what's missing · ask · fix

Example 1 · Government leave portal
⚠️ Ambiguous AC"User receives a notification when their leave is approved."
❓ Clarifying questionsWhich channel — email, SMS, in-app, all three? What's the message text? When exactly — instantly, or by end of day? What if the user is offline?
✅ Fixed ACGiven a user's leave request is approved by their manager, when the approval is saved, then the system sends an SMS within 2 minutes with the text "Your leave from {start} to {end} is approved" and shows the same message in the app's Notifications tab.
Example 2 · School attendance app
⚠️ Ambiguous AC"Teacher can mark students absent."
❓ Clarifying questionsCan they mark only their own class, or any class? Can they edit yesterday's record? What if the student already has a leave application? How does the parent get told?
✅ Fixed ACGiven a teacher is logged in and viewing today's roll-call for a class they are assigned to, when they tap "Absent" next to a student's name, then the student's status is saved as Absent for today, the parent receives an SMS within 5 minutes, and the teacher cannot change the record after midnight.
10 Ambiguity · example 3 & lesson
Example 3 · mBoB transfer
⚠️ Ambiguous AC"Transfer fails gracefully if there are not enough funds."
❓ Clarifying questionsWhat does "gracefully" mean here? What message does the user see? Does the amount stay typed-in or get cleared? Is the transaction logged anywhere? Can they tap "Retry"?
✅ Fixed ACGiven a user enters a transfer amount greater than their available balance, when they tap "Confirm transfer", then the system shows the message "Insufficient balance. Available: Nu {x}" in red below the amount field, keeps the amount and recipient filled in, and does not deduct any money or charge any fee.

The pattern in all three

The original AC sounded right when you wrote it. It only became "wrong" when a developer tried to build it. Vague verbs ("notify", "mark", "gracefully") hide a dozen choices. The fix isn't more text — it's more specifics: which channel · who can do it · what message · when exactly · what happens to the data.

Part Four

Defect Triage

Bugs are filed. Now: which ones get fixed, when, by whom?

04
12 Severity vs priority · quick recap

Two different questions. Two different answers.

You met these in Quality & Compliance Class 02. Here's the short version.

🔴 Severity

How broken is it? A technical fact about the bug.

  • Critical — app unusable, data lost
  • High — major feature broken, no workaround
  • Medium — minor feature broken, workaround exists
  • Low — cosmetic, typo, alignment

Set by QA when filing. Mostly objective.

🔵 Priority

How urgent is the fix? A business decision.

  • P0 — fix now, drop other work
  • P1 — fix this sprint
  • P2 — fix next sprint
  • P3 — backlog · maybe never

Set in triage by BA + Product. Subjective. Your main lever.

Severity is about the bug. Priority is about the business. Set them independently.

13 Severity × priority · what to do

Four corners · four very different responses

High priority →
Low priority →
High
severity
🚨 Stop the line

Drop the sprint. Fix today. Hotfix to production if it's already live.

e.g. mBoB transfer takes the money but doesn't reach the receiver — for everyone.
📋 Schedule it

Yes, it's bad — but it almost never happens. Plan a fix in an upcoming sprint, don't panic.

e.g. crash that triggers only on Feb 29 in a leap year.
Low
severity
⏱️ Fix this sprint

Tiny bug, but it's visible to important people right now. Get it cleaned up before the audience sees it.

e.g. typo on the leave-portal homepage during a regulator visit tomorrow.
📦 Backlog

Log it. Fix when convenient. Maybe never. Don't waste a sprint slot on it.

e.g. footer copyright still says 2025 in mid-2026.

High severity ≠ urgent. Low severity ≠ ignore. The combination tells you what to do.

14 The triage call · who decides

Triage is a meeting · not a vote · not a guess

Most teams meet 2–3 times a week for 20 minutes. Walk every new bug. Decide four things per bug.

PersonBrings to the tableDecides
📋 BA (you)Business impact · which user is hurt · is this a bug or a missing AC?Priority (with Product)
🔍 QA LeadHow often does it happen · severity rating · how did it slip past testingSeverity
💻 Dev LeadHow risky is the fix · is it 1 hour or 3 days · any side effectsEffort & owner
📦 ProductRoadmap · upcoming releases · external commitmentsPriority (with BA)

Four people in a 20-minute call. Faster than a 3-day chat thread.

Part Five

Acceptance
Testing

QA tests. You're the source of truth on what the user actually wanted.

05
16 Your role in the QA walkthrough

Sit with QA · watch the feature run · answer "is this what we wanted?"

✅ Do

Sit with QA when they walk a feature for the first time. Speak up the moment something looks off. You're the only person in the room who knows what the user actually meant.

✅ Do

If a developer met the AC but the feature feels wrong — that's a signal the AC was incomplete. Open a new story or update this one. Don't pretend it's fine.

❌ Don't

Don't be a second QA. You're not there to find typos. Your job is intent — does this match what the stakeholder asked for?

A useful question to ask in every walkthrough

"If I were the user the story is about — would I be happy with this?" If you hesitate, the team needs to talk before you sign off.

17 Hands-on · 20 minutes

Triage 8 bugs on the GMC online appointment system

Imagine GMC has launched an online appointment system for visa, business permit, and clinic bookings. QA filed 8 bugs this morning.

BUG-101App crashes when a user taps "Book appointment" on iOS 17 — happens for everyone, no workaround.
BUG-102The "Submit" button on the visa form is light blue instead of SELISE blue.
BUG-103If a user picks a date in the past, the system accepts it and creates an appointment.
BUG-104Confirmation SMS arrives in English even when the user selected Dzongkha. (Minister demoing the app to press tomorrow.)
BUG-105Calendar widget overlaps the keyboard on Android phones with screens smaller than 5 inches.
BUG-106Logo on the login screen is slightly off-centre on tablets.
BUG-107If two users try to book the last slot at the exact same second, both get "Confirmed" — system overbooks.
BUG-108The booking history page shows times in UTC instead of Bhutan time. Users are showing up at the wrong hour.

In pairs · do three things

  • For each bug, write severity (Critical / High / Med / Low) and priority (P0 / P1 / P2 / P3).
  • Write a one-line decision: fix this sprint · next sprint · backlog · clarify with reporter.
  • Pick the one bug that was clearly a missing AC, not a real defect. Write the AC that should have caught it.

Tips while you work

  • Don't argue about severity for 5 minutes. Pick one. Move on.
  • Priority depends on context — re-read the bug for the business clue.
  • "Missing AC" bugs sound like a feature instead of a defect.
  • Two pairs share in debrief — pick your most disputed bug.

15 min triage · 5 min debrief.

18 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ A BA sits in the loop — stories go out, bugs come in. Both flows fail in known ways.
  • ✅ A clarifying question from a developer is a gift — don't dismiss, update the story, kill tribal knowledge.
  • ✅ Vague verbs ("notify", "gracefully", "handle") hide a dozen choices. Specifics close the gap.
  • ✅ Severity = how broken (QA's call) · Priority = how urgent (your call, with Product). They're independent.
  • ✅ The four corners of severity × priority drive four different responses — not the same playbook.
  • ✅ Triage is a 20-minute meeting · BA + Dev Lead + QA + Product · 4 decisions per bug.
  • ✅ In acceptance testing, your job is intent — does this match what the user actually wanted?

Good BAs don't just write specs. They keep the team aligned through the whole loop.
