Recap

Verification
vs Validation

Two questions. Two checks. UAT is the second one.

01
04 The two questions

Did we build it right? — vs — Did we build the right thing?

Verification

"Did we build it right?"

Does the system match the written spec? Reviews, unit tests, integration tests, system tests. QA does most of this.

Example: the spec says "leave balance must update after approval". Did the code do that?

Validation

"Did we build the right thing?"

Does it actually solve the user's problem in real life? Real users walking through real workflows. UAT is the main validation activity.

Example: leave balance updates correctly — but does the manager trust the new screen enough to approve from it?

UAT lives on the validation side

UAT (User Acceptance Testing) is real business users confirming the system solves the problem it was built for — before it goes live. It is not a second round of QA.

Already covered in: BA+QA Quality #01 (Testing Fundamentals) · BA+QA Combined #04 (Acceptance Criteria).

Part One

What UAT Is
(and isn't)

A clear definition before we plan one.

02
06 The 30-second definition

UAT is real business users running through real workflows on a realistic system, signing off that it solves the problem.

It is the last check before go-live. If UAT passes, the business says "yes, ship it." If UAT fails, we don't ship — even if every QA test passed.

Who is "the business user"?

The people who will actually use the system every day. For an mBoB feature: a Druk PNB account holder. For a leave-approval system: a real line manager and a real HR officer. Not the developer. Not the QA. Not the BA pretending to be a user.

07 What UAT is — and isn't

Easy to confuse with QA testing. They are not the same.

✅ UAT IS
  • Business users walking through real workflows end-to-end
  • A check that the system solves the problem it was built for
  • Anchored to user stories and acceptance criteria — same ones the BA wrote
  • The final gate before go-live and sign-off
  • Conducted in a UAT environment with realistic data
❌ UAT IS NOT
  • Functional re-testing — QA already did that
  • Bug hunting — bugs are a side-effect, not the goal
  • Exploratory testing — we follow scripts, not curiosity
  • Performance or security testing — different activities, different specialists
  • Done by developers, QAs, or the BA alone — that's not "user" testing

If you find yourself "trying to break it" during UAT — stop. That's QA's job. UAT asks: "can a normal user finish this task and trust the result?"

10 Planning · Timing

When does UAT happen?

Earlier
Dev + QA testing
Then
UAT (1–2 weeks)
Finally
Go-live

The rule

UAT starts after QA has signed off on functional testing — and ends before go-live, with a buffer.

  • Allow 1–2 weeks for UAT itself. Less than a week and users feel rushed; more than two and they lose focus.
  • Add 3–5 days buffer after UAT to fix any blocking issues found, before the go-live date.
  • Don't squeeze it. UAT compressed into two days is theatre, not testing.
  • Don't run it during month-end or festival weeks. Real users have day jobs — Losar week is not the time.

Example: GMC visa system go-live is set for May 1. QA sign-off must land by April 14. UAT runs April 15 – April 26. Buffer + go-live prep April 27 – May 1.

11 Planning · Environment

Where do users test — and with what data?

UAT environment, not production

A separate UAT environment that looks and behaves like production — but isn't.

  • Same screens, same buttons, same flows as the real system. If the UAT screen looks different, the test is invalid.
  • Realistic-but-fake data. Test accounts named "Tashi Dorji" and "Karma Wangmo" — not real customers. Realistic balances, realistic dates.
  • Never use production. A test approval in prod is a real approval. A test withdrawal in prod is a real withdrawal. This rule is non-negotiable.
  • Connections to other systems (SMS, email, payment) point to safe test endpoints — not real customer phone numbers.

Concrete: for an mBoB onboarding test, the UAT environment uses fake CIDs, fake phone numbers (test SIMs), and a sandboxed payment gateway. No real customer ever receives a UAT SMS.

12 Planning · Participants

Who is in the room?

3–7 real business users, plus a small support cast

Pick people who will actually use the system in their day jobs.

  • 3–7 business users. Fewer than 3 = not enough perspective. More than 7 = chaos to coordinate.
  • BA = facilitator. Walks users through scripts, answers questions, captures issues. Does not "do" the test for them.
  • Dev + QA on standby. Available to triage issues as they appear, not in the same room dictating.
  • One business sponsor. Senior person from the business side who will sign off at the end.

Example for a school registration system: 2 admission officers, 2 parents (or BAs role-playing parents only if real ones are not available), 1 principal. BA facilitates. Dev + QA reachable on chat.

13 Planning · Defect handling

What happens when something doesn't work?

Log · severity-tag · decide

Bugs will appear in UAT. Have the process ready before day one.

  • Log every issue in a single tracker (Jira, a shared sheet — pick one and stick to it). Title, steps, expected, actual, screenshot.
  • Severity-tag each one: Blocker · High · Medium · Low. Defined upfront, not improvised.
  • Daily triage — BA + Dev + QA + sponsor decide for each issue: fix-now (blocks UAT) or post-launch (acceptable to ship, fix later).
  • Re-test fixed issues in UAT before sign-off — don't accept a developer's "trust me, it's fixed".

A clear severity ladder prevents the worst UAT pattern: the team arguing about whether to ship for two days while the user waits for an answer.

14 Planning · Sign-off

How does UAT end?

Explicit. Written. Specific.

UAT ends with a written sign-off — not a verbal "looks fine, go ahead".

  • Written — email, document, ticket. Not a hallway conversation.
  • Names what was tested. "All 12 scripts in the leave-approval workflow were executed." Not "we tested the system."
  • Names what was NOT tested. "Mobile view was deferred to post-launch." Honesty here saves arguments later.
  • Lists known issues being shipped with — and which release will fix them.
  • Signed by the business sponsor — the person whose name carries weight if something breaks.

We dig into the sign-off conversation in a few slides — what to actually say, and what not to.

Part Three

Anatomy of a
UAT Script

Five parts. Every script. Every time.

04
16 What every UAT script contains

A script is a recipe. Same five ingredients, every time.

  1. Story it covers. Which user story (and which AC) is this script validating? One script may cover one story.
  2. Pre-conditions. What state must the system, account, and data be in before the user starts? (Logged in? Account has Nu. 5,000 balance? Test biller already saved?)
  3. Numbered steps. Plain-language actions the user takes. "Tap Pay Bills." "Enter 500 in the amount field." "Tap Confirm."
  4. Expected outcome at each step. What should the user see or experience after that step? "Confirmation screen with reference number appears."
  5. Pass / Fail / Comments column. The user (or facilitator) marks each step. Comments capture surprises.

A good UAT script can be handed to any business user — they should be able to run it without asking what to do next.

17 Worked example · the story

Story: a manager approves a sick leave request

User Story
Approve a sick leave request

As a line manager,
I want to approve or reject sick leave requests from my team
so that my team's leave balance updates and HR sees the decision the same day.

AC 1 — Given a pending sick leave request from a direct report, when the manager taps Approve, then the request status changes to Approved within 2 seconds and the team member is notified by email.
AC 2 — Given an approved sick leave, when the manager opens the team's leave balance, then the days approved are deducted from the team member's annual sick leave balance.
AC 3 — Given the manager rejects a request, when they confirm the rejection, then the team member is notified with the rejection reason and the leave balance is unchanged.

19 The sign-off conversation

What to actually say when UAT ends

The four things every sign-off names

✅ What's in scope (and passed)

"All 12 scripts of the leave-approval workflow ran end-to-end and passed. Approve, reject, balance update, email notification — all working."

📋 What's out of scope

"Bulk approval of multiple requests at once was not part of this release — it's planned for v1.2."

⚠️ Known limitations
  • Mobile app rejection reason field is limited to 200 characters — confirmed acceptable
  • Email notifications take up to 5 minutes during peak hours
📌 Conditions for go-live
  • 2 medium-severity issues (#JIRA-203, #JIRA-211) must be fixed before launch
  • HR team trained on the new screens before go-live day

A vague "looks good to me" is not sign-off. The four-part shape above is what protects the BA, the team, and the business.

20 Common UAT failures

Four ways UAT goes wrong — spot them early

1. Treated as a tick-box at the end

UAT is added to the plan a week before go-live, with no time to fix anything users find. Becomes ceremony.

Fix: book UAT into the plan from day one. Two weeks, with a buffer. Defend that calendar.
2. Wrong people doing it

Devs, QAs, or "the BA pretending to be the user" run the scripts. Real users never see the system before launch.

Fix: get 3–7 actual business users committed up-front, written into the project plan.
3. Wrong environment

Tests are run in production (creates real records) — or in a stripped-down dev environment with no realistic data.

Fix: a UAT environment that mirrors prod, with realistic-but-fake data. Set this up before users arrive.
4. Vague scripts

"Try the leave approval flow." That's not a script — that's a hint. Users wander, important paths get missed.

Fix: numbered steps, clear expected outcomes, anchored to AC. The scripts you saw on slide 18.

Part Four · Hands-on

Write a UAT Script
for mBoB

Pairs · 25 minutes · happy path + error path.

05
22 The brief

Write two UAT scripts: one happy, one error

User Story
Cancel a confirmed Druk Air booking via mBoB

As an mBoB user with a confirmed Druk Air ticket,
I want to cancel the booking from inside mBoB
so that I get my refund processed without calling the airline.

AC 1 — Given the user has a confirmed Druk Air booking visible in mBoB, when they tap "Cancel booking" and confirm, then the booking status changes to "Cancelled" within 5 seconds and a refund reference number is shown.
AC 2 — Given the user cancels more than 24 hours before departure, when the cancellation completes, then the full ticket amount is credited back to the original mBoB account within 3 working days.
AC 3 — Given the user tries to cancel less than 2 hours before departure, when they tap "Cancel booking", then the system shows "Cancellation not allowed within 2 hours of departure — please call Druk Air" and does nothing.

What to produce

  • Script 1 — Happy path: 5–8 numbered steps, expected outcome per step, Pass/Fail column. User cancels > 24h before flight and gets the refund reference.
  • Script 2 — Error path: 5–8 numbered steps. User tries to cancel within 2h of departure — system blocks them with the right message.
  • Both scripts must include pre-conditions at the top (account, booking, time-to-departure).

How to work

  • Pair up.
  • One person drafts, the other plays "the user" and questions every step ("how do I know it worked?").
  • Use the worked example on slide 18 as your template.
  • 15 min writing · 5 min pair check · 5 min volunteer share.

🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ Verification = built it right · Validation = built the right thing · UAT = validation
  • ✅ UAT is real business users on a realistic system, signing off that it solves the problem
  • ✅ Plan six things up-front: timing · environment · participants · scripts · defects · sign-off
  • ✅ Every UAT script has 5 parts: story · pre-conditions · numbered steps · expected outcomes · pass/fail
  • ✅ Sign-off is written and names what was tested, what wasn't, known limitations, and conditions
  • ✅ The four common failures: last-minute · wrong people · wrong environment · vague scripts
  • ✅ The BA owns UAT — facilitates, doesn't perform it

If real users haven't run real scripts in a real-ish environment — you haven't done UAT.
