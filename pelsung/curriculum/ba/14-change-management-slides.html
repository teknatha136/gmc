03 The hard truth
💥 The thing nobody warns you about
A truth from real projects

The software ships.
But change is what makes the software actually used.

Most "failed projects" did not fail because the code was bad. They failed because people did not switch over — they kept using the old way. The new system sat there, mostly empty, while the old habits continued.

A BA who only delivers requirements is half-done. The other half is helping people stop doing the old thing and start doing the new thing. That's change management.

04 Change is everywhere around us

You have already seen change management — and seen it fail

Each of these is a real change. Some went smoothly. Some did not. The software was rarely the reason.

💵 → 📱 Cash to QR at a Thimphu shop 📄 → 💻 Paper to digital records in a govt office 📓 → 📲 Tablets in schools for attendance / lessons 🏦 mBoB rolls out a new feature to all users 🪪 Druk e-Pass replacing physical IDs 🧾 e-leave replacing paper leave forms

Today we learn the playbook — so the next time something like this rolls out, you know how to plan it well, not just hope.

Part One

Why People
Resist Change

If you understand the reasons, you stop being surprised — and start being prepared.

01
06 Five reasons people resist

Resistance is not stupidity. It's a signal.

When people push back, they are telling you something. Listen first.

😨 Fear

"Will I lose my job? Will I look stupid? Will I be slower than the kid who already knows it?"

A 50-year-old shopkeeper hears "QR payments" and pictures losing customers because she can't operate it.
🛋️ Comfort with the old way

"It works. I've done it for 15 years. Why fix what isn't broken?"

A clerk who knows every page of the paper register can find a record in 10 seconds. The new app feels slower at first.
🤷 No "What's in it for me"

If a person cannot see what they gain, they will not adopt. The benefit must be personal, not just organisational.

"It saves the office 40% time" — great for the boss. The clerk needs a different answer.
🔥 Bad memory of the last change

The last system was launched with no training, broke for 2 weeks, and they got blamed. Why trust this one?

A staff member who was burned once is the loudest sceptic at the next launch. That's a memory, not stubbornness.
📵 Poor communication

People hear about the change late, from the wrong person, with the wrong tone. By the time you arrive to "explain", the rumour is already louder.

Staff find out from a WhatsApp group, not from their manager — and the group's version is half wrong.
🧠 Habit, not opinion

Most "resistance" is not a thought-out position. It's the body just doing what it has always done, without thinking.

Even a willing user reaches for the paper form on autopilot for the first 2 weeks. That is normal — plan for it.

Part Two

Kotter's 8 Steps
Lite Version

A famous playbook for leading change. We'll keep it practical.

02
08 The 8 steps at a glance

Eight steps · in order

We'll zoom into the four highlighted steps next — those are where most rollouts succeed or die.

  1. Create urgency — give a clear reason this change must happen, and now
  2. Build a guiding coalition — gather the people who can actually unblock work
  3. Form a strategic vision — paint the picture of "after" so people can imagine it
  4. Enlist a volunteer army — find willing early adopters and let them lead from the inside
  5. Enable action — remove barriers — strip away the things that make the new way painful
  6. Generate short-term wins — find a quick visible victory to prove the change works
  7. Sustain acceleration — keep going · don't declare victory after the first win
  8. Institute change — bake the new way into how the place runs, so it doesn't slip back

Highlighted steps are where Bhutanese rollouts most often stumble. Let's look at each one.

09 Kotter Step 1 · Urgency

Step 1 · Create urgency

Step 1 of 8

Give a clear, honest reason this must happen — now

Without urgency, people put the change at the bottom of their list. "We'll get to it." A good urgency message is short, true, and tied to something the audience cares about. It is not threats, and it is not hype.

Bhutanese example · QR payments at a Thimphu shop

"Tourists are walking out because they don't carry cash. Three shops on this street already lost weekend sales. We have 6 weeks before the festival rush." That's urgency — concrete, recent, and pointed at the shopkeeper's wallet.

What weak urgency sounds like

"Digital payments are the future." True, but it is a slogan, not a reason to act this month.

10 Kotter Step 5 · Remove barriers

Step 5 · Enable action — remove barriers

Step 5 of 8

Make the new way easier than the old way

People do not adopt the better thing. They adopt the easier thing. If your new system requires 5 extra steps, a password they don't remember, and a printer that's broken — they will quietly go back to paper.

Bhutanese example · paper-to-digital records, govt office

The new system is great. But staff need to walk to the one shared computer in the corridor, log in with a password they wrote on a sticky note, and the wifi cuts out. Until you fix the laptop, the password reset, and the wifi, no training session will help.

What "remove barriers" looks like in practice

One person, one laptop, on their desk. SSO so they don't fumble with passwords. A 2-page cheat sheet pinned to the wall. A WhatsApp number they can text when stuck.

11 Kotter Step 6 · Short-term wins

Step 6 · Generate short-term wins

Step 6 of 8

Find a small, fast, visible win — and tell everyone about it

People stop believing after 4–6 weeks if nothing concrete has changed. You need a real, measurable result early. Then you publicise it — loudly. The story is the win, not the feature.

Bhutanese example · tablet attendance in schools

In Week 3, the principal walks into morning assembly and announces: "Last week, our teachers saved 4 hours combined just on attendance. Pema-ma'am sent her class register from her classroom while the kids were still settling in." That story does more than 10 training sessions.

Pick wins that the sceptics can see

Not "we improved data quality by 18%". That's a slide for the headmaster. Pick the win the resisting teacher feels in her own day.

12 Kotter Step 8 · Institute change

Step 8 · Institute change

Step 8 of 8

Bake the new way into "how things are done here"

Change does not stick because people change their minds. It sticks when the system around them changes — when it would actually be harder to go back than to keep going. If the old system is still running in the back room, it will win.

Bhutanese example · e-leave replacing paper leave forms

Six months in, leave is "mostly digital but HR still accepts paper if you bring it". Result: half the office is still on paper. The fix: HR stops accepting paper. The form is removed from the cabinet. The old leave register is archived. Now there is no back door.

Signals that change has stuck

New hires learn the new way as the only way. Performance reviews track usage. Old templates are deleted. Senior staff use the new system in front of others.

Part Three

The 3-Track
Rollout Plan

Every change runs three tracks at the same time. Drop any one and the rollout limps.

03
14 Three tracks · in parallel

Communication · Training · Support

All three start before launch and continue after. They are not phases — they run together.

📣 1. Communication

What message · when · to whom · by what channel

  • Who needs to hear, in what order? (Boss → managers → staff)
  • What is the message at each stage? (Why · what · when · what's in it for me)
  • Which channel? (Town hall · email · WhatsApp · poster · 1-on-1)
  • How often? (Day 0, Day 7, Day 14, Day 30 — not just one announcement)
🎓 2. Training

Who needs to learn what · by when · in what format

  • Who needs which level of training? (Power users · daily users · occasional users)
  • What format? (Hands-on session · short video · printed cheat sheet · WhatsApp tip-of-the-day)
  • When? (Before go-live, not on the day. Repeat at week 2 and week 4.)
  • How do you know it worked? (A simple test — can they do task X unaided?)
🛟 3. Support

When they get stuck · who do they call

  • A help desk or single phone number — known to everyone
  • Top 10 FAQs — answered in plain Dzongkha / English on a one-pager
  • "Super-users" inside each team — peers who can help in the moment
  • An escalation path for the hard problems — and a promise on response time

Drop comms — rumours fill the silence. Drop training — adoption stalls. Drop support — people give up at the first error.

15 Resistance toolkit

When you meet resistance — what to actually do

Five moves. Use them in order. Most BAs skip the first two and wonder why nothing works.

1. 👂 Listen first

Before you defend the change, ask: "What's your worry?" Then shut up and let them finish. You will hear the real reason — which is rarely what they said in the first sentence.

A clerk who says "I'm too old for this" usually means "I'm scared I'll be slower in front of my juniors."
2. 🙏 Acknowledge the loss

Every change takes something away — even good change. Status, comfort, mastery, a familiar routine. Name it out loud: "Yes, this is harder than what you were used to." That single sentence opens doors.

"You were the fastest with the paper register. We know. The new system will feel slow for 3 weeks." Honest. Disarming.
3. 🎯 Find the WIIFM

"What's In It For Me" — for this person, not the org. Different roles have different answers. The shopkeeper, the cashier, the back-office clerk all need their own version.

Cashier's WIIFM for QR: "No more counting cash at end of shift. Faster close-out. Home earlier."
4. 🌟 Use influencers within the group

Every group has 2–3 people whose opinion others copy. Find them. Win them first. They will do more than 10 of your emails ever could.

In a school, the senior teacher everyone respects. In a shop, the cashier the others ask when they're confused.
5. 🛤️ Make the new way easier than the old way

Same as Kotter Step 5. Worth saying twice. If the old way is one click and the new way is six, you will lose. Do the painful work to flatten the path.

Pre-fill data. Auto-login. Default values. A button that says "Do this for me." Reduce typing.
6. ⚠️ Know when not to push

A small number of people will never come along. Don't burn the rollout fighting them. Move ahead with the willing 80%; the resisters either join when momentum is clear, or they self-select out.

Time-box arguments. After 2 conversations with no movement, stop persuading and keep building wins elsewhere.
16 Pilot vs big-bang

How do you switch over? Two main strategies.

🧪 Pilot — start small, learn, scale

Roll the change out to one team, one shop, one school first. Watch what breaks. Fix it. Then expand to ten. Then a hundred.

Pick a pilot when…
  • The change is risky or new — you don't yet know what will go wrong
  • You can afford the time (you have 3+ months)
  • A bad first impression would kill the whole programme
  • You have a willing, friendly group to test with
Bhutanese example

QR-payments to 200 Thimphu shops — start with 10 shops on one street. Iron out the wrinkles for 4 weeks. Then expand by 30 shops a week.

💥 Big-bang — everyone at once

On the chosen date, everyone switches together. The old system is turned off. There is no "running both."

Pick big-bang when…
  • The change is regulatory or legal — must happen on a fixed date
  • Running two systems in parallel is impossible or dangerous (e.g. payroll, tax)
  • The change is small and well-understood
  • Delay is more expensive than risk
Bhutanese example

A bank moves to a new core banking date — 1 March, midnight, switch. Pilot is impossible because customers can't have two account systems running at once.

Default to pilot when you can. Default to big-bang only when you must.

17 Measuring success

How do you know the rollout is actually working?

"People seem happy" is not a measurement. Pick numbers — the same numbers, every week.

📈 Adoption rate

What % of the target users have used the new system at least once this week?

e.g. 38 of 45 teachers used the tablet attendance app this week → 84%.
⚖️ Volume — old vs new system

How many transactions / records / forms went through the new system vs the old? You want the new line going up and the old line going down.

Week 4: paper leave forms = 12, digital leave forms = 56. Going the right way.
📞 Help-desk ticket trend

How many "how do I…" tickets per week? A good rollout shows tickets rising in week 1, peaking by week 3, falling by week 6. If they keep rising past week 6, something is wrong.

Watch for the same question repeated — that's a training gap, not a software bug.
😊 User satisfaction

One simple question, asked monthly: "On a scale of 1 to 5, how easy is the new way?" Track the score over time. Read the comments — they tell you the next problem.

Score of 2.8 in week 2, 3.6 in week 6 = healthy. Flat or falling = act now.

Pick 2–4 metrics, publish them weekly to the team, and let the trend tell you what to do next.

19 Hands-on · the brief
🛠️ 25 min · groups of 4–5

Pick one scenario.
Build the rollout plan.

25:00

15 min plan · 5 min internal review · 5 min volunteer share. One person from each group will present.

Three scenarios — pick exactly one

A
Replace paper leave forms with a digital leave system at GMC offices

~150 staff across 5 buildings. Mix of senior officers, junior officers, support staff. Some are very comfortable with phones, some are not.

B
Roll out QR-payment acceptance to 200 small Thimphu shops

Mostly family-run shops. Owners aged 30–60. Many already use mBoB personally but have not used it for their business. Tourist season starts in 8 weeks.

C
Schools moving from paper attendance to a tablet-based attendance app

20 schools, ~600 teachers total. Tablets and wifi already provided. Teachers range from 22 to 60 years old. School year starts in 6 weeks.

20 What to produce

For your chosen scenario · 4 deliverables

Use one flipchart sheet or one shared doc. Headings only — no essays.

Your group will hand in:
  1. 3 stakeholder groups — for each, list (a) who they are, (b) their WIIFM in this rollout, (c) the resistance you most expect from them.
  2. 3-track plan — for communication, training, and support — list 2 concrete actions per track. Not "do training" — "1-hour hands-on session for power users in week 2".
  3. Pilot or big-bang? — make the call. Then write 2 sentences justifying it.
  4. 2 success metrics — what will you measure weekly to know it's working?

Tip: do it in this order

Stakeholders + WIIFM first (5 min) — once you know who and what they care about, the comms / training / support choices fall out almost automatically. Then pilot vs big-bang. Metrics last.
