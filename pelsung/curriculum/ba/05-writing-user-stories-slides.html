Part One

The Story
Format

One sentence. Three parts. Read by everyone on the team.

01
04 The shape of a user story

Every user story has the same three parts

User story · template

As a [role],
I want to [action]
so that [value].

A story is short on purpose. It is not the full requirement — it is a placeholder for a conversation. The acceptance criteria (last class) carries the testable detail.

05 Why each part matters

Each part answers a different question

Part 1 · Who
As a [role]

Names the persona. Grounds the story in a real human, not "the user." Connects back to the personas we built last class.

As a Class IV employee on monthly leave…
Part 2 · What
I want to [action]

Describes the behaviour the user wants — not the screen, button, or solution. The dev decides the "how".

…I want to request a leave day…
Part 3 · Why
so that [value]

Forces the WHY. If you cannot finish this part, the story has no business value and shouldn't exist yet.

…so that my supervisor can approve it before I travel.

Three parts. Three different jobs. Each one stops the BA from skipping a question.

06 A good story vs a bad story

Same need · two stories · only one is workable

❌ Bad story
As a user,
I want a button on the home
screen that opens a leave form
with a dropdown for leave type,
so that I can apply for leave.

Vague role ("user"). Action describes the screen, not the behaviour. Value just repeats the action. The dev has no room — and no reason.

✅ Good story
As a Class IV employee,
I want to request a leave day
without visiting the HR office,
so that I don't lose half a day
travelling to submit the form.

Specific role. Action is the behaviour ("request leave online"). Value is real ("don't lose half a day"). The dev can choose buttons, dropdowns, or none.

07
Part Two

Three Story
Smells

If your story has any of these — fix it before passing it on.

02
08 Three smells to spot in your stories

Most "bad" stories fall into one of three traps

1. Solution leaking in

The action describes the screen, button, or design — instead of the behaviour the user wants.

"As a user I want a dropdown to pick my leave type…"
Fix: ask "what do they want to do?" not "what do they want to see?"
2. Missing or empty value

The "so that" repeats the action, or has been deleted because it felt obvious. The WHY is gone.

"…so that I can request leave."
Fix: ask why twice. "Why request leave?" → "So I can travel home."
3. Vague role

"As a user…" tells you nothing. Every system has users. The whole point of the role is to be specific.

"As a user I want to recharge my phone…"
Fix: use the persona name. "As a Tashi Cell prepaid customer…"

If a story smells, the dev guesses. If the dev guesses, you get something nobody asked for.

09
Part Three

INVEST

Six checks for a healthy user story.

03
10 The INVEST checklist

A good story is INVEST · run every story past these six checks

I Independent

The story can be built on its own. It doesn't depend on three other stories shipping first.

"Save a favourite biller" can ship without "Edit a saved biller".
N Negotiable

The story is not a contract. The team can discuss the details and adjust before building.

"…request a leave day" — exact form fields are open for discussion.
V Valuable

Someone real benefits when the story ships. If you can't say who or how — drop the story.

"…so that I don't lose half a day travelling" — the value is plain.
E Estimable

The team can roughly say how big it is. If they can't, the story is too vague or too big.

"Build a school portal" — not estimable. "Show today's attendance" — yes.
S Small

Fits in one short delivery cycle (typically < 1 week of build). Big stories are split.

"Recharge any Tashi Cell number" — small. "Run all telecom payments" — not.
T Testable

You can write acceptance criteria for it. If you can't — the story is wishful, not real.

"App should feel modern" — not testable. AC writes itself for "Save a favourite".
11 INVEST in practice

Run a real story past the six checks

Story under test

As a mBoB customer, I want to recharge my own Tashi Cell number so that I don't run out of balance during the day.

Walk it through INVEST
  • Independent — Yes. Doesn't need "recharge a friend's number" to ship first.
  • Negotiable — Yes. Maximum amount, saved-recharge values, OTP rules — all open for discussion with the bank.
  • Valuable — Yes. Customer keeps connectivity; bank keeps the customer in-app.
  • Estimable — Yes. The team can sketch a rough size — small to medium.
  • Small — Yes. Single feature, one telecom, one customer's own number.
  • Testable — Yes. Easy to write AC: registered customer, valid number, balance updates within a known time.

Six green checks. This story is ready for AC.

12 Epic · Feature · Story · Task

Stories sit inside a hierarchy

Example: an Online Leave Request System for a government office.

EpicMonths of work
Online Leave Request System for the officeBig goal. Many teams. Months of work. Cannot be built as one piece.
FeatureWeeks of work
Employee self-service leave applicationOne slice of the epic. Still bigger than one story.
StoryDays of work
As a Class IV employee, I want to request a leave day online so that I don't lose half a day travelling to HR.One user, one behaviour, one piece of value. Has acceptance criteria.
TaskHours of work
Build the leave-type dropdown · validate dates · save the application to the database · send a notification to the supervisor.Dev's internal breakdown. The BA usually doesn't write tasks.
13
Part Four

Story + AC
Paired Artifacts

A story without AC is wishful thinking.

04
14 Story is the WHAT/WHY · AC is the verifiable behaviour

A story always travels with its AC

User Story
What we want and why

As a library member,
I want to check out a book online
so that I can reserve it before walking to the library.

Acceptance Criteria
How we know it's done

Given a member is logged in and the book shows "Available", when they tap "Check out", then the book is held in their name for 24 hours and shows "Reserved · expires tomorrow".

Given the book is already checked out by someone else, when the member taps "Check out", then the system shows "Currently unavailable. Join the waitlist?" and offers a Join button.

Given a member already has 3 books on hold, when they try to check out a fourth, then the system shows "Limit reached. Return one to reserve another." and does not place the hold.

Story is one sentence. AC is three to seven testable behaviours. Together they are a complete unit of work — never separated.

Part Five · Hands-on

Rewrite Three
Vague Requirements

Pairs · 20 minutes · story + INVEST + 2–3 AC each.

05
16 The brief · 20 minutes

Take each vague requirement · turn it into a clean story + AC

Raw requirement #1 · Leave

"The system should let employees request leave easily and managers approve it without too many clicks."

Raw requirement #2 · mBoB recharge

"Customers want to recharge their phones from the bank app."

Raw requirement #3 · School portal

"We need parents to be able to check kids' attendance on the school portal."

Step 1
Write a story

One story per requirement. Use As a [specific role] / I want to [behaviour] / so that [real value].

Step 2
Run INVEST

Walk each story past all six letters. Mark which pass and which need work. Fix any that fail.

Step 3
Add 2–3 AC

For each story write 2–3 acceptance criteria in Given / When / Then form. Cover happy path + at least one edge.

Pair up · 6–7 min per requirement · two pairs share their leave story in debrief.

17 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ A user story is one sentence: As a [role], I want [action], so that [value]
  • ✅ Each part has a job: role grounds the story · action is behaviour, not a screen · value forces the WHY
  • ✅ Watch for three smells: solution leaking in · missing value · vague role
  • ✅ Run every story past INVEST — Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable
  • ✅ Stories sit inside a hierarchy: Epic → Feature → Story → Task
  • ✅ A story never travels alone — it is paired with its acceptance criteria from last class
  • ✅ The story is the placeholder for a conversation, not the contract

A clear story is the cheapest thing a BA writes — and the most expensive thing to get wrong.
