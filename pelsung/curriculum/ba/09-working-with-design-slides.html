03
Part One

Where Design
Sits

After stories. Before code. The shape of the product gets decided here.

01
04 The order of work

Three roles · three jobs · in this order

Step 1 · The BA
Writes intent

User stories, acceptance criteria, personas, business rules. Words on a page. What the product should do, and why.

Output → stories + AC, ready for design
Step 2 · The Designer
Makes form

Turns those words into screens. Buttons, layout, flow, colour, typography. How the product looks and feels to the user.

Output → wireframes, mockups, prototype, specs
Step 3 · The Developer
Builds it

Turns the design + AC into working software. Picks the technology. How the product runs under the hood.

Output → tested, deployable code

Design is not optional, and it is not "decoration". It is the bridge between your words and the developer's code. If the bridge is wrong, the code will be wrong.

05 Class 06 vs Class 09

You already sketched. Today is different.

Class 06 · Wireframing for Communication
You sketched to communicate

You drew rough boxes-and-lines so a developer or stakeholder could see what was in your head. Your sketches were a conversation tool — not the final design.

Class 09 · Working with Design
Today you receive design

A real designer hands you finished screens. You are not the designer. You are the BA who must check the design against the stories — and tell the designer what's missing.

SELISE Cohort 3 has no Design vertical — but in real jobs and in your capstone, a designer will hand you screens. You need the muscle to review them well.

06
Part Two

What a Designer
Hands Off

Four artifacts, increasing in fidelity. You will see all of them.

02
07 The handoff ladder

From rough boxes to a clickable, build-ready spec

1 · Wireframe
Boxes and lines

No colour. No real text. Shows what's on the screen and roughly where.

2 · Mockup
Pixel-accurate look

Real colours, fonts, real text, real icons. Looks like the final app — but no clicking yet.

3 · Prototype
Clickable mockups

A Figma link. Tap a button on screen 1 → it goes to screen 2. You can walk the flow.

4 · Dev specs
Build-ready details

Sizes, spacing, exact colours, behaviours, exported icons and images. Everything the developer needs to build.

A real handoff often skips wireframes and starts at mockups. Your job is the same either way: review against the AC.

08 What's actually in a handoff

A typical Figma handoff page contains all of this

1. Screens

Every screen the user can see — happy path and edges.

2. Specs

Sizes, colours, fonts, padding. So a developer doesn't guess.

3. Behaviours

"Tap this → that happens." Loading, success, error.

4. Assets

Exported icons and images, ready to drop into code.

5. Flow links

Arrows or a clickable prototype showing screen-to-screen flow.

6. Edge states

Empty, loading, error, offline, no-permission.

09
Part Three

The BA's
Review Lens

Five questions. Every screen. Every time.

03
10 Five questions to ask every design

You are not judging visual taste. You are checking against intent.

"I don't like the orange" is not a BA review. These five questions are.

1
Match to story & AC?

Does what's on screen actually deliver the user story and every acceptance criterion?

"AC says user can save biller — where's the button?"
2
Anything in AC missing?

Walk every AC. Tick off where each one shows up in the design. Untickable AC = gap.

"AC 4 says 'limit reached' message — I don't see it anywhere."
3
Edges handled?

Empty state. Error state. Loading state. Offline. No permission. Are they shown?

"What does this list look like for a brand-new user?"
4
Accessibility basics?

Text readable against background? Buttons big enough to tap (~44 × 44 px)? Form fields labelled?

"Grey text on light grey — can my mother read this?"
5
Flow makes sense to the persona?

Walk it as the user from your persona doc. Does each step feel obvious to them?

"A first-time user — would they know to scroll?"
11 Heuristics · quick recap

Nielsen's 10 heuristics — your reference card

A one-page reference to glance at while reviewing a design. Don't memorise — recognise the patterns. (See Extra E1 — UX & HCI Basics for a deeper dive if you want one.)

#1Visibility of system status
#6Recognition rather than recall
#2Match between system & real world
#7Flexibility & efficiency of use
#3User control & freedom
#8Aesthetic & minimalist design
#4Consistency & standards
#9Help users recognise, diagnose & recover from errors
#5Error prevention
#10Help & documentation

When you spot a problem in a design, naming the heuristic makes your feedback land. "Heuristic #1 — there's no loading indicator on the transfer screen" beats "feels slow".

12 Heuristics · in apps you already use

Spotting heuristic violations in everyday Bhutanese apps

Heuristic #1 · Visibility
mBoB · funds transfer

You tap "Send", and the screen freezes for 4 seconds with no spinner, no "Processing", nothing. You don't know if it worked or if the network is down. Status is invisible.

Heuristic #5 · Error prevention
Druk Air booking

The date picker lets you pick a return date earlier than the departure date. Only when you submit do you get a "Bad dates" error. The design should not let the bad input happen.

Heuristic #9 · Recover from errors
Tashi Cell self-service

You enter the wrong recharge amount. The screen says "Error code 4012". Nothing about what went wrong. No "Try again". The error message tells you nothing actionable.

Reviewer's habit: when a screen feels off, ask "which heuristic is being broken here?" — that's the language to use with the designer.

13
Part Four

Giving Useful
Design Feedback

Specific. Behavioural. Action-oriented.

04
14 Bad → good feedback

Three rewrites — same problem, different result

A designer cannot act on a feeling. They can act on a specific, observable problem with a suggested next step.

❌ Bad — taste-based

"I don't like the colours."
What does the designer change? Nothing — there's no problem named.

✅ Good — specific & actionable

"The orange CTA blends into the orange header — Heuristic #4, consistency. Can we test the SELISE blue from the brand palette so the button stands out?"

❌ Bad — vague

"This screen is confusing."
Confusing how? To whom? Doing what?

✅ Good — persona + behaviour

"Walking this as our 'first-time user' persona — once they tap Continue, there's no indication anything happened. Heuristic #1. Can we add a loading state?"

❌ Bad — opinion as fact

"The button is too small."
Smaller than what? By how much?

✅ Good — measurable

"The 'Submit' button is ~28 px tall. Mobile target guideline is ~44 px. On a phone it'll be hard to tap accurately. Can we bump it to match the other primary buttons?"

15 The handoff conversation

Never approve a design without walking every story it covers

Design review is a meeting — not an email. Schedule 30 minutes with the designer and run this loop.

Step 1
Open the stories

Have the user stories and AC open beside the design file. You will read them out loud.

Step 2
Walk every AC

For each AC, point to where in the design it is satisfied. If you can't point — flag it.

Step 3
Walk the edges

Ask: "Show me empty. Show me error. Show me loading." If the designer doesn't have it — flag it.

Step 4
Write it down

Every flag becomes a written comment in Figma or a row in a feedback list. Verbal feedback evaporates.

The one rule

If a story has no design — or a design has no story — you've found a gap. Both directions matter. Never let the conversation end without resolving every gap or writing it down.

Part Five · Hands-on

Flawed Handoff

Pairs · 20 min · Pelsung leave-request app.

05
17 The flawed handoff

A designer just sent these 4 screens for "Pelsung Leave Request"

1 · Home / My leaves
2 · New request
3 · Confirm submit
4 · Status
Activity · 20 min
In pairs, review these 4 screens with the BA review lens
  • 5 min — read the brief below + the AC. Walk all 4 screens silently.
  • 10 min — list every problem you find. Tag each with the lens question (1–5) or the heuristic number.
  • 5 min — for your top 3 problems, write the feedback you'd send the designer (specific, behavioural, action-oriented).

Story: "As a Pelsung trainee, I want to request leave from my phone so my supervisor can approve it without paperwork." AC includes: see my pending leaves on home, submit a new request with type/dates/reason, get confirmation, see status with reason if rejected. Brand colour is SELISE blue (#0066B2).

18 Debrief · what a strong reviewer would catch

A defensible list of findings — mapped to the lens

Screen 1
No empty state. A first-time trainee with zero leaves sees a blank box. No "You haven't requested any leave yet — tap + to start."
Q3 · edges · H#1
Screen 1
"Process" button is meaningless. Process what? The label should describe the action: "+ New leave request".
Q5 · flow · H#2, H#4
All screens
Brand colour is wrong. Header is orange (#F08533) — brief said SELISE blue (#0066B2). Doesn't match the brand palette.
Q1 · match · H#4
Screen 2
Required fields not marked. No asterisks, no "(required)". User won't know what's mandatory until submit fails.
Q4 · accessibility · H#5
Screen 2
Date fields too small. ~14 px tall — way under 44 px tap target. Hard to tap on a phone.
Q4 · accessibility
Screen 2
"Go" is the wrong submit label. The user is submitting a leave request — say "Submit request".
Q5 · flow · H#2
Screen 3
Low-contrast text (#D4D6D8 on white). Reason and approver are nearly invisible. Fails accessibility contrast.
Q4 · accessibility
Screen 3
"Done" is ambiguous. Has the request been submitted, or is "Done" what the user taps to submit? Should be "Submit" or "Confirm & submit".
Q5 · flow · H#1, H#3
Screen 3
No loading or error path. What happens between tap and the next screen? AC implies confirmation must be shown.
Q3 · edges · H#1
Screen 4
"Pending" gives no information. Pending with whom? Since when? AC says rejected status must show a reason — what about pending or approved?
Q2 · AC missing · H#1
Screen 4
No rejected or approved variant. AC requires "see status with reason if rejected" — that screen variant is missing entirely.
Q2 · AC missing

If your pair caught 6+ of these, you're already reviewing like a real BA. The lens questions are how you got there — every finding is tagged to one.

19 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ Design sits between BA and Dev — words become form, then form becomes code
  • ✅ A handoff includes screens, specs, behaviours, assets, flow links, edge states
  • ✅ The BA review lens is five questions — not visual taste
  • ✅ Use the Nielsen heuristic numbers to name problems precisely
  • ✅ Feedback must be specific, behavioural, action-oriented — never "I don't like it"
  • ✅ Never approve a design without walking every story and every edge
  • ✅ A story with no design, or a design with no story, is a gap

Your job is not to design. Your job is to make sure the design delivers the story.
