Part One

Scope

The "yes" list and the "no" list — both matter equally.

01
06 In-scope & out-of-scope

Example · planning a small Bhutanese wedding

Before you list tasks, you decide what's in the project and what's not. Saying "no" out loud is half the work.

✅ In scope
  • 120 guests, one venue in Thimphu
  • Lunch · 3 dishes · vegetarian option
  • Decoration · main hall + entrance
  • Music · one local band, 2 hours
  • Photographer · half-day
❌ Out of scope
  • Pre-wedding party (separate event, separate budget)
  • Traditional 3-day celebration — only 1 day
  • Travel for outstation guests
  • Live video stream
  • Custom souvenir gifts for every guest

Why the "no" list matters

If you only write the "yes" list, every aunt and uncle will quietly add things — souvenir gifts, an extra band, a pre-wedding ceremony. By the wedding day you've spent double. The "no" list is your shield.

09
Part Three

Three Estimation
Techniques

Three simple ways to put a number on each task.

03
10 Pick the simplest one that fits

Three techniques · use whichever the task allows

1
Expert judgment

Ask someone who has actually done this thing before. Their gut number is usually within 30%.

"Aunt Pema has cooked for 5 weddings. She says food prep takes 2 days." Use 2 days.
2
Analogous estimation

Find a past project that was similar. Use its actual numbers as your starting point — adjust for differences.

"Last year's Tshechu took 8 weeks of prep with 200 guests. We have 150 — start with 6 weeks."
3
Three-point estimation

Best case + likely case + worst case, weighted. Forces you to face uncertainty instead of pretending.

"Booking the venue: best 1 day, likely 3 days, worst 7 days." We'll do the math next slide.

When to use which

Expert — when someone has truly done this exact task. Fastest.
Analogous — when you have past project numbers but not the same person.
Three-point — when the task is uncertain or risky. Slower, but honest about the range.

11 Three-point · worked example

Task: "Book the wedding hall"

You ask three short questions and apply one simple formula.

Best case (B)
1 day
Hall is free, owner agrees.
Most likely (M)
3 days
Visit · negotiate · sign.
Worst case (W)
7 days
Owner away · need backup hall.
Estimate
3.3 days
Round up to 4.
Estimate = (B + 4×M + W) ÷ 6  =  (1 + 4×3 + 7) ÷ 6 = 20 ÷ 6 ≈ 3.3 days

Why the formula works

Most-likely is weighted four times. Best and worst pull the number a little — but the everyday case wins. The formula is older than computers and still does the job.

When to use it

Use three-point on the 5–10 most uncertain tasks in your project. Don't bother for "buy candles" — just guess. Spend the time where the risk is.

12 MoSCoW recap · prioritize the WBS

Now tag every task in the WBS · what's a Must vs a Could?

Must

Without it, the wedding fails.
Venue, food, invites.

S

Important but day works without it.
Photographer, band.

C
Could

Nice if budget allows.
Souvenirs, second cake.

Not this time.

This is a recap, not a re-teach

You learned MoSCoW (and RICE, MVP, story-point sizing) in BA+QA Combined Class 05. Today we just apply MoSCoW to our WBS — tag every task and use it to decide cuts when time runs short.

15 Dependencies & the longest chain

Some tasks must wait for others · the longest waiting chain decides the deadline

Dependency · plain English

Task B is dependent on Task A if you can't start B until A is done.

  • Send invites depends on having the venue (so guests know where).
  • Cook food depends on having the menu and the cook hired.
  • Set up the hall depends on the rice arriving.

The longest chain · "critical path"

Look at all your dependency chains. The one that takes longest end-to-end sets the project deadline. If anything on that chain slips, the whole project slips.

Example chain: Book venue → Pick menu → Hire cook → Order rice → Cook → Wedding day.
If venue takes 1 extra week, the whole wedding moves a week.

What to actually do

For your project: list 3–5 dependencies. Find the longest chain. Watch those tasks like a hawk — that's where slippage hurts most.

16 Buffer · plan for things going wrong

Add 20–30% buffer · but only on the risky tasks

❌ Bad: pad every task

Every task gets +50% added "just in case." The plan now says 3 months for what should take 6 weeks. Stakeholders don't believe it. Team gets lazy because the buffer is huge.

✅ Good: pad the riskiest 5

Pick the 3–5 tasks where you're least certain (new vendor · new venue · external dependency). Add 20–30% buffer to those only. Keep the safe tasks tight.

A simple rule of thumb

If your gut says "3 weeks" for an uncertain task, write 4 weeks in the plan. If a stakeholder pushes back, point at the worst-case from your three-point estimate.

17 Risk register · top 5 things that could go wrong

A simple table — risk · likelihood · what we'd do

Write this once at the start. Re-read at every weekly check-in. That's it.

RiskLikelihoodImpactMitigation · what we'd do
Cook is double-booked closer to the dateMedHighConfirm cook in writing in week 1. Keep one backup cook's number.
Heavy rain on the day · outdoor decor ruinedMedMedTarpaulin rented in advance. Indoor backup spot identified by W4.
Guest count grows by 30% as RSVPs come inHighMedOrder food for +20% from day one. Cap invites at week 4.
Band cancels in the final weekLowHighSign a deposit-paid contract. Have one local backup name ready.
Photographer falls sick on the dayLowMedTwo phone numbers from the studio, not one. Studio's standby policy in writing.

Five is enough

Don't write 30 risks. Pick the top 5 (highest likelihood × highest impact). When something changes, swap a row. We'll cover how risks become formal change requests in BA Class 14 · Change Management.

19 The brief

Pick one project · plan it in 25 minutes

🎓 Pelsung graduation ceremony

Plan a one-day graduation event for 250 trainees and family. Venue, programme, food, certificates, photography.

🥟 Open a 5-table momo shop in Thimphu

Plan opening day of a small momo shop. Location, license, kitchen, staff, supplies, opening event.

🧠 Mental-health awareness camp at GMC

Plan a 1-day awareness camp. Speakers, venue, registrations, materials, medical standby, communications.

Your group's deliverables · 25 minutes
  • 1. Write a short scope: 5 in-scope items + 5 out-of-scope items.
  • 2. Build a WBS · 8 to 12 tasks total.
  • 3. Tag every task M / S / C / W (MoSCoW).
  • 4. Three-point estimate the top 5 tasks (best · likely · worst → formula).
  • 5. Sketch a rough Gantt on paper · 4–6 weeks across the top, tasks down the side.
  • 6. Write 3 risks · likelihood (L/M/H) · what you'd do about each.
