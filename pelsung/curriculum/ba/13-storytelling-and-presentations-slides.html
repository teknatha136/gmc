03 Why this matters
🎤 A familiar moment

Your aunt asks you which phone to buy for your cousin. You spent two evenings comparing six phones. You know the right answer. But when you sit down with her, you start with screen sizes, then battery, then prices, then warranties…

…and after 20 minutes she just buys whatever the shopkeeper suggests.

A BA's job is full of moments like this. Build vs buy. Vendor A vs B. Change this or change that. The analysis is only half the work. The other half is making it land in someone else's head.

04
Part One

Audience
First

Who is in the room? Their job decides your story.

01
05 Same recommendation, three rooms

Before you write a single slide, ask: who is reading this?

The same recommendation needs three different presentations for three different audiences.

1 · Decision-makers
The dzongdag
  • 1–3 people, time-poor
  • Want the recommendation up front
  • Will skim, not read
  • Care about cost, risk, outcome
"Sir, we recommend X. Here's why in three points."
2 · Influencers
Your peers and seniors
  • Want the detail
  • Want to challenge your logic
  • Care about how you ruled out other options
  • Help shape the final decision
"Walk us through the comparison. Why not B?"
3 · Implementers
Devs, designers, ops
  • Want clarity on what to build or do
  • Care about scope, dependencies
  • Don't need the "why we chose this"
  • Need crisp acceptance criteria
"What do I need to deliver, and by when?"

Most BA decks fail because they were built for the wrong audience.

06 A note on style

Bhutanese conversation has a long warm-up. Decks don't.

😴 Conversational style

  • "As you know, last year we noticed…"
  • Background first, problem second
  • Recommendation comes after 10 minutes
  • Polite, indirect, lots of context

Great for tea. Bad for the dzongdag's 15 minutes.

⚡ Recommendation style

  • "We recommend mBoB-Lite. Three reasons."
  • Recommendation first, evidence second
  • Background only if asked
  • Direct, structured, time-aware

Feels abrupt at first. It isn't — it's respectful of their time.

You are not being rude. A busy decision-maker thanks you for getting to the point.

Part Two

The Pyramid
Principle

Recommendation first. Then key points. Then evidence.

02
08 A pyramid you'd actually present

Top says the answer. Below it explains why. Below that proves it.

We recommend buying mBoB-Lite rather than building our own payment app.
Faster
Live in 6 weeks vs ~10 months in-house.
Cheaper
~Nu 4 lakh/year vs ~Nu 80 lakh build.
Lower risk
Already used by 200k+ Bhutanese; RMA-compliant.
Vendor SLA, 3 references, 14-day pilot result.
Cost breakdown — license, support, devices.
Compliance letter from RMA, security audit.

A decision-maker reads only the top box and decides. An influencer reads down one level. An implementer keeps going. Same pyramid serves all three.

09
Part Three

The Story Arc

Five parts. One slide each. Use this every time.

03
10 Story arc · 1 of 5
1

Context

"What's the world we're in? What's true today?"

Example. "GMC will open citizen services online in October. Today we have no payment system. Every other agency uses mBoB or builds custom."

Keep it short. 2–3 sentences. Just enough so the audience and you start in the same place.

11 Story arc · 2 of 5
2

Conflict

"What's the problem? Why are we even talking?"

Example. "We need to accept payments by October. Building it ourselves takes 10 months and costs ~Nu 80 lakh. We don't have either."

A clear problem creates a need for a decision. No conflict = no reason to listen.

12 Story arc · 3 of 5
3

Choice

"What are our options? What did we consider?"

Example. "Three options: (A) Build in-house · (B) Use mBoB-Lite · (C) Use Druk Pay. We compared on time, cost, compliance, support."

Showing options builds trust. The audience sees you didn't jump to a conclusion — you weighed alternatives.

13 Story arc · 4 of 5
4

Recommendation

"What do we suggest, and why?"

Example. "We recommend mBoB-Lite. It's faster (6 weeks), cheaper (Nu 4 lakh/year), and lower-risk (RMA-compliant, already at scale)."

This is the headline. Three reasons is the sweet spot. One sounds weak. Five sounds desperate.

14 Story arc · 5 of 5
5

Call to action

"What decision do we need from you today?"

Example. "We need approval today to sign the mBoB-Lite contract by end of week, so we go live by October 15."

If you don't ask for a decision, you won't get one. Be specific: what you need, by when, from whom.

Part Four

Slide Hygiene

Six small rules. They make every deck better.

04
16 Six rules · and a side-by-side

Six rules every BA slide should follow

1 · One idea per slide

If a slide has two points, split it. Two slides > one crowded slide.

2 · Headlines are full sentences

"Costs are rising" is a topic. "Costs are up 30% this year" is the takeaway. Use the takeaway.

3 · Less text. More visual.

If you'd put 6 bullets, use a chart, a number, or a single image instead.

4 · Contrast — use SELISE blue

One brand colour for emphasis. No rainbow. No 6 different fonts.

5 · No bullet salad

Max 3–5 items. Parallel structure (all start with a verb, or all are nouns).

6 · Footer essentials

Page number · your name · date. So a printed copy still tells someone what they're holding.

❌ Crowded — fails rules 1, 2, 3, 5
Costs Overview
  • The current cost of building our own payment system in-house is approximately Nu 80 lakh, which is significantly higher than the alternative which is to use mBoB-Lite at around Nu 4 lakh per year.
  • It will also take 10 months to build vs 6 weeks to integrate.
  • There are also concerns regarding compliance with RMA regulations.
  • Support and maintenance costs need to be considered.
  • Risk of going over budget is high.
  • Other agencies use mBoB-Lite already.
✅ Clean — follows all six rules

17 The headline test

If someone reads only your headlines, do they get the story?

Three slides. Their headlines alone. Read top-to-bottom.

Slide 4 headline
Building in-house takes 10 months — we have 5.
Slide 5 headline
mBoB-Lite goes live in 6 weeks at 5% of the cost.
Slide 6 headline
We recommend mBoB-Lite. Sign by Friday to launch by 15 October.

A reader who only saw these three sentences still gets the answer. That's the test.

18 Common BA presentation failures

Four ways most first-time BA decks die

1 · Burying the recommendation

Slide 14 of 16 is the first time you say what you actually think they should do.

Fix: recommendation in slide 1 or 2. The rest is the "why".
2 · Walls of text

You read the slide. They read the slide. Nobody listens to anybody.

Fix: ≤ 30 words on a content slide. Charts, numbers, or one clear sentence.
3 · "Background slides" that aren't background

5 slides of history before the problem is even stated.

Fix: 1 slide of context, 1 of conflict. Move the rest to an appendix.
4 · No call to action

You finish, the room goes quiet, the dzongdag says "thank you", nothing happens.

Fix: last slide names the decision, the deadline, and the decider.

Hands-on · 25 minutes

Build a 5-slide pitch

Pick one brief. Use index cards or paper. One slide per part of the story arc.

Brief A
GMC citizen portal vendor

Recommend Vendor A or Vendor B for the new GMC online citizen services portal. (Mock specs are on your handout.)

Brief B
Build vs SaaS

Recommend buying a SaaS product or building in-house for a school attendance system in Gelephu.

Brief C
MVP feature set

Recommend the MVP feature set for a Pelsung trainee feedback app — what's in for v1, what waits for v2.

15 min build · 5 min walk-around · 5 min for 3 trainees to give 90-second pitches · group critiques each pitch using the headline test.

05
20 How to work · pair rubric

5 slides. Story-arc order. One headline per slide.

Build (15 min) · pair up

  • One card per slide · 5 cards total
  • Slide 1 = Context · 2 = Conflict · 3 = Choice · 4 = Recommendation · 5 = Call to action
  • Write only the headline (full-sentence takeaway) on each card
  • One supporting visual or 1–3 short bullets max under the headline

Critique (with the headline test)

  • Read only the 5 headlines aloud. Do they tell the story?
  • Where would the dzongdag stop reading and still know what to do?
  • Is there exactly one clear ask on slide 5?
  • Did anyone fall into the four common failures?

Pitch tip · 90 seconds is enough

Lead with the recommendation. "We recommend X. Three reasons." Then context only if asked. End with the ask. If you finish in 60 seconds, that's fine — better short than rambling.

21 Wrap-up
🎯 Key Takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ Audience first. Decision-maker, influencer, implementer — different rooms, different decks.
  • ✅ Pyramid principle. Recommendation on top. Key reasons below. Evidence at the bottom.
  • ✅ Story arc: Context → Conflict → Choice → Recommendation → Call to action.
  • ✅ Headlines are full sentences. The takeaway, not the topic.
  • ✅ Slide hygiene: one idea, less text, contrast, no bullet salad, footer always.
  • ✅ The headline test: reading only the headlines should still tell the story.
  • ✅ End with an ask. What decision · by when · from whom.

A great BA isn't the one with the best analysis. It's the one whose analysis lands.
