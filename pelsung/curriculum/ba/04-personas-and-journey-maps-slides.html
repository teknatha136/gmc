03 The empathy gap
🤔 Think about it

Who is "the user"?

A team builds an mBoB feature. Someone says "the user wants this." But the developer is picturing a 28-year-old in Thimphu with fast Wi-Fi. The BA is picturing a shopkeeper in Phuentsholing. The QA is picturing her own grandmother in Punakha.

Three different "users." Three different products.

A persona is how we all start picturing the same person.

04
Part One

What a Persona
Actually Is

A fictional person, built from real research.

01
05 The 30-second definition

A persona is a fictional but realistic picture of a real type of user — built from things we actually learned about people.

Think of it like a character in a novel — not a real person, but believable. You can guess what they would do in a new situation, because you know who they are.

Why "fictional but realistic"?

If we talk about "Pema, 34, shopkeeper in Gelephu," the whole team pictures the same Pema. If we just say "the shopkeeper user," everyone pictures someone different.

06 What it is · what it isn't

Get this right or the whole exercise breaks

✅ A persona IS
  • A picture of a type of user
  • Built from real research — interviews, observation, surveys
  • Specific enough to feel like a person
  • Useful for making product decisions ("would Tashi do this?")
  • Updated when we learn new things
❌ A persona is NOT
  • A real, named person from your village
  • A demographic stereotype ("women aged 40+ like…")
  • "The average user" — the average user doesn't exist
  • Random invention — guessing at a desk
  • A marketing target list

If you made it up at your desk in 5 minutes, it's not a persona — it's a guess.

07 Where the persona comes from

Real research feeds the persona

A persona is only as good as the homework behind it.

🎤 Interviews

Sit with 5–10 actual users. Ask them about their day, their frustrations, what they were trying to do.

👀 Observation

Watch them use the current product (or the competitor). Notice where they pause, retry, ask for help.

📊 Surveys & data

Look at support tickets, app reviews, age data, location data — patterns across many users.

Rule of thumb: if you can't say "we learned this from talking to 5 real people," you don't have a persona — you have an assumption.

09 The persona template · 6 fields

The persona template · 6 fields

One page, six labelled boxes. This is the shape you'll fill out in the exercise.

👤
Persona
6 fields · 1 page
Anchor: Tashi · 62 · Punakha
🪪 Name + photo

A real-sounding name and a simple picture so the team pictures the same someone. Tashi Wangmo, with a small portrait.

📋 Demographics

Just enough background to ground the persona — age, place, work, language, tech comfort. 62, retired teacher in Punakha, Dzongkha first.

🎯 Goals

What does this person actually want to accomplish, in life terms not button terms? Tashi wants to receive his pension on his phone without help.

😣 Pain points

Real frustrations they feel today, in their words — not a wishlist. "The OTP disappears before I can read it."

📱 Behaviors

The apps and habits they actually have today — not what we wish they did. Uses WhatsApp daily; opens mBoB only when his son visits.

🎬 Scenarios

A short story of when they'd use the product — time, place, who's around. 1st of the month, 8 am at home, pension SMS just arrived.

Six fields. One page. Pin it to the wall — and from now on, decisions get tested against this person.

10 Worked example · Tashi

Meet Tashi — a full persona on one page

🧓
Tashi Wangmo
"Pension by phone"
62 · Punakha
Demographics

62 yrs · retired primary-school teacher · Punakha · Dzongkha first language · basic Android phone (gift from son).

Goals

Receive pension at home each month. Send small amounts to grandchildren. Avoid the long walk to the bank.

Pain points

OTP SMS disappears too fast. Text is small. Forgets MPIN. Worries about pressing wrong button and losing money.

Behaviors

WhatsApp daily with family. YouTube on home Wi-Fi. Calls son when stuck. Never uses email. Doesn't trust ads.

Typical scenario

1st of the month, 8 am at home. Pension SMS arrives. Wants to send Nu. 5,000 to his daughter before she leaves for work. Grandson is at school — no help nearby.

Now when the team designs an OTP screen, someone says "would Tashi be able to read it in time?" — and the question is concrete, not abstract.

Part Three

Journey Maps

The steps a persona takes to reach a goal — and how they feel along the way.

03
12 What a journey map is

A journey map shows every step a persona takes to reach a goal — and what they do, think, and feel at each step.

Think of it like a story-board for one trip. Across the top: the phases of the trip. Down the side: what's happening inside the user (action, thought, emotion) and where they touch your product.

Why bother?

A persona tells you who. A journey map tells you what they go through. Until you draw the steps, your product is a pile of screens, not an experience.

13 The five rows of a journey map

Across: phases. Down: what we want to know.

🦵 Action

What the user actually does — open app, tap button, enter number, ask grandson.

💭 Thought

What's running through their head — "is this safe?", "did it work?", "where's the confirm button?"

😀 Emotion

How they feel — hopeful, confused, frustrated, relieved. Use simple emojis to mark highs and lows.

📱 Touchpoint

Which screen, message, app, or person they interact with at this step.

💡 Opportunity

Where the experience could be better. Each pain point is a chance for the team to act.

Five rows is the standard. Some teams use four. Don't add ten — the map stops being readable.

Part Four · Hands-on

Build a Persona
+ Journey Map

Groups of 3 · 25 minutes · pick one scenario.

04
16 The brief

Pick ONE scenario. Build one persona + a 5-step journey.

Groups of 3. One sheet per group. Use the persona template (6 fields) and the journey grid (5 phases × 5 rows).

Scenario A
🧓 Bhutanese elder using a pension portal

An older relative who needs to check or claim their pension online from home for the first time.

Scenario B
🛂 First-time tourist booking a Bhutan visa online

A foreign visitor planning their first trip — needs to apply, pay, and receive an e-visa via the official portal.

Scenario C
🎓 Pelsung trainee enrolling in a new course

One of you, signing up for a fresh course on the learning portal — registering, choosing a track, getting confirmed.

Scenario D
🏪 Shopkeeper accepting first QR payment

A small Druk PNB / mBoB shopkeeper, taking their very first QR payment from a customer at the counter.

17 How to work · 25 minutes

Three roles in your group of 3

📝 Scribe

Holds the pen. Writes the persona fields and the journey rows on the sheet.

🗣️ Storyteller

Tells the story step-by-step ("she opens the app… she sees…"). Narrates each phase out loud.

🤔 Sceptic

Pushes back: "Is this real or are we guessing? Would she really do that? Where's the pain?"

Suggested timing

0–10 min: Build the persona (all 6 fields) · 10–20 min: Map the 5-step journey (action, thought, emotion, touchpoint, opportunity) · 20–25 min: Pick the biggest emotional dip and write one improvement. Two volunteer groups present in debrief.

Part Five

What These
Feed Into

A persona on the wall is decoration. A persona in your specs is leverage.

05
19 Where personas + journeys go next

From empathy → into the artifacts you're already learning

Feeds →
📜 Requirements

Pain points become real requirements. "OTP must stay on screen for 30 seconds" — because Tashi told us why.

Feeds →
📝 User stories

"As Tashi, I want larger OTP digits so that I can read them before they disappear."

Feeds →
✅ Acceptance criteria

Each opportunity becomes a testable AC. The journey map is a checklist of what "done" must cover.

Feeds →
🎨 Design

Designers test mockups against the journey: "would the screen at step 3 actually help Tashi?"

Feeds →
🧪 Testing

QA uses the journey to check the real flow — not just one screen at a time.

If a persona never shows up in any other document, it was a poster. Use it.

20 Wrap-up
🎯 Key takeaways

What we covered today

  • ✅ A persona is a fictional but realistic picture of a user type — built from real research, not guessing
  • ✅ Six fields: name + photo · demographics · goals · pain points · behaviors · scenarios
  • ✅ A persona is NOT a real person, a stereotype, or "the average user"
  • ✅ A journey map shows the steps a persona takes to reach a goal — across phases, down rows (action / thought / emotion / touchpoint / opportunity)
  • ✅ The emotional dip in the journey is where the team should focus first
  • ✅ Personas + journeys feed into requirements, user stories, AC, design, and testing
  • ✅ A persona on the wall is decoration. A persona inside your specs is leverage.

"Would Tashi be able to do this?" — the most useful question a BA can ask.
