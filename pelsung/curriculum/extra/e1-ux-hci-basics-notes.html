Part 1

Why this matters for every role

UX is often seen as "the designer's job". It isn't. Every person shipping software makes UX decisions — often without realising it:

  • BAs who write specs choose what belongs on a screen and what doesn't.
  • Developers pick button sizes, error messages, loading states, keyboard behaviour — dozens of micro-decisions per feature.
  • QA testers spot bad UX long before they find functional bugs — but only if they have the vocabulary to name what's wrong.
  • Designers obviously — but their work is easier when the rest of the team speaks the same language.

AI changes this too

Claude Code and similar tools can generate a working UI in seconds. They follow patterns from their training data. Sometimes those patterns are good. Often they're generic. Only a human who knows UX can tell the difference — and push the AI towards the right answer.

Part 2

UX · UI · HCI — what's the difference?

  • UX (User Experience) is how it feels to use a product. Easy? Frustrating? Trustworthy? UX is the outcome. Every decision contributes: design, copy, speed, reliability, how errors are handled.
  • UI (User Interface) is the visible surface — buttons, colours, layout, typography. UI is a subset of UX. Great UI can still deliver bad UX if the flow is broken, the app is slow, or the error messages are useless.
  • HCI (Human–Computer Interaction) is the academic field studying how humans and computers communicate. HCI gives us principles (for example, Fitts's Law). UX practitioners apply them.

A useful test

If someone says "the UI is beautiful but I can't find the button", that's a UX problem (findability) hiding behind good UI. If someone says "the button is ugly but I know exactly what it does", that's bad UI, acceptable UX.

Part 3

Nielsen's 10 heuristics

Published in 1994 by Jakob Nielsen. Thirty years later, they still explain most of the UX problems you'll find. Treat them as a checklist you run against every screen.

1. Visibility of system status

Always keep the user informed about what's happening. Loading? Show it. Saved? Confirm it. Processing? Tell them for how long.

mBoB: spinner + "Processing payment…" after tapping Pay, then a green ✓ confirmation with transaction ID.

2. Match between system and real world

Use words and concepts your users know — not internal jargon or developer terms.

"Send money" beats "Initiate transfer request". "Biller" beats "Merchant ID 4814".

3. User control & freedom

Everyone makes mistakes. Let them undo, cancel, or go back. Every dead-end flow is a UX failure.

A clear "Cancel" on the payment confirm screen. An "Undo" toast after deleting a favourite.

4. Consistency & standards

Same thing, same word, same place — across the whole app. If the back arrow is top-left on one screen, it should be top-left on every screen.

Primary action always blue. Destructive action always red. Nav always in the same spot.

5. Error prevention

Better than a good error message: an error that can't happen in the first place. Design the impossible to be impossible.

Disable the "Pay" button until the amount is valid. Don't allow selecting a deactivated biller.

6. Recognition over recall

Show users their options. Don't make them remember things from earlier screens or elsewhere.

mBoB Favourites let users recognise the biller they want; they don't have to recall a 12-digit account number.

7. Flexibility & efficiency of use

Provide shortcuts for experts; keep simple paths for beginners. Both kinds of users exist in the same app.

Aum Pema navigates via the menu. Tashi uses Quick Scan & Pay. Both get what they need.

8. Aesthetic & minimalist design

Every extra element competes for attention and slows comprehension. Cut what isn't essential.

The 9-tile home screen + "Others" — not 30 tiles flat with everything always visible.

9. Help users recognise, diagnose, and recover from errors

Error messages in plain language. Explain the problem. Suggest a fix.

❌ "Error 401" → ✅ "MPIN incorrect. You have 2 tries left before your account is locked. Forgot MPIN? →"

10. Help & documentation

Ideally users shouldn't need help — but when they do, it should be easy to find, searchable, and focused on their task.

FAQ in side menu, 1095 call icon on login screen, contextual tooltips for new features.

Using the heuristics in practice

Run through all 10 against any screen. It takes 60 seconds once you know them. You'll catch 80% of UX issues before they reach a user.

Part 4

Laws of UX

These are psychology-grounded principles that predict how humans will behave. If you design against them, you'll fight your users. If you design with them, everything feels easy.

Fitts's Law (1954)

time ∝ log(distance / size)

The time to reach a target depends on how big it is and how far away. Bigger and closer = faster.

Apply: 44×44px minimum tap target. Primary action big, centred or bottom. Destructive actions small, separated.

Hick's Law (1952)

time = a + b · log₂(n + 1)

Decision time grows with the number of choices. Every extra option makes the user slower AND less confident.

Apply: If a screen has more than 7 primary actions, group them. Use "Others" as an overflow.

Jakob's Law (2000)

Users spend most of their time on other apps. They expect yours to behave like the ones they already know. Don't invent new patterns without a very strong reason.

Apply: Standard hamburger menus, back arrows, notification icons, pull-to-refresh, swipe-to-delete. Save originality for branding.

Miller's Law (1956) — 7 ± 2

Short-term memory holds around 7 items. Don't ask users to carry more than that between screens.

Apply: Chunk content into small groups. Summarise on confirmation screens so users don't have to remember.

Proximity Principle (Gestalt)

Things that are close together look related. Things far apart look unrelated. Whitespace is how you communicate structure.

Apply: Label next to its input. Group related fields. Separate unrelated groups with whitespace, not borders.

Doherty Threshold — 400 ms

If a system responds within 400 ms, users stay engaged. Past that, attention drifts. Past 3 seconds, most people give up.

Apply: <400 ms = feels instant. <1 s = show spinner. >3 s = show progress bar + "this might take a while".

Peak–End Rule

People judge an experience by its most intense moment and its final moment — not by the average. A rough middle is forgiven if the end feels good.

Apply: Make the confirmation screen satisfying. Celebrate completed flows. Nail the last mile.

Tesler's Law (of conservation of complexity)

Every system has inherent complexity. The question is who absorbs it — the user, or the designer/developer. Good products absorb the complexity so the user doesn't have to.

Apply: If something feels awkward for a user, there's probably a smarter default you could implement behind the scenes.

Part 5

Mobile-first thinking & thumb zones

Most Bhutanese users live on their phone. Many hold the phone in one hand while doing something else. Design for that reality, not for a desktop developer pointing a mouse.

Thumb zones — where the thumb reaches

  • Easy zone (bottom 45% of screen) — the thumb reaches here without stretching. Put primary actions (Pay, Send, Continue) here.
  • OK zone (middle) — reachable with a small stretch. Good for content, lists, secondary actions.
  • Hard zone (top 30%) — requires repositioning the phone or using two hands. Use only for infrequent actions — back button, close, status indicators.

Mobile do's & don'ts

❌ Don't
  • Make tap targets smaller than 44×44px
  • Rely on hover (there is no hover on touch)
  • Put the close button in a tiny corner
  • Use full-screen modals with no visible escape
  • Let the keyboard cover the submit button
  • Use body text smaller than 14px (prefer 16px)
  • Require two hands for the primary flow
✅ Do
  • Big, spaced tap targets
  • Discoverable tap, long-press, swipe gestures
  • Bottom sheets instead of centred modals
  • Keep the primary CTA above the keyboard
  • Use system fonts and readable sizes
  • One-handed as default; two-handed as power option
  • Test on a budget Android as well as iPhone
Part 6

Accessibility — POUR

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are built around four principles. Every product you ever ship — SELISE or otherwise — will be measured against them.

P · Perceivable

Users can see or hear the content. Alt text on images. Captions on video. Don't hide information behind colour alone.

O · Operable

Users can navigate with keyboard, screen reader, finger, or voice. No mouse-only traps. Focus indicators visible.

U · Understandable

Plain language. Predictable behaviour. Errors explain themselves. Forms clearly label what's required and why.

R · Robust

Works with assistive tech today and in 5 years. Use standard HTML elements — not custom div soup.

The quiet truth

Accessibility isn't about rare edge cases. It's about the tired user on a bright bus, the grandmother with reading glasses, the outdoor shopkeeper squinting in sun. Designing for accessibility makes the product better for everyone.

Part 7

Colour contrast

Of all accessibility rules, this is the one you cannot skip. WCAG AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text and UI components.

8.6:1 ✓
SELISE blue on white
15.2:1 ✓
Body text on bg
1.1:1 ❌
white on yellow
1.6:1 ❌
faint on border

Tools: WebAIM Contrast Checker, Chrome DevTools (right-click any element → Inspect → colour picker shows contrast ratio), Figma plugins like "Stark" or "Contrast".

Part 8

Bhutan-specific considerations

Global UX guidelines are written mostly by people in California and Europe. Our users are different. Here's what matters for Bhutanese products:

🔤 Dzongkha rendering

Dzongkha characters are taller than Latin. Always test with real Dzongkha text — not just Lorem Ipsum — or buttons overflow and labels wrap awkwardly.

📶 Variable connectivity

3G in rural areas. 4G in Thimphu. Unstable Wi-Fi everywhere. Design offline-first where possible. Show cached state clearly ("last updated 2 hours ago").

👴 Wide age range

Users span 15 to 75. The default font size should work for someone over 60. Provide a way to increase font size in settings.

☀️ Outdoor use

Shopkeepers, drivers, workers use apps in direct sunlight. High contrast and bold colours beat subtle greys. Avoid low-contrast dark modes as a default.

🗣️ Second-language English

Many users think in Dzongkha / Nepali / Sharchop but read in English. Use short sentences, common words, avoid idioms and bank jargon.

💰 Lower-end devices

Not everyone has a flagship phone. Test on a Nu. 10,000 Android. If it works there, it works everywhere. Don't assume 60fps animations.

Part 9

The UX process — Double Diamond

The British Design Council's Double Diamond describes how designers actually work. Four phases across two diamonds:

  1. Discover (diverge) — explore the problem space. Interview users. Observe behaviour. Gather evidence.
  2. Define (converge) — narrow down to the real problem worth solving. Write a clear problem statement.
  3. Develop (diverge) — explore many possible solutions. Sketch, wireframe, prototype.
  4. Deliver (converge) — pick one, refine, test, build, ship.

The most common mistake

Skipping the first diamond and jumping straight into solutions. You end up with an elegantly-designed answer to the wrong question.

Part 10

Fidelity ladder — don't start in Figma

Each step up the fidelity ladder costs more than the one before. Explore cheap; commit expensive.

  1. Sketch — pen on paper. Seconds per idea. Explore 20 ideas in 20 minutes.
  2. Wireframe — grey boxes, no colour. Minutes per screen. Test structure without worrying about style.
  3. Hi-fi mockup — real colours, real type, real content. Hours per screen. Only for the ideas that survived the wireframe stage.
  4. Prototype — clickable, navigable, in Figma or similar. Hours to days. Lets you test flows, not just screens.
  5. Real code — days to weeks. The most expensive fidelity. Commit here only after the prototype has survived user testing.

The principle

Test the idea at the cheapest level that answers your question. If the question is "is the flow right?", a paper sketch can answer that. Don't pixel-push a flow that might be wrong.

Part 11 · Applied

mBoB teardown

Let's apply everything to an app you already use.

What mBoB gets right

  • ✅ Big service tiles on home — Fitts's Law in action. Easy tap targets, even with wet fingers.
  • ✅ 9 tiles + "Others" pattern — Hick's Law. Not 30 options flat.
  • ✅ Hamburger top-left, notifications top-right — Jakob's Law. Matches every other app.
  • ✅ Balance hidden by default with eye-toggle — respects privacy in public (shopkeeper in a shared space).
  • ✅ Quick Send chips with avatars — recognition over recall. Heuristic #6.
  • ✅ Dzongkha / English toggle on pre-login — inclusive at the very first screen.
  • ✅ 1095 call icon on login screen — help is findable, heuristic #10.

What could be better

  • ⚠️ Service tile labels wrap — "Cardless Withdrawal" on two lines looks untidy. Either rephrase or grow the tile.
  • ⚠️ Favourites exist for Quick Send, not Bill Payments — same problem, better solution not extended.
  • ⚠️ Side nav mixes frequent and rare — "Biometric" sits next to "Loan Interest Rate". Group by use-frequency.
  • ⚠️ No clear system status on slow operations — did my payment go through? Still processing? Heuristic #1 gap.
  • ⚠️ Error messages sometimes use codes — "Error 401" breaks heuristic #9. Should say "MPIN incorrect".
  • ⚠️ Secondary text contrast — some greys are borderline for outdoor use.

This is the kind of review any UX-literate PM, developer, or tester can do in 15 minutes. You now have that skill.

Part 12

Your 5-question toolkit

Ask every screen you build or review

  1. Is the primary action obvious and big enough to tap?
  2. If something went wrong, can I tell what happened and undo it?
  3. Does this screen follow the same patterns as the rest of the app?
  4. Can someone with poor eyesight or a slow network still use it?
  5. Would Aum Pema, Tashi, and Karma (from Class 09) all succeed here?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure" — you've found UX work to do.

Key takeaways

  • UX isn't just a designer's job — every role makes UX decisions.
  • Nielsen's 10 heuristics explain 80% of UX problems.
  • Laws of UX — Fitts, Hick, Jakob, Miller, Proximity, Doherty, Peak-End, Tesler — predict user behaviour.
  • Design for the thumb, not the pointer. One-handed is the default.
  • Accessibility = POUR. Start with colour contrast.
  • Bhutan specifics: Dzongkha rendering, variable connectivity, outdoor use, wide age range.
  • Work the Double Diamond; test at the cheapest fidelity that answers your question.
  • A well-designed screen is one where the user never notices the design.
