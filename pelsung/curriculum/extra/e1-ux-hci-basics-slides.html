02 Hook
🚪 The Door Test

You walk up to a door. You push it.
It doesn't move. You pull it. Still nothing.
Then someone shows you it slides.

Was that your fault? Or the door's?

Don Norman, the father of UX, calls these "Norman Doors" — doors where the design doesn't tell you how to use them. Bad UX exists everywhere — not just on screens.

03 UX is everywhere

You already judge UX — you just don't call it that

🚰
Good: water tap

Turn right = more water. Universal. No instruction manual needed.

📺
Bad: TV remote

52 buttons. You use 5. The rest just make you nervous about pressing the wrong one.

🚗
Good: car steering

Turn left, car goes left. Worldwide. Even children understand it.

🏧
Bad: some ATMs

"Insert card" — which way? The slot doesn't tell you. You guess. You get it wrong.

🔌
Good: USB-C

Goes in both ways. Finally. After decades of "flip and try again".

🏥
Bad: hospital signs

"Radiology Dept, Floor G3, Wing B, Section 4". You just want to know: go left or right?

If you've ever been frustrated by a product, you understand UX. Now let's give you the vocabulary to explain why.

04 Why this matters for YOU

This isn't just for designers

If you're a BA / PM

Your wireframes and specs describe UI. UX vocabulary lets you explain why — not just what.

Example: "Put 'Pay' at the bottom per Fitts's Law" is clearer than "Put the button somewhere easy".

If you're a Dev

You make micro-UX decisions every hour — button size, error message wording, loading state, keyboard behaviour.

Example: AI generates an error saying "null pointer exception". You know to change it to "Something went wrong — try again".

If you're a QA

Most "bugs" users report are actually UX bugs. Knowing the rules helps you spot and name them.

Example: "Heuristic #1 violated — no loading indicator on funds transfer" is a much better bug report.

If you're a Designer

This is your foundation. Your vertical will go much deeper — but everyone needs this baseline so they speak your language.

AI can generate a UI in seconds. Only a human who knows UX can tell if it's actually good.

05 What is UX? What is HCI?

Three related ideas, often confused

UI · User Interface

The visible surface — buttons, colours, layout, typography. UI is what you see.

Think of it as the dashboard of a car.

UX · User Experience

How it feels to use a product — easy, frustrating, delightful, confusing. Design, speed, reliability all contribute.

Think of it as the full driving experience.

HCI · Human–Computer Interaction

The academic field studying how humans and machines communicate. HCI gives us principles.

Think of it as the physics of driving.

A restaurant with a beautiful dining room (great UI) but terrible service and cold food = bad UX. A street food cart with no decor but amazing food served fast = great UX, minimal UI.

Part One

Nielsen's
10 Heuristics

Published in 1994. Still explain 80% of UX problems you'll ever find.

01
07 Heuristic #1

1. Visibility of system status

Always tell the user what's happening. Right now. No surprises.

GOOD
WhatsApp message ticks

One grey tick = sent. Two grey ticks = delivered. Two blue ticks = read. You always know the status — without asking anyone.

BAD
mBoB payment processing

You tap "Pay". Nothing happens for 3 seconds. Did it work? Did it fail? Should you tap again? Now you're scared you'll pay twice.

GOOD
YouTube video loading bar

Grey bar = buffered. Red bar = watched. You see exactly where you are and how much remains.

REAL-WORLD
Elevator floor indicator

The number changes as the elevator moves. Without it, you'd anxiously wonder "is this thing even moving?"

08 Heuristics #2 & #3

2. Match the real world

Use words your users know. Not developer words. Not bank words.

❌ "Initiate fund transfer"
✅ "Send money"
❌ "Authentication failed"
✅ "Wrong password"
❌ "Insufficient balance"
✅ "Not enough money"

Real world: a grocery store sign says "Fruits" — not "Perishable produce category A".

3. User control & freedom

Everyone makes mistakes. Let them undo, cancel, go back. No dead ends.

✅ Gmail: "Undo Send" for 10 seconds
❌ "Are you sure?" with no undo after
✅ WhatsApp: "Delete for everyone"
❌ mBoB: accidentally paid wrong person, no reversal

Real world: every document has Ctrl+Z. Imagine a world where it didn't exist.

09 Heuristics #4 & #5

4. Consistency & standards

Same thing, same word, same place — everywhere in the app.

  • If "back" is an arrow on one screen, it's an arrow on every screen
  • If blue means "primary action", blue ALWAYS means primary action
  • If you say "beneficiary" on one screen, don't say "recipient" on another

Facebook example: the blue "Like" button is always in the same spot. What if it moved randomly? You'd hate it.

5. Error prevention

Better than a good error message: make the error impossible.

  • mBoB: "Pay" button is disabled until amount is entered — you literally can't pay Nu. 0
  • Google: "Did you mean…?" — catches typos before you search
  • ATM: dispenses cash AFTER returning your card — so you can't forget it
  • Date picker: prevents impossible dates like Feb 30

Real world: plugs that only go in one way. You can't get electrocuted because the design prevents it.

10 Heuristics #6 & #7

6. Recognition over recall

Show options. Don't make users remember things from other screens.

  • mBoB Quick Send: shows faces + names of recent contacts — tap, don't type
  • Google search: autocomplete shows your past searches
  • Netflix: "Continue watching" — don't make users remember where they stopped

Real world: a restaurant menu with photos. Easier to pick than a text-only list.

7. Flexibility & efficiency

Shortcuts for experts. Simple paths for beginners. Serve both.

  • mBoB: Aum Pema taps through the menu. Tashi uses Quick Scan & Pay on the home screen.
  • WhatsApp: tap mic to record. Hold mic to send instantly. Both work.
  • Google Maps: tap "Directions" OR type the destination straight into search.

Real world: a kitchen has a dishwasher AND a sink. Expert cook uses both. Beginner uses the sink.

11 Heuristics #8, #9 & #10

8. Minimalist design

Every extra element competes for attention.

Google's homepage: one search box. That's it. The most-visited site on earth.

mBoB: 9 tiles + "Others". Not 30 tiles. Smart.

9. Help with errors

Error messages: plain language, explain problem, suggest fix.

❌ "Error 401"
✅ "Wrong MPIN. 2 tries left. Forgot MPIN? →"

Real world: GPS says "Turn around when possible" — not "Error: wrong direction".

10. Help & docs

Users shouldn't need help — but when they do, make it findable.

mBoB: 1095 call icon on login. FAQ in side nav. Both are one tap away.

WhatsApp: "Settings → Help → Contact us" — 3 taps, always there.

That's all 10. Run them against any screen in 60 seconds. You'll catch 80% of problems.

Part Two

Laws of UX

Principles from psychology that predict how humans will behave.

02
14 Fitts's Law

Bigger & closer = faster to tap

Fitts's Law (1954)

The time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. Bigger and closer = faster and fewer errors.

time ∝ log(distance / size)
APPS
mBoB's 9-tile grid

Large tiles, spaced evenly. Even someone with shaky hands can tap "Funds Transfer" without hitting "Recharge" next to it.

REAL WORLD
Elevator buttons

Big physical buttons, well spaced. Imagine if elevator buttons were the size of rice grains. You'd miss every time.

Minimum: Apple & Google both require 44×44px tap targets. Anything smaller = users will miss.

15 Fitts in practice

Where Fitts goes right — and wrong

❌ Breaking Fitts
  • "Confirm" and "Cancel" are the same size, right next to each other → people send Nu. 50,000 to the wrong person
  • Tiny 24px "X" close button in the top-right corner of a popup
  • The "Submit" button is far from the form you just filled — you scroll past it
  • Two tiny radio buttons labelled "Yes" and "No" with no padding
✅ Applying Fitts
  • Primary action (Pay, Send) is large and centred at the bottom — thumb reaches easily
  • Destructive action (Delete, Cancel) is smaller, separated, requires a confirm step
  • WhatsApp: the send button is big, round, always in the same corner — you tap it 100x/day without thinking
  • Google search: one giant text box in the middle of the screen. Can't miss it.
16 Hick's Law

More choices = slower decision

Hick's Law (1952)

Decision time increases with the number of options. More buttons = users get slower AND less confident.

time = a + b · log₂(n + 1)
PROBLEM
Restaurant with 200-item menu

You spend 15 minutes reading. You still don't know what to pick. You end up anxious. Finally you ask the waiter "what do you recommend?"

SOLUTION
Restaurant with 15 items + "Chef's picks"

You pick in 2 minutes. You feel confident. This is Hick's Law in action — fewer choices, faster decisions, happier users.

mBoB

Home screen: 9 service tiles + "Others". Not 30 flat. Power users know to tap "Others". Beginners see exactly what they need.

Netflix

"Top 10 in Bhutan" and "Because you watched…" — curated rows, not the full 10,000-movie library dumped on you.

17 Jakob's Law

Users expect your app to behave like other apps

Jakob's Law (2000)

Users spend most of their time on other apps. They bring those expectations to yours. Don't invent new patterns.

📱
Back arrow = top-left

Because WhatsApp, Facebook, YouTube, mBoB, Gmail — ALL put it there.

🔔
Bell = notifications

Because every app since 2010 has trained your brain to look for the bell.

🍔
Three lines = menu

Hamburger menu. Learned. Universal. Don't replace it with something "creative".

👇
Pull to refresh

Instagram, Twitter, Gmail — trained this gesture into muscle memory.

👈
Swipe left to delete

Gmail, WhatsApp, Apple Mail all do this. Your app should too.

⚠️
Don't invent gestures

Making "pay" require a three-finger swipe? Your users will never discover it.

Save originality for your brand. Not for core interactions.

18 Miller's Law & Proximity

How memory and grouping work

Miller's Law — 7 ± 2

Short-term memory holds ~7 items. Don't make users carry more than that between screens.

Phone numbers: 17 555 123 456 is chunked into groups. You can remember it. 17555123456 as one block? Much harder.

mBoB masks account as 101XXXX09 — you remember the ending, not the whole number.

Proximity principle (Gestalt)

Things close together look related. Things far apart look separate. Whitespace IS meaning.

mBoB: "Favourites" and "Quick Send" are separate sections with a visible gap between them. You instantly know they're different things.

In a form: first name and last name should be grouped. Phone and email can be separate — different topic.

19 Doherty & Peak–End

Speed perception & memory bias

Doherty Threshold — 400 ms

If a system responds within 400 ms, users feel it's instant. Past that, they notice. Past 3 seconds, they leave.

  • < 400 ms → feels instant. No feedback needed.
  • 400 ms–1 s → show a subtle spinner.
  • 1–3 s → show a progress message.
  • > 3 s → show a progress BAR + "This might take a moment".

YouTube learned this: even a tiny perceived speed increase keeps people watching 20% longer.

Peak–End Rule

People judge an experience by its peak moment (best or worst) and its ending — not the average.

  • Duolingo: the "You're on fire! 🔥" streak celebration is the peak. Makes the whole session feel fun.
  • Amazon: the "Your order is on its way" confirmation with tracking — satisfying ending to a checkout.
  • mBoB payment: if the last screen says "Transaction successful ✓ Ref: TXN001234" with a clear green checkmark — the whole experience feels trustworthy.

A frustrating middle is forgiven if the end feels great. Nail the last step.

20 Tesler's Law

Every system has complexity. Who absorbs it?

Tesler's Law — Conservation of Complexity

Complexity can't be destroyed — only moved. Either the user deals with it, or the developer/designer absorbs it.

USER ABSORBS IT
Booking a flight on some airline sites

"Choose departure, return, class, meal, seat, baggage, insurance, loyalty number…" — 9 screens. The developer was lazy. The user suffers.

SYSTEM ABSORBS IT
Google Flights

Type "Paro to Bangkok". Google figures out airlines, stops, prices, dates. One screen. Complexity is hidden behind smart defaults.

mBoB example: Quick Send remembers your last 4 recipients and shows them as avatar chips. The user taps one face instead of re-entering a 10-digit number. The app absorbed the complexity of "look up contact + enter number".

03
22 Thumb Zones

Where the thumb can comfortably reach

75% of phone interactions are one-handed. Design for the thumb, not the pointer.

⚠ HARD
OK
✓ EASY
  • Easy (bottom 45%): primary actions — Pay, Send, Continue
  • OK (middle): content, lists, secondary actions
  • Hard (top): infrequent actions — back, close, settings

mBoB: notification bell & logout = top-right (rarely tapped). Service tiles = bottom half (tapped daily). Not an accident.

23 Apps that get mobile right

Study these — they've solved mobile UX

💬
WhatsApp

Bottom tab bar. Type area at bottom. Send button on right — all thumb-friendly. Used by 2 billion people who never read a manual.

📸
Instagram

All primary actions (home, search, post, reels, profile) in a bottom navigation bar. You never reach for the top to do anything important.

🗺️
Google Maps

Search bar top. Everything else bottom. The "Start" button for navigation is a huge green pill at the bottom — can't miss it.

Pattern: the most popular apps in the world all put the most important actions at the bottom. If they do it, you should too.

24 Mobile Do's & Don'ts

Hard-won rules for small screens

❌ Don't
  • Tap targets under 44×44px
  • Hover-only interactions (no hover on touch screens)
  • Tiny close buttons in the corner (Fitts violation)
  • Full-screen popups with no visible escape
  • Keyboard covering the submit button
  • Body text under 16px (14px absolute minimum)
  • Require two hands for the primary flow
  • Heavy animations on budget phones
✅ Do
  • Big touch targets with spacing between them
  • Tap & long-press as discoverable patterns
  • Bottom sheets instead of centred modals
  • Keep primary CTA visible above the keyboard
  • System fonts, readable sizes
  • One-handed default, two-handed for power users
  • Test on a Nu. 10,000 Android — not just iPhone
  • Offline states: show cached data, mark staleness

Part Four

Accessibility

Designing so everyone can use what you build — the tired user on a bright bus, the grandmother with reading glasses, the outdoor shopkeeper.

04
26 The Core Four — POUR

WCAG's four principles

P · Perceivable

Users can see/hear the content. Alt text on images. Captions on video. Enough colour contrast.

Example: don't use ONLY colour to show an error. Add a ❌ icon + text too. Colour-blind users need it.

O · Operable

Users can navigate with keyboard, screen reader, or finger. No mouse-only traps.

Example: every button should be reachable with Tab + Enter. Test: unplug your mouse and try to use the app.

U · Understandable

Language is clear. Errors explain themselves. Behaviour is predictable.

Example: if clicking "Save" sometimes saves and sometimes deletes, that's unpredictable. People stop trusting your app.

R · Robust

Works with assistive tech today and in 5 years. Use standard HTML, not custom div soup.

Example: use <button> for buttons, not <div onclick>. Screen readers need real HTML elements.

27 Colour Contrast

Contrast ratio — the one rule you cannot skip

WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for normal text · 3:1 for large text & UI components.

8.6:1 ✓
SELISE Blue on white
15.2:1 ✓
Body text on bg
1.1:1 ❌
white on yellow
1.6:1 ❌
faint on border

Think about shopkeepers in Thimphu using mBoB in direct sunlight. Low contrast = invisible text. Check with WebAIM Contrast Checker or Chrome DevTools.

28 Bhutan-specific considerations

Designing for our users

🔤 Dzongkha rendering

Characters are taller than Latin. Test with real Dzongkha text — "བཀྲ་ཤིས་བདེ་ལེགས" overflows a button designed for "Hello".

📶 Variable connectivity

3G in Trashigang, 4G in Thimphu, no signal between. Show cached state: "last updated 2 hrs ago".

👴 Age range 15–75

Default font size must work for 60+. mBoB's big tiles help Aum Pema. But side-nav text is still too small.

☀️ Outdoor / sunlight use

Shopkeepers, taxi drivers, farmers — all outdoors. Bold colours & high contrast beat subtle greys.

🗣️ Second-language English

Users think in Dzongkha/Nepali/Sharchop but read in English. Short sentences. Common words. No bank jargon.

💰 Budget devices

Test on a Nu. 10,000 Android. Heavy animations and 5MB images will freeze it. If it works there, it works everywhere.

30 Double Diamond

Diverge, converge, diverge, converge

First diamond: explore many problems, pick the real one. Second diamond: explore many solutions, pick the one that fits.

Most teams skip the first diamond. They jump straight to "build it". That's how you end up with a beautiful answer to the wrong question.

06
33 mBoB Login Screen

Before you even log in

✅ 4-digit MPIN

Not a 12-character password. Fitts: big keypad buttons. Hick: only 10 digits to choose from. Fast.

✅ Fingerprint option

Even faster. Zero recall (#6). Zero typing. The system absorbs the complexity (Tesler).

✅ Dzongkha / English toggle

Available BEFORE login — not buried in settings. Inclusive from step zero.

✅ 1095 call icon

Help is one tap away — even before login. Heuristic #10. Critical for locked-out users.

⚠️ Quick Scan & Pay on login

Great for speed. But some users don't know what a QR code is — Aum Pema might accidentally tap it. Error prevention (#5)?

⚠️ "Register" vs "Sign up" link

Some apps use "Register", some "Sign up". Jakob's Law says pick one and be consistent with what Bhutanese users know.

34 mBoB Home Screen

After login — the main dashboard

✅ Account card with masked balance

Eye-toggle to show/hide. Privacy in public (shopkeeper in a crowd). Miller's Law: only shows last 2 digits of account.

✅ Quick Send Money chips

Faces + names of frequent contacts. Recognition (#6), not recall. Tesler: system remembers contacts for you.

✅ 9-tile Services grid

Fitts: big tiles, spaced well. Hick: not 30 options. Chunked into a scannable grid with "Others" for the rest.

⚠️ "Entertainmen t" wrapping

The word "Entertainment" doesn't fit in the tile and wraps mid-word. Test with long labels before shipping!

⚠️ Two different home layouts

Some users see Accounts/Recharge/Scan&Pay tiles, others see Open Account/Manage Beneficiary. Consistency (#4)?

⚠️ No Bill Payment favourites

Quick Send has favourites. Bill Payments doesn't. Same problem, inconsistent solution.

35 mBoB Side Navigation

The hamburger menu

What works

  • Hamburger icon top-left — Jakob's Law, standard position
  • Profile photo with camera icon — clear affordance for "change photo"
  • Icons next to every item — recognition over recall
  • Logout at the very bottom — conventional & safe (rarely tapped accidentally)

What could improve

  • Hick's Law: 10 menu items mixing daily use (Biometric) with rare use (Loan Interest Rate, EMI Calculator)
  • Group by frequency: "Security" (Biometric, M-PIN, T-PIN) vs "Info" (Rates, EMI, FAQ) vs "Settings" (Category, Audio, Logout)
  • Change M-Pin / Change T-Pin / Forgot T-Pin are 3 separate items — could be one "PIN Settings" screen
  • "Audio Notification" — unclear what this does without tapping it

This side nav serves both Tashi (power user, knows what he wants) and Aum Pema (scans everything, gets overwhelmed). Group by purpose to serve both.

36 Your personal toolkit

The 5 questions to ask every screen

  1. Is the primary action obvious and big enough to tap? (Fitts)
  2. If something went wrong, can I tell what happened & undo it? (Heuristic #1, #3, #9)
  3. Does this screen follow the same patterns as the rest of the app? (Jakob, #4)
  4. Can someone with poor eyesight or slow network still use it? (POUR, contrast)
  5. Would Aum Pema, Tashi, and Karma all succeed here? (personas)

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure" — you've found UX work to do. You now have the language to explain why.
