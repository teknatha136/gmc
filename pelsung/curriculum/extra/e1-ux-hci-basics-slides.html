02 Why this matters

This isn't just for designers

If you're a BA / PM

Your wireframes and specs describe UI. UX vocabulary lets you explain why — not just what.

If you're a Dev

You make micro-UX decisions every hour (button size, error message, loading state). AI won't always get them right.

If you're a QA

Most "bugs" filed by users are UX bugs. Knowing the rules helps you spot them earlier and write clearer reports.

If you're a Designer

This is your foundation. Your vertical will go much deeper — but everyone needs this baseline.

AI can generate a UI in seconds. Only a human who knows UX can tell if it's actually good.

03 What is UX? What is HCI?

Two related ideas, often confused

UX · User Experience

How it feels to use a product — easy, frustrating, delightful, confusing. UX is an outcome. Design, copy, speed, reliability all contribute.

HCI · Human–Computer Interaction

The academic field that studies how humans and machines communicate. HCI gives us the principles. UX applies them.

UI (User Interface) is the visible surface — buttons, colours, layout. UX is the full experience. Great UI can still have bad UX if the flow is broken.

Part One

Nielsen's
10 Heuristics

The 30-year-old rules that still explain 80% of UX problems.

01
05 Heuristics 1–5

The first five

1. Visibility of system status

Keep users informed. Show progress, confirmations, what's happening.

mBoB: spinner + "Processing payment…" toast after tap.
2. Match the real world

Use words and concepts users know — not developer jargon.

"Send money" beats "Initiate transfer request".
3. User control & freedom

Let people undo, cancel, go back. Everyone makes mistakes.

Clear "Cancel" button on payment confirm screen.
4. Consistency & standards

Same thing, same word, same place — across the whole app.

Back arrow always top-left. Primary action always blue.
5. Error prevention

Better than error messages: stop the error from being possible.

Disable "Pay" until amount is valid. Don't let user tap an expired biller.

06 Heuristics 6–10

The second five

6. Recognition over recall

Show options. Don't make users remember things from elsewhere.

mBoB "Favourites" lets users recognise billers, not recall account numbers.
7. Flexibility & efficiency

Shortcuts for power users; simple paths for beginners. Both matter.

Aum Pema uses the menu. Tashi uses Quick Scan & Pay.
8. Aesthetic & minimalist design

Every extra element competes for attention. Cut what isn't essential.

Don't show 20 options on home. Show 9 tiles + "Others".
9. Help users recover from errors

Error messages: plain language, explain the problem, suggest a fix.

❌ "Error 401" · ✅ "MPIN incorrect. 2 tries left before lockout."
10. Help & documentation

Users shouldn't need help — but when they do, it should be findable and searchable.

FAQ, call 1095, in-app help bubbles on new features.
💡 Use these as a checklist

Every screen review: run through all 10. You'll spot the problems in 60 seconds.

Part Two

Laws of UX

Principles from psychology that predict how humans will behave.

02
08 Fitts's Law

Bigger & closer = faster to tap

Fitts's Law (1954)

The time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. The bigger and closer it is, the faster users hit it.

time ∝ log(distance / size)

❌ Breaking Fitts

  • Tiny 24px buttons on mobile
  • Primary action in a corner far from the thumb
  • Critical "Confirm" right next to "Cancel" — easy mis-tap

✅ Applying Fitts

  • 44×44px minimum tap target (Apple/Google both say so)
  • Primary action big, centred or bottom — easy to reach
  • Destructive actions small, separated, require confirm

mBoB's 9-tile services grid? Fitts's Law in action.

09 Hick's Law

More choices = slower decision

Hick's Law (1952)

The time to make a decision grows with the number of choices. Add more buttons — users get slower AND less confident.

time = a + b · log₂(n + 1)

❌ Choice overload

30 services tiles on the home screen. User stands there reading each one. Many give up.

✅ Chunked & grouped

9 tiles + "Others". Most-used on top. Rare ones one tap away. Fast for power users, scannable for beginners.

Rule of thumb: if a screen has more than 7 primary actions, group them. Users don't read — they scan.

10 Jakob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other apps

Jakob's Law (2000)

Users expect your app to behave like all the other apps they already know. Don't invent new patterns unless you have a very good reason.

  • 📱 Back arrow on top-left — because every other app does
  • 🔔 Notifications bell on top-right — because every other app does
  • 🍔 Hamburger menu for secondary nav — because every other app does
  • 🔍 Pull down to refresh, swipe to delete — because users already know these
  • ⚠️ Inventing a new gesture for "pay" = your users will be confused

Originality is for branding, not for core interactions.

11 More laws worth knowing

Four more, quick

Miller's Law — 7 ± 2

Short-term memory holds ~7 items. Don't make users hold more than that in their head between screens.

Proximity principle

Things that are close together look related. Things far apart look unrelated. Use whitespace to group meaning.

Doherty Threshold — 400 ms

If a system responds within 400 ms, users stay engaged. Past that, show a spinner. Past 3 seconds, show progress.

Peak–End Rule

People judge an experience by its peak moment and its end — not the average. Make the last step of a flow delightful.

13 Thumb Zones

Where the thumb can comfortably reach

One-handed phone use is the default. Design for the thumb, not the pointer.

⚠ HARD
OK
✓ EASY
  • Easy (bottom 45%): primary actions — Pay, Send, Continue
  • OK (middle): content, lists, secondary actions
  • Hard (top): infrequent actions — back, close, status

Look at mBoB: notification bell & logout are top-right (rarely tapped). Primary tiles are in the middle-bottom. This is deliberate.

14 Mobile Do's & Don'ts

Hard-won rules for small screens

❌ Don't
  • Tap targets under 44px
  • Hover-only interactions (no hover on touch)
  • Tiny close buttons in the corner
  • Full-screen popups with no escape
  • Keyboards covering the submit button
  • Text under 14px body / 16px preferred
  • Requiring two hands for primary flow
✅ Do
  • Big touch targets, spaced apart
  • Tap and long-press as discoverable patterns
  • Swipe gestures for secondary actions
  • Bottom sheets instead of centred modals
  • Keep primary CTA above the keyboard
  • System fonts, readable sizes
  • One-handed default, two-handed for power

Part Four

Accessibility

Designing so everyone can use what you build — not just the young, sighted, English-speaking few.

04
16 The Core Four

WCAG's four principles · POUR

P · Perceivable

Users can see/hear the content. Alt text on images. Captions on video. Sufficient colour contrast.

O · Operable

Users can navigate with keyboard, screen reader, or finger. No mouse-only traps.

U · Understandable

Language is clear. Errors explain themselves. Behaviour is predictable.

R · Robust

Works with assistive tech today and in 5 years. Use standard HTML; don't invent weird markup.

17 Colour Contrast

Contrast ratio — the one rule you cannot skip

WCAG AA: 4.5:1 for normal text · 3:1 for large text & UI components.

18 Bhutan-specific considerations

Designing for our users

🔤 Dzongkha rendering

Test your UI with long Dzongkha strings. Characters are taller than Latin — buttons overflow.

📶 Variable connectivity

Rural 3G, urban 4G, unstable Wi-Fi. Design for offline-first where possible. Show cached state clearly.

👴 Age range

Users span 15 to 75. Default font size should work for 60+. Let users increase it if they want.

☀️ Sunlight use

Shopkeepers use apps outdoors. High contrast & bold colours beat subtle greys.

🗣️ Second-language English

Many users think in Dzongkha/Nepali/Sharchop, read in English. Use short sentences, common words.

💰 Low-end phones

Not everyone has an iPhone. Test on a ₹10,000 Android. If it works there, it works everywhere.

23 What mBoB gets right

The wins

  • ✅ Big tiles on home — Fitts's Law. Easy tap targets.
  • ✅ 9 + Others pattern — Hick's Law. Not 30 options flat.
  • ✅ Hamburger top-left, notifications top-right — Jakob's Law. Standard.
  • ✅ Balance hidden by default with eye-toggle — privacy in public.
  • ✅ Quick Send chips with faces — recognition over recall. Heuristic #6.
  • ✅ Dzongkha/English toggle on pre-login — inclusive.
  • ✅ 1095 call icon on login screen — help is findable. Heuristic #10.
24 What could be better

The opportunities

  • ⚠️ Service tile text — "Cardless Withdrawal" wraps awkwardly. Fitts is about target size, but labels matter too.
  • ⚠️ Favourites on Quick Send only — what about bill payments? Same problem, better solution missing.
  • ⚠️ Side nav mixes frequent + rare — "Biometric" sits next to "Loan Interest Rate". Different use frequencies.
  • ⚠️ No system status on slow operations — is my payment still processing? Heuristic #1.
  • ⚠️ Error messages use codes in some flows. "Error 401" breaks heuristic #9.
  • ⚠️ Contrast on secondary text — some greys are borderline on bright outdoor light.

This is the kind of review any UX-literate PM, dev or QA can do in 15 minutes.

25 Your personal toolkit

The 5 questions to ask every screen

  1. Is the primary action obvious and big enough to tap?
  2. If something went wrong, can I tell what & undo it?
  3. Does this screen follow the same patterns as the rest of the app?
  4. Can someone with poor eyesight or slow network still use it?
  5. Would Aum Pema, Tashi, and Karma all succeed here?

If any answer is "no" or "I'm not sure" — you've found UX work to do.
