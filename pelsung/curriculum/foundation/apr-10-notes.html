Professional Skills

Communication, email etiquette, and time management — the invisible infrastructure of every effective professional.

10:15 – 10:45 · 30 min
💬 Communication Fundamentals

Communication is more than just sending a message. It's about making sure the right information reaches the right person in the right way — and that they've understood it. In professional settings, most misunderstandings, project delays, and conflicts come from communication that broke down somewhere along the way.

The Communication Model — Noise Can Enter at Any Step
🧑‍💻Sender
→
💭Message
→
📡Channel
→
🧑‍💼Receiver
→
🔁Feedback

Noise can be: unclear intent, wrong channel choice, a distracted receiver, or no confirmation of understanding.

Think of this model every time you send a message or give instructions. Ask yourself: Could this be misunderstood? Am I using the right channel? Did I confirm they understood? Most workplace miscommunication happens because someone skipped one of these steps.

🗣️ Verbal Communication

  • Speak at a pace others can follow — slow down when explaining something new
  • Use clear, simple language; avoid jargon when speaking to someone outside your field
  • Check in: "Does that make sense?"
  • Avoid filler words like "um", "like", "basically" — they undermine clarity
  • Volume and tone matter as much as the actual words

✍️ Written Communication

  • One idea per sentence — don't try to say everything at once
  • Lead with the most important information, then add context
  • Use bullet points for lists — easier to scan than run-on paragraphs
  • Always proofread before hitting send
  • Shorter is almost always better — if you can cut a sentence without losing meaning, cut it

👁️ Non-Verbal Cues

  • Eye contact signals engagement and confidence
  • Open posture (arms uncrossed, leaning forward) signals you're approachable
  • Nodding while listening = you're following along
  • Response time in messaging conveys urgency: a 3-day reply to an urgent Slack feels dismissive
  • Emojis in work chats: read the room — fine in casual team chats, avoid in formal emails
1

Be Clear, Not Just Correct

A technically accurate message that confuses the reader has failed. Always ask: "Would someone who knows nothing about this understand exactly what I need from them?" Correctness is about facts; clarity is about whether the message works.

2

Choose the Right Channel

Urgent & quick? → Slack or chat. Decision or record-keeping needed? → Email. Sensitive, nuanced, or emotional? → In-person or video call. One rule: don't send a 500-word Slack message. That's what email is for.

3

Active Listening is a Skill

Most people listen while already composing their reply. That's not listening — it's waiting. Listen to understand, not to respond. When someone finishes, summarize what you heard back to them ("So what you're saying is...") to confirm you actually got it.

🎯 Discussion question: Think of a time communication broke down — at school, at home, or at work. What went wrong? Was it a sender problem? Wrong channel? Distracted receiver? No feedback loop?

📧 Email Etiquette

Email is still the primary formal communication channel in most organizations. A well-written email gets a fast, useful response. A poorly written one gets ignored, misunderstood, or causes the person to ask three follow-up questions before they can act. The goal of every professional email is for the reader to know exactly what's needed from them in under 30 seconds.

📝 Subject Line Rules

  • Bad examples: "Hi" / "Question" / "Important!!!" / (blank)
  • Good pattern: [Action type] What it's about — deadline if relevant
  • Use action tags in brackets: [FYI] [Action needed] [Decision] [For your records]
  • Keep it under 60 characters — most email clients cut off anything longer
  • The subject line is the first thing they see. Make it count.

👥 To / CC / BCC — Use Them Correctly

  • To: People who must read and act or respond
  • CC: People who need to be aware but don't need to do anything
  • BCC: Use sparingly — for sensitive situations (e.g., removing someone from a chain gracefully)
  • Reply-All: only use it if everyone in the chain genuinely needs your reply
  • Don't CC your manager as a passive-aggressive power move — solve issues directly first

Here's what a well-structured professional email looks like. Notice it has a clear subject, a greeting, a single clear ask, a deadline, and a polite sign-off. It takes under 20 seconds to read and act on.

From:Karma Wangchuk <karma@selisegroup.com>
To:Bhuwan Sharma <bhuwan@selisegroup.com>
CC:Tek Nath Acharya <tek@selisegroup.com>
Subject:[Action needed] API documentation draft — feedback by Apr 14 CLEAR SUBJECT

Hi Bhuwan, GREETING


I've finished the first draft of the API documentation for the user auth module and would appreciate your review before we share it with the QA team.


What I need from you: CLEAR ASK

→ Review the endpoint descriptions for accuracy
→ Flag anything that's unclear or missing
→ Reply with comments by Tuesday, April 14 DEADLINE


The document is here: [link to doc]


Happy to jump on a quick call if you have questions.


Thanks,
Karma SIGN-OFF
Software Developer · SELISE Bhutan

⚠️ Common mistakes to avoid: Writing paragraph-long essays when a bullet list works. Using "per my last email" — it's passive-aggressive and damages relationships. Never send an email while you're upset — write the draft, walk away, re-read it in 30 minutes. And never CC a manager just to pressure someone into responding faster.

Email Rewrite Challenge

2

⏰ Time Management

Everyone starts every day with 24 hours. The difference between people who get things done and people who feel perpetually overwhelmed is not ability — it's knowing what to prioritize and protecting their time to do it. In a software team, your wasted time often means someone else's task is blocked. Time management is a team skill, not just a personal one.

Most wasted time isn't laziness. It's unclear priorities, digital distraction, and not knowing how to say no professionally. The Eisenhower Matrix is a simple tool that makes priorities visible.

The Eisenhower Matrix — Sort Every Task Into One of Four Quadrants
⚡ Urgent
🌙 Not Urgent
Important
🔥 Q1 · Do First Deadlines, client issues, bugs in production
📅 Q2 · Schedule Learning, planning, building skills, health
Not Important
🤝 Q3 · Delegate Interruptions, some meetings, admin tasks
🗑️ Q4 · Eliminate Doom-scrolling, unnecessary notifications, 12-reply chains

The most important quadrant is Q2 — things that are important but not urgent. Learning a new skill, planning your week, reviewing code carefully — none of these feel "on fire" today, but they're what separate good professionals from great ones. If you always live in Q1 (firefighting), it's usually because you're not spending enough time in Q2.

📦 Time Blocking

  • Reserve calendar blocks for deep, focused work
  • Group similar tasks — answer all emails at once instead of every 10 minutes
  • Protect your mornings for your hardest or most important work — energy is highest then
  • Don't let "quick meetings" fragment your focus blocks
  • Even a 90-minute uninterrupted block dramatically improves output quality

📵 Digital Discipline

  • Turn off non-critical notifications during deep work
  • Check email and Slack at set times — not constantly throughout the day
  • Use phone-free time for important or creative work
  • Every context switch (phone notification, colleague interruption) costs ~20 minutes of refocus time
  • The "one more scroll" trap is designed by billion-dollar companies — set app timers
🗣️ Discussion question: What's the biggest thing that wastes your time on a typical day? Is it Q3 (urgent but not important interruptions) or Q4 (things that shouldn't be happening at all)? What one change could free up an extra hour?

AI Basics & Prompting

What AI actually is, how tools like Claude and ChatGPT work, and how to write prompts that get you real results — not generic filler.

12:10 – 12:35 · 25 min
🤖 What is AI? (And What Isn't It?)

Artificial Intelligence is not a single technology. It's a broad category of software that can perform tasks that previously required human intelligence. The AI tools you'll use most in this program — ChatGPT and Claude — are a specific type called Large Language Models (LLMs). Understanding how they actually work will help you use them better and avoid common mistakes.

💬 Language AI (LLM)

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Trained on billions of documents — books, websites, code, articles. Reads and writes text. This is what we'll use throughout training.

🎨 Image AI

Midjourney, DALL·E, Stable Diffusion. Generates or edits images from text descriptions. Useful for design work.

🎵 Audio / Video AI

Whisper (transcription), Suno (music generation), Runway (video editing). Rapidly improving category.

🧠 The simplest way to understand an LLM: It's a very sophisticated autocomplete. Given everything you've written so far, it predicts the most likely next word — over and over — at massive scale. That's why it sounds so fluent, and also why it can confidently say wrong things.

🟢 What AI Is Good At

  • Drafting and rewriting text
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Explaining complex topics simply
  • Generating ideas and brainstorming
  • Writing and debugging code
  • Translating between languages

🔴 What AI Is Bad At

  • Knowing current events (it has a training cutoff)
  • Precise numbers and statistics — verify these
  • Tasks requiring lived experience or true judgment
  • Remembering past conversations (each session starts fresh)
  • Knowing what it doesn't know — it doesn't say "I'm not sure" often enough

⚠️ What AI Is Not

  • Not a search engine — it doesn't look things up in real time
  • Not always right — it can hallucinate facts confidently
  • Not a person — it has no feelings, no memory across sessions
  • Not a replacement for your thinking — it's a thinking partner

The two AI tools you'll use most in this program:

💚 ChatGPT (by OpenAI)

  • Made by OpenAI, founded 2015
  • Current flagship model: GPT-4o
  • Most widely known AI tool worldwide
  • Has web search capability (paid tier)
  • Strong at: general writing, coding, analysis, Q&A
  • Free tier available at chat.openai.com

🟤 Claude (by Anthropic)

  • Made by Anthropic, founded 2021
  • Current flagship: Claude Sonnet 4
  • Designed to be safer and more careful in responses
  • Very large context window — handles long documents
  • Excellent at nuanced writing, reasoning, and long-form tasks
  • Claude Code: specialized for software development — our primary tool in this program
💡 Live demo: Watch how the same question gets completely different responses when context changes. Ask Claude: "Explain what a software API is to a 10-year-old." Then: "Now explain it to a senior software engineer." Same question — different vocabulary, different depth, different examples. This is context-sensitivity in action.

✏️ The Art of Prompting

A prompt is the instruction you give to an AI. The quality of the output you get is almost entirely determined by the quality of the prompt you give. Prompting is a learnable skill — not magic, not luck. The same task given to the same AI can produce wildly different results depending on how you frame it.

A well-structured prompt has up to four parts. You don't always need all four, but the more context you give, the better the output.

Anatomy of a High-Quality Prompt
ContextI am a software developer at a company building a digital ID system for Bhutan.
TaskWrite a professional email to a client explaining a 2-week delay in the API delivery.
FormatUse a formal but empathetic tone. Keep it under 150 words. Include a subject line.
ExampleSubject line style: "[Project name] Update — [topic]"
Context — who you are and what situation you're in Task — exactly what you want the AI to produce Format — how you want it (length, tone, structure) Example — optional but powerful; shows the style you want
1

Give it a Role

"You are a senior UX designer reviewing a mobile app..." Role-setting dramatically shifts the quality and perspective of the response. The AI will adopt the knowledge, vocabulary, and priorities of that role. Try: "You are a patient teacher explaining to someone with no coding background."

2

Be Specific About the Output

Vague: "Write something about APIs." → You get a generic response. Specific: "Write a 3-paragraph explanation of REST APIs for someone who has never coded before, using real-world analogies like ordering food at a restaurant." → You get something useful.

3

Iterate — Don't Settle for the First Response

AI is a conversation, not a one-shot machine. If the first response isn't right, just say: "That's good, but make it shorter and less formal." Or: "Add a real-world example." Or: "Change the tone to be more encouraging." Keep refining in the same chat — the AI remembers the full conversation.

4

Give Examples When You Can (Few-Shot Prompting)

"Here's an example of the writing style I want: [paste example]. Now write something similar about [topic]." Showing the AI what you want is often more powerful than describing it. This is called few-shot prompting and it's one of the most reliable techniques.

Weak vs. Strong — See the Difference

✗ Weak Prompt
Write about time management.
✓ Strong Prompt
You are a productivity coach speaking to a group of 20-year-old tech trainees in Bhutan who have no prior corporate work experience. Write a 5-tip guide to time management that is practical, specific, and uses examples relevant to software teams. Keep each tip under 50 words.
✗ Weak Prompt
Fix my code.
✓ Strong Prompt
I have a JavaScript function that should filter an array of user objects by age. It's returning nothing. Here is the code: [paste code]. Identify the bug, explain why it's wrong, and give me the fixed version with a comment explaining what changed.
✗ Weak Prompt
Write an email.
✓ Strong Prompt
Write a 5-line professional email to a client explaining that the project is delayed by 1 week due to a technical blocker. Use an empathetic but confident tone. Start with the most important fact: the new delivery date.
✍ Exercise 2 · Pairs

The Prompt Battle

⏱ 30 minutes 👥 Pairs (~22 pairs) 📱 Phone or laptop with Claude or ChatGPT
1
Round 1 — Weak vs Strong (10 min): Each pair gets the same task. Person A writes a weak prompt. Person B writes a strong prompt using the Context → Task → Format → Example framework. Both submit to the AI. Compare the outputs side by side. Which one is actually useful?
2
Round 2 — Iterate (8 min): Take the weak prompt's output. Improve it using only follow-up messages — don't rewrite the original prompt. Use instructions like: "Make it shorter", "Use bullet points", "Give a real-world example", "Change the tone to be more formal". See how much you can improve it in 3 messages.
3
Share (8 min): 4 pairs share their best prompt + output on the projector. Class votes: which response was most useful and why?
4
Debrief (4 min): What made the strong prompts better? What surprised you about what the AI could do — or couldn't do?
Tasks to use (rotate between pairs)
A. Write an email to a client explaining that you need more information before you can proceed with their request.
B. Explain what a database is to someone who has never used a computer.
C. Create a 5-day study plan for learning HTML and CSS from scratch.
D. Write a job posting for a junior software tester at a tech company in Bhutan.

⚖️ AI Dos & Don'ts

AI is powerful, but power without judgment leads to problems. As professionals, you need to understand not just how to use AI effectively, but when to trust it, when not to, and what it's never appropriate to hand over to a machine.

✅ Do

  • Use AI to draft, then edit and own the output yourself
  • Verify facts AI gives you — especially numbers, dates, and statistics
  • Use AI to learn — ask it to explain, not just give you the answer
  • Treat it as a thinking partner, not a replacement for thinking
  • Tell your team when you've used AI to produce something
  • Use AI to explore options or approaches you wouldn't have thought of
  • Iterate and refine — a second or third prompt is often much better than the first

❌ Don't

  • Paste confidential client data, passwords, or personal IDs into public AI tools
  • Submit AI output as your own work without reading and reviewing it first
  • Blindly trust code AI writes — always read it and test it before deploying
  • Use AI for decisions that require human judgment (hiring, firing, medical, legal)
  • Assume AI knows current events — it has a training knowledge cutoff
  • Use AI to bypass learning something you actually need to understand

Hallucinations: AI tools can confidently state false information. This happens because LLMs predict what sounds right, not what is right. They don't have a truth-detection system. If AI tells you a fact you're going to use in a report, a proposal, or in conversation — verify it independently.

Privacy: Most public AI tools (free tiers of ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) can use your conversations to improve their models. Never paste: client contracts, internal company financials, personal identification numbers, passwords, health information, or anything marked "confidential" or "for internal use only."

🧠 Knowledge Check — Think About These

1

You ask Claude to write code. It gives you something that looks correct. Should you copy-paste it directly into a production system? Why or why not?

2

Your manager asks you to summarize a 50-page NDA for a client. Is it okay to paste the full document into ChatGPT free tier? What's the risk?

3

AI tells you the population of Bhutan is 3.2 million as of 2025. How would you verify this before using it in a presentation? What's the danger of trusting it directly?

🔨 The key mental model: AI is a power tool, not a cheat code. A carpenter with a nail gun is 10× more productive — but they still need to know where to put the nails. Your job is to know what to build. AI helps you build it faster. A carpenter who doesn't know what they're building and just fires nails everywhere isn't productive — they're destructive.
