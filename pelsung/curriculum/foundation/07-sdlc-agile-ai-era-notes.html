Part 1

The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

Every software product you've ever used — mBoB, Druk Ride, WhatsApp, Facebook, Instagram — went through the same seven stages. Teams do them differently, but the stages are the same.

1. Plan

Who is this for? What problem are we solving? What's in scope, what's not? Is it worth doing at all?

2. Analysis

Detailed requirements gathering. User stories, acceptance criteria, data models. Turning the plan into something concrete.

3. Design

Mockups, user flows, database shape, architecture. How will it look and how will it work?

4. Build

Write the code. Integrate parts. Commit to Git.

5. Test

Does it work? Does it break in weird ways? Is it fast enough? Secure enough?

6. Deploy

Release to real users. App Store, website goes live, customers can sign up.

7. Maintain

Fix bugs users find. Add features. Update dependencies. Keep the servers running.

Why learn this?

Whatever vertical you end up in — Dev, Design, QA, or BA — you will work on some stage of this cycle. Knowing the whole picture helps you understand why your work matters and what the next person needs from you.

Part 2

Running Example: mBoB (Mobile Banking)

Think of mBoB — the Bank of Bhutan mobile app you probably have on your phone. Log in, transfer money, pay the electricity bill, recharge a TashiCell number. How did BoB actually build it? Here's how it moved through the SDLC:

  • Plan: Users = BoB customers with smartphones. Problem = long queues at branches for basic transactions (transfers, bill payments, balance checks). Goal = let customers do 80% of their banking from their phone.
  • Analysis: User stories — "As a customer, I can log in with my PIN"; "I can transfer money to another account"; "I can pay my electricity bill"; "I can see my balance". Acceptance criteria per story. Required data: accounts, transactions, saved beneficiaries, bill payees.
  • Design: Screens — Login, Home, Transfer, Bills, Statement, Profile. Flow = Login → PIN → Action → Confirm → Receipt. Architecture = mobile app + secure API + core banking system.
  • Build: Native Android + iOS apps. Secure backend API. Integrations with SMS OTP, biometric login, RMA/BFS clearing.
  • Test: Happy path (login, transfer Nu. 100, receive SMS confirmation). Edge cases (wrong PIN 3 times → lockout, network drops mid-transfer → does money vanish?). Security testing (can an attacker steal the PIN? Is SSL configured correctly?).
  • Deploy: Soft-launch with BoB staff. Then selected branches, then all customers. Published on Play Store and App Store.
  • Maintain: 24/7 uptime, fraud monitoring, security patches. New features (QR pay, scheduled transfers, international remittance). Bug fixes and customer support tickets.
Part 3

Waterfall vs Agile vs Scrum

These are the three most common methodologies. They're all ways of moving through the SDLC stages — but with very different rhythms.

💧 Waterfall

Finish each stage completely before moving to the next. Plan everything up front. Classical engineering style.

Good for: Fixed-scope projects, regulated industries (aviation, defence), projects where change is expensive.

Weakness: If you discover halfway through that you built the wrong thing — too late, too expensive to change.

Use when requirements are crystal clear and won't change.

⚡ Agile

A philosophy, not a recipe. Build in small chunks, show them to users, learn, adjust, repeat.

Good for: Most modern products. Software is soft — it should be easy to change.

Weakness: Requires active involvement from customers. Hard for teams that want a detailed plan up front.

Use when you expect to learn as you go.

🏃 Scrum

A specific flavour of Agile. Work in 2-week "sprints". Daily 15-minute stand-ups. Sprint planning at the start. Review & retro at the end. Clear roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers.

Good for: Stable teams with a clear backlog and a steady weekly rhythm.

Weakness: Ceremony overhead. Easy to do the rituals without the agile spirit.

Use when you need predictability and a stable rhythm.

Scrum ≠ Agile

A lot of people treat Scrum and Agile as the same thing. They're not. Agile is the mindset (value change, deliver often, talk to users). Scrum is one way to practice it — with specific roles and meetings. You can be agile without Scrum, and you can do Scrum without being agile.

Part 4

Other Methodologies — Know They Exist

You'll hear these terms thrown around. Don't memorise the details — just know what they are and when they fit.

Kanban

A visual board with columns: "To Do → Doing → Done". The rule: limit how many things are in "Doing" at once. Work flows continuously — no sprints.

Use for: support teams, IT help-desks, continuous ticket flow.

Extreme Programming (XP)

Very disciplined engineering: pair programming, test-first development, frequent small releases, continuous integration.

Use for: critical systems where quality cannot slip.

Lean

Borrowed from Toyota. Eliminate waste. Build only what the user actually needs. Measure everything and learn fast.

Use for: startups validating a new idea with minimum effort.

Spiral

Repeat Plan → Build → Evaluate in widening spirals. Each spiral is bigger than the last. Focus on reducing the biggest risk first.

Use for: large, high-risk systems — aerospace, medical devices, big government programs.

RAD (Rapid Application Development)

Build working prototypes with the user in the room. Show, get feedback, refine, repeat. Very fast, very informal.

Use for: internal tools, demos, MVPs where you need to see it to know what you want.

DevOps

Not just a methodology — a culture. Developers and Operations as one team. Heavy automation of build, test, deploy. Releases can happen every day (or every hour).

Use for: products with frequent releases and high uptime needs.

Scrumban

Scrum's structure + Kanban's flow. Use sprints as timeboxes but don't strictly commit the backlog. Popular with teams transitioning from Scrum to continuous delivery.

Use for: teams moving away from rigid Scrum but still wanting some rhythm.

SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)

Scrum for large organisations — hundreds of developers coordinating on one product. Adds layers of planning above team-level Scrum.

Use for: enterprises, telecoms, big banks.
Part 5

How SELISE Actually Works

SELISE doesn't follow any methodology 100% strictly. It's a mix of Scrum and Kanban:

  • We use sprints as timeboxes (typically 2 weeks) to plan rough chunks of work.
  • No rigid daily stand-ups. No forced retrospectives every sprint.
  • GitHub Projects is our board — issues move from Backlog → In Progress → Review → Done.
  • Communication happens async on GitHub and Teams; sync only when needed.
  • PRs link back to issues so you can trace any line of code to the requirement it fulfilled.

What this means for you

When you join a SELISE team, don't expect a textbook Scrum setup. Read the team's GitHub board — that's where the truth lives. Ask your tech lead what rituals this specific project follows. Every project is slightly different.

Part 6

The Hourglass Model — Software in the AI Era

Before AI coding tools, the Build stage consumed most of the time. Developers typed every line of code by hand. Specs were often thin — it was faster to just "figure it out in code".

AI changes the shape. Tools like Claude Code can generate working code from a clear description in seconds. What used to take an afternoon takes ten minutes.

But AI is bad at deciding what to build. It takes your spec literally. A vague spec gives you vague software. A wrong spec gives you polished wrong software — the most dangerous kind.

The shape shift

  • Spec & Plan → get bigger. Much more time clarifying what to build and why.
  • Design → gets bigger. Clearer handoffs to AI via prompts and examples.
  • Build → shrinks. AI drafts, humans steer and review.
  • Test & Review → grows. You can't trust AI output without reading it carefully.
  • Deploy → automated. CI/CD pipelines handle it.

Think of the shape like an hourglass

  • Wide at the top: lots of time on specs, design, architecture.
  • Narrow in the middle: AI does the typing.
  • Wide at the bottom: lots of time on review, testing, making sure the AI didn't do something weird.

This is why we say: your taste and judgement become more valuable, not less. AI is a power tool. It amplifies good engineers and amplifies bad engineers equally — which is to say, bad engineers produce bad software faster. Good engineers produce great software faster.

Practice Exercise (Redo at Home)

Pick a small app idea that serves Bhutan — examples: Inter-dzongkhag bus ticket app, local farmer-to-market app, Dzongkha language-learning app.

Then write down:

  1. 3 specs — what the app must do. Be specific. "User can see available seats" is better than "users can see things".
  2. 3 test ideas — what could go wrong. "Two users book the same seat at the same time" — what happens?
  3. 2 things AI can help with & 2 things only a human should decide. Why?

Bring your answers to the next session if you want feedback.

🎯 Key takeaways from Class 07

  • Every product moves through the same 7 SDLC stages — the methodology just changes the rhythm.
  • Agile is a philosophy; Scrum is one flavour of it. They're not interchangeable.
  • SELISE uses a mix of Scrum and Kanban — read each project's GitHub board to see how that team works.
  • In the AI era, the SDLC takes on an hourglass shape: specs & review expand, typing shrinks.
  • AI amplifies your taste. It does not replace it.
