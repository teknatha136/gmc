💬 Communication Fundamentals

Communication is more than just sending a message. It's about making sure the right information reaches the right person in the right way — and that they've understood it. In professional settings, most misunderstandings, project delays, and conflicts come from communication that broke down somewhere along the way.

The Communication Model — Noise Can Enter at Any Step
🧑‍💻Sender
→
💭Message
→
📡Channel
→
🧑‍💼Receiver
→
🔁Feedback

Noise can be: unclear intent, wrong channel choice, a distracted receiver, or no confirmation of understanding.

Think of this model every time you send a message or give instructions. Ask yourself: Could this be misunderstood? Am I using the right channel? Did I confirm they understood? Most workplace miscommunication happens because someone skipped one of these steps.

🗣️ Verbal Communication

  • Speak at a pace others can follow — slow down when explaining something new
  • Use clear, simple language; avoid jargon when speaking to someone outside your field
  • Check in: "Does that make sense?"
  • Avoid filler words like "um", "like", "basically" — they undermine clarity
  • Volume and tone matter as much as the actual words

✍️ Written Communication

  • One idea per sentence — don't try to say everything at once
  • Lead with the most important information, then add context
  • Use bullet points for lists — easier to scan than run-on paragraphs
  • Always proofread before hitting send
  • Shorter is almost always better — if you can cut a sentence without losing meaning, cut it

👁️ Non-Verbal Cues

  • Eye contact signals engagement and confidence
  • Open posture (arms uncrossed, leaning forward) signals you're approachable
  • Nodding while listening = you're following along
  • Response time in messaging conveys urgency: a 3-day reply to an urgent Slack feels dismissive
  • Emojis in work chats: read the room — fine in casual team chats, avoid in formal emails
1

Be Clear, Not Just Correct

A technically accurate message that confuses the reader has failed. Always ask: "Would someone who knows nothing about this understand exactly what I need from them?" Correctness is about facts; clarity is about whether the message works.

2

Choose the Right Channel

Urgent & quick? → Slack or chat. Decision or record-keeping needed? → Email. Sensitive, nuanced, or emotional? → In-person or video call. One rule: don't send a 500-word Slack message. That's what email is for.

3

Active Listening is a Skill

Most people listen while already composing their reply. That's not listening — it's waiting. Listen to understand, not to respond. When someone finishes, summarize what you heard back to them ("So what you're saying is...") to confirm you actually got it.

🎯 Discussion question: Think of a time communication broke down — at school, at home, or at work. What went wrong? Was it a sender problem? Wrong channel? Distracted receiver? No feedback loop?

📧 Email Etiquette

Email is still the primary formal communication channel in most organizations. A well-written email gets a fast, useful response. A poorly written one gets ignored, misunderstood, or causes the person to ask three follow-up questions before they can act. The goal of every professional email is for the reader to know exactly what's needed from them in under 30 seconds.

📝 Subject Line Rules

  • Bad examples: "Hi" / "Question" / "Important!!!" / (blank)
  • Good pattern: [Action type] What it's about — deadline if relevant
  • Use action tags in brackets: [FYI] [Action needed] [Decision] [For your records]
  • Keep it under 60 characters — most email clients cut off anything longer
  • The subject line is the first thing they see. Make it count.

👥 To / CC / BCC — Use Them Correctly

  • To: People who must read and act or respond
  • CC: People who need to be aware but don't need to do anything
  • BCC: Use sparingly — for sensitive situations (e.g., removing someone from a chain gracefully)
  • Reply-All: only use it if everyone in the chain genuinely needs your reply
  • Don't CC your manager as a passive-aggressive power move — solve issues directly first

Here's what a well-structured professional email looks like. Notice it has a clear subject, a greeting, a single clear ask, a deadline, and a polite sign-off. It takes under 20 seconds to read and act on.

From:Karma Wangchuk <karma@selisegroup.com>
To:Bhuwan Sharma <bhuwan@selisegroup.com>
CC:Tek Nath Acharya <tek@selisegroup.com>
Subject:[Action needed] API documentation draft — feedback needed CLEAR SUBJECT

Hi Bhuwan, GREETING


I've finished the first draft of the API documentation for the user auth module and would appreciate your review before we share it with the QA team.


What I need from you: CLEAR ASK

→ Review the endpoint descriptions for accuracy
→ Flag anything that's unclear or missing
→ Reply with comments within 3 working days DEADLINE


The document is here: [link to doc]


Happy to jump on a quick call if you have questions.


Thanks,
Karma SIGN-OFF
Software Developer · SELISE Bhutan

⚠️ Common mistakes to avoid: Writing paragraph-long essays when a bullet list works. Using "per my last email" — it's passive-aggressive and damages relationships. Never send an email while you're upset — write the draft, walk away, re-read it in 30 minutes. And never CC a manager just to pressure someone into responding faster.
✍ Exercise · Groups of 4–5

Email Rewrite Challenge

⏱ 30 minutes 👥 Groups of 4–5 (~9 groups) 📋 Pen & paper or phone
1
Setup (3 min): Divide into groups of 4–5. Each group gets one "bad email" scenario from the list below.
2
Rewrite (12 min): Groups rewrite the bad email to be professional, clear, and well-structured. Apply all the rules: subject line with brackets, proper greeting, clear ask, deadline, sign-off.
3
Share (10 min): 3 groups share their rewrites aloud. The class gives feedback — what did they do well? What would you change?
4
Debrief (5 min): Trainer wraps up with the 3 most common improvements observed across the groups.
Scenarios (rotate between groups)
A. "hey can you do the thing we talked about yesterday by tomorrow thanks" → Rewrite as a proper deadline email to a teammate.
B. A 300-word rambling email asking a manager to approve a day off → Rewrite to be 5 lines maximum.
C. A reply-all chain of 12 messages about where to have lunch → Rewrite as a single clear Slack message with a decision question.

⏰ Time Management

Everyone starts every day with 24 hours. The difference between people who get things done and people who feel perpetually overwhelmed is not ability — it's knowing what to prioritize and protecting their time to do it. In a software team, your wasted time often means someone else's task is blocked. Time management is a team skill, not just a personal one.

Most wasted time isn't laziness. It's unclear priorities, digital distraction, and not knowing how to say no professionally. The Eisenhower Matrix is a simple tool that makes priorities visible.

The Eisenhower Matrix — Sort Every Task Into One of Four Quadrants
⚡ Urgent
🌙 Not Urgent
Important
🔥 Q1 · Do First Deadlines, client issues, bugs in production
📅 Q2 · Schedule Learning, planning, building skills, health
Not Important
🤝 Q3 · Delegate Interruptions, some meetings, admin tasks
🗑️ Q4 · Eliminate Doom-scrolling, unnecessary notifications, 12-reply chains

The most important quadrant is Q2 — things that are important but not urgent. Learning a new skill, planning your week, reviewing code carefully — none of these feel "on fire" today, but they're what separate good professionals from great ones. If you always live in Q1 (firefighting), it's usually because you're not spending enough time in Q2.

📦 Time Blocking

  • Reserve calendar blocks for deep, focused work
  • Group similar tasks — answer all emails at once instead of every 10 minutes
  • Protect your mornings for your hardest or most important work — energy is highest then
  • Don't let "quick meetings" fragment your focus blocks
  • Even a 90-minute uninterrupted block dramatically improves output quality

📵 Digital Discipline

  • Turn off non-critical notifications during deep work
  • Check email and Slack at set times — not constantly throughout the day
  • Use phone-free time for important or creative work
  • Every context switch (phone notification, colleague interruption) costs ~20 minutes of refocus time
  • The "one more scroll" trap is designed by billion-dollar companies — set app timers
🗣️ Discussion question: What's the biggest thing that wastes your time on a typical day? Is it Q3 (urgent but not important interruptions) or Q4 (things that shouldn't be happening at all)? What one change could free up an extra hour?
