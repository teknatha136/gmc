Part 1

The Cast of Characters

When most people think "software team", they think "developers". In reality, a real product team has many more roles. Some are obvious (Designer, Tester). Some hide in the background (DevOps, SRE). Here's the full list you'll encounter at SELISE and in the industry.

🧭 BA / PM (Business Analyst / Product Manager)

Defines what to build and why. Writes requirements and user stories. Talks to customers. Prioritises the backlog.

🎨 UX / Designer

Designs how the product looks and feels. Mockups, user flows, design system. Works closely with PM & devs.

💻 Frontend Dev (FE)

Builds what the user sees — web pages, mobile screens. React, Angular, React Native, Flutter.

⚙️ Backend Dev (BE)

Builds the invisible engine — APIs, databases, business logic. Node.js, .NET, Java, Python.

🧪 QA / PAA (Quality Assurance / Product Acceptance Analyst)

Tests that the product works. Catches bugs before users do. Writes test plans. Some QAs write automated tests (SDET).

🏗️ Architect

Designs the overall shape of the system — how big pieces talk to each other, what tech to pick, what scales and what doesn't.

🔧 DevOps / SRE / Platform / DevSecOps — one role, many names

In theory these are four separate disciplines — DevOps automates build/deploy, SREs keep production running and go on-call, Platform engineers build internal tools for other devs, DevSecOps adds security. In practice, at most companies one person does all of it. You'll hear any of these titles used interchangeably. Only huge organisations (Google, banks, telcos) actually staff them as separate roles.

📞 Client-facing PM / Support

Bridges dev team and customer. Handles client questions. Manages expectations. At SELISE, often speaks the client's language.

📈 Data Analyst / Data Engineer

Turns product usage data into insight. Builds dashboards, runs experiments, sets up data pipelines.

Why so many roles?

Not every team has every role. Small teams combine them — one person might be "FE + BE + Designer". Large teams split them — "Backend" alone might be 10 people divided into services. The list above is the full vocabulary, not a required roster.

Part 2

Handoffs — How Work Moves

A typical flow looks like this:

  1. BA/PM → Designer: "Here are the requirements and user stories."
  2. Designer → Dev: "Here are the mockups and interaction specs."
  3. FE ↔ BE: "We agreed on this API contract — you build it, I'll call it."
  4. Dev → QA: "Here's the build. These are the changes. Please verify."
  5. QA → DevOps/SRE: "All tests pass. Ready to deploy."
  6. SRE → All: "Deployed at 14:23. Monitoring for issues."

Most bugs come from bad handoffs

When the spec is unclear, the designer guesses. When the design ignores technical limits, the dev improvises. When the tester wasn't told what changed, they miss regressions. Clear handoffs = fewer bugs = faster delivery.

Part 3

Domain Flavours — Industry Shapes the Team

The core roles stay the same, but each domain adds its own specialists on top.

🏦 Finance & Banking

Add: Compliance Officer, Risk Analyst, External Auditor. Expect heavy documentation, formal approvals, slow releases. Every change has to pass audit.

🏥 Health & Medical

Add: Clinical Informatics Lead, Privacy Officer (HIPAA/GDPR), Regulatory Affairs, Patient Safety Reviewer. Patient data is highly protected; mistakes can harm people.

⛓️ Blockchain / Crypto

Add: Smart Contract Auditor, Protocol Engineer, Tokenomics Lead. Heavy emphasis on security review — a single bug in a smart contract can lose millions.

🛍️ E-commerce

Typically smaller teams. Add: Merchandiser, Content Manager. Fast, frequent releases, lots of A/B testing. (SELISE's Swiss-Ski Store is this kind of project.)

When you join a project, one of the first things to learn is: what domain is this? The domain tells you who your "hidden stakeholders" are — the compliance officer who must sign off, the auditor who'll review next quarter, the regulator who demanded this feature.

Part 5

RACI — The Industry Standard

When a team gets bigger than 3 people, "who does what" starts getting fuzzy. RACI is a matrix that makes ownership explicit.

  • R — Responsible: The person (or people) doing the work.
  • A — Accountable: The one person who owns the outcome. Exactly one per task.
  • C — Consulted: People whose input is needed before a decision. Two-way dialogue.
  • I — Informed: People kept in the loop after the decision. One-way notification.

Here's what it looks like for a "Release a new PSP feature" flow using real Project #238 team members:

Task @khushwanth @CMRai92 @ngawang-zangphel @wangchuk99 @pemawangyel @DedalusRoot
Write requirementIR/ACCIC
Design mockupICR/AIIC
Build APIICCR/AII
QA sign-offICIIR/AI
Release decisionACRRRC
Client communicationCIIIIR/A

Rules for a good RACI:

  • Every row must have exactly one A.
  • Every row should have at least one R (often the A is also the R).
  • Don't over-consult — if everyone is C on everything, nothing moves.
  • Don't over-inform — respect people's attention.
Part 6

RCI — The SELISE Adaptation

SELISE uses a simpler version: RCI. Our CEO Julian's insight was:

  1. In German, there is no clean word that distinguishes "Responsible" from "Accountable". The two English words collapse into one in practice.
  2. In a well-run team, R is always exactly one person — otherwise no one truly owns it.
  3. If R is one person, and A is one person, and they're almost always the same person… why have both?

So at SELISE:

  • R — Responsible: The one person who owns the outcome AND makes sure the work gets done. Exactly one.
  • C — Consulted: Input needed before the decision.
  • I — Informed: Told after the decision.

Why this matters for you

When you join a SELISE team and something is unclear about who owns a task, the first question to ask is: "Whose R is this?". If two people answer "mine", there's a problem in the matrix. If nobody answers, also a problem. Fix the matrix before the conflict becomes a missed deadline.

You should still learn RACI — it's what the rest of the world uses, and you'll see it in every enterprise and government setting. But RCI is what you'll use at SELISE day-to-day.

Part 7

The AI-Era Team

Does AI mean teams get smaller? Yes — but not in the way people fear.

  • Fewer junior devs doing boilerplate. AI writes most of the repetitive code.
  • More senior judgement. Someone has to read what the AI produced and say "yes, this is right" or "no, try again, this misses X".
  • More "T-shaped" people. One person now covers more of the stack — an FE dev who also writes API code, a QA who also writes automated tests.
  • Spec-writing becomes a first-class skill. Whoever writes the clearest spec gets the best output from AI.

Implication for you

Don't aim to be the person who just "writes code fast". AI will do that. Aim to be the person who understands the problem deeply, writes clear specs, and reviews output critically. Those skills multiply your output 10x when you hand the typing to AI.

🎯 Key takeaways from Class 08

  • A real software team has 10+ roles — not just "developer".
  • Domain (finance, health, crypto) adds specialists on top of the base roles.
  • Team size follows the project stage — active = big, maintenance = small.
  • RACI (R, A, C, I) is the global industry standard.
  • RCI is SELISE's simpler version — one person per R, no "A".
  • In the AI era, team shape shifts toward fewer but more senior, more T-shaped people with sharper spec-writing skills.
