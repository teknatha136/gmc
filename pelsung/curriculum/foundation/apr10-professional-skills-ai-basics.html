Class 02 · 10:00 – 11:55

Professional Skills

Communication, email etiquette, and time management — the invisible infrastructure of every effective professional.

10:00 – 10:15 · 15 min
👋 Icebreaker — Two Truths, One Work Style

Start the room. Everyone shares 3 statements about their work style — two are true, one is false. The class guesses. This is low-stakes, breaks hesitation, and sets a collaborative tone for the day.

💡 Trainer tip: You go first to model it. Example: "I reply to every message within 5 minutes. I use 3 different to-do apps. I've never sent a work email." Then let 5–6 volunteers share. Tie each reveal back to today's topics (email, time management, communication style).

💬 Communication Fundamentals
Sender–receiver model Verbal vs written vs non-verbal Active listening Clarity & brevity

🔁Feedback

Noise can enter at any step — unclear intent, wrong channel, distracted receiver, no confirmation.

🗣️ Verbal Communication

  • Speak at a pace others can follow
  • Use clear, simple language
  • Confirm understanding — "Does that make sense?"
  • Avoid filler words (um, like, basically)
  • Volume & tone matter as much as words

✍️ Written Communication

  • One idea per sentence
  • Lead with the most important info
  • Use bullet points for lists
  • Always proofread before sending
  • Shorter is almost always better

👁️ Non-Verbal Cues

  • Eye contact = engaged & confident
  • Open posture = approachable
  • Nodding = active listening
  • Response time in messaging = urgency signal
  • Emojis in work chat = know your audience
1

Be Clear, Not Just Correct

A technically correct message that confuses the reader has failed. Ask: "Would someone who knows nothing about this understand what I need from them?"

2

Choose the Right Channel

Urgent & quick → Slack/chat. Decision-requiring or documented → Email. Nuanced or emotional → in-person/video call. Don't send a 500-word Slack message.

3

Active Listening is a Skill

Don't prepare your response while the other person is still speaking. Listen to understand, not to reply. Summarize what you heard to confirm.

🎯 Discussion prompt (5 min): Ask the class — "Think of a time communication broke down — what went wrong?" Take 3–4 answers and map them to the model on the board (sender problem? channel problem? noise?).
10:45 – 11:05 · 20 min
📧 Email Etiquette
Subject lines Greetings & sign-offs Tone & brevity Reply-all etiquette CC vs BCC

📝 Subject Line Rules

  • Bad: "Hi" / "Question" / "Important!!!"
  • Good: "[Action needed] API docs review by Apr 15"
  • Include action type in brackets: [FYI] [Action needed] [Decision]
  • Keep it under 60 characters
  • Never leave subject blank

👥 To / CC / BCC

  • To: People who must act or respond
  • CC: People who need to know (no action needed)
  • BCC: Sensitive situations only — use sparingly
  • Don't CC the whole team on every reply
  • Reply-All = only when everyone needs your response
From:Karma Wangchuk <karma@selise.ch>
To:Bhuwan Sharma <bhuwan@selise.ch>
CC:Tek Nath Acharya <tek@selise.ch>
Subject:[Action needed] API documentation draft — feedback by Apr 14

Hi Bhuwan, GREETING


I've finished the first draft of the API documentation for the user auth module and would appreciate your review before we share it with the QA team.


What I need from you: CLEAR ASK

→ Review the endpoint descriptions for accuracy
→ Flag anything that's unclear or missing
→ Reply with comments by Tuesday, April 14


The document is here: [link to doc] LINK


Happy to jump on a quick call if you have questions.

⚠️ Common mistakes to highlight: Writing essays when a bullet list works. Using "per my last email" (passive-aggressive). Sending angry emails when still upset — always draft, walk away, re-read. Cc-ing managers as a power move instead of solving the issue directly.
✍ Exercise 1 · Groups of 4–5

Email Rewrite Challenge

⏱ 30 minutes 👥 Groups of 4–5 (~9 groups) 📋 Pen & paper or phone
1
Setup (3 min): Divide into groups of 4–5. Each group gets one "bad email" scenario (rotate between groups).
2
Rewrite (12 min): Groups rewrite the bad email to be professional, clear, and well-structured. Apply all the rules: subject line, greeting, clear ask, sign-off.
3
Share (10 min): 3 groups share their rewrites aloud. Class gives feedback — what did they do well? What would you change?
4
Debrief (5 min): Trainer wraps up with the 3 most common fixes observed across the room.
Scenarios to use:
A. "hey can you do the thing we talked about yesterday by tomorrow thanks" → Rewrite as a proper deadline email to a teammate. B. A 300-word rambling email asking a manager to approve a day off → Rewrite to be 5 lines max. C. A reply-all chain of 12 messages about lunch → Rewrite as a single Slack-appropriate message with a clear question.

⏰ Time Management
Eisenhower Matrix Time blocking Saying no professionally Avoiding digital distraction
Eisenhower Matrix — How to Prioritize Everything
⚡ Urgent
🌙 Not Urgent
Important
🔥 Do First Deadlines, crises, bugs in production
📅 Schedule Learning, planning, relationship building
Not Important
🤝 Delegate Interruptions, some meetings, admin tasks
🗑️ Eliminate Doom-scrolling, unnecessary notifications

📦 Time Blocking

  • Reserve calendar blocks for deep work
  • Group similar tasks (emails → all at once)
  • Protect mornings for your hardest work
  • Don't let "quick meetings" destroy focus blocks
  • Even 90-min blocks change output quality

📵 Digital Discipline

  • Turn off non-critical notifications during deep work
  • Check email/Slack at set times (not constantly)
  • Use phone-free zones for important work
  • Every context switch costs ~20 min of refocus time
  • The "one more scroll" trap is real — set app timers
🗣️ Quick discussion (5 min): Ask — "What's the biggest thing that wastes your time?" Take 4–5 answers. Usually: phone notifications, unclear tasks, waiting for others. Connect to the matrix — are those Q3 or Q4 activities?

AI Basics & Prompting

What AI actually is, how tools like Claude and ChatGPT work, and how to write prompts that get you real results — not generic nonsense.

12:10 – 12:35 · 25 min
🤖 What is AI? (And What Isn't It?)
LLMs explained simply ChatGPT vs Claude What AI is good at & bad at AI in your daily workflow

🧠 What AI Is

  • Pattern recognition trained on text
  • Predicts the most likely next word
  • Very good at language tasks
  • Learns from your conversation context
  • Gets better prompts → gives better output

🚫 What AI Is Not

  • Not a search engine (can hallucinate)
  • Doesn't "know" things — predicts text
  • Not always right — verify important facts
  • No real-time knowledge (has a cutoff date)
  • Not a person — no feelings, no memory across chats

⚡ What AI Excels At

  • Drafting & rewriting text
  • Summarizing long documents
  • Explaining complex topics simply
  • Generating ideas / brainstorming
  • Writing & debugging code

💚 ChatGPT

  • Made by OpenAI
  • Very popular, widely known
  • GPT-4o is the current flagship
  • Strong at general tasks
  • Has web search (paid tier)
  • Great for: writing, coding, analysis
vs

🟤 Claude

  • Made by Anthropic
  • Designed to be safer & more careful
  • Claude 4 Sonnet is the current flagship
  • Very long context window
  • Excellent at nuanced writing & reasoning
  • Great for: coding (Claude Code), long docs
💡 Live demo (5 min): Open Claude or ChatGPT on the projector. Ask: "Explain what a software API is to a 10-year-old." Show how the model responds. Then ask: "Now explain it to a senior software engineer." Show how it adapts. This demonstrates context-sensitivity and the power of well-framed prompts.
12:35 – 13:00 · 25 min
✏️ The Art of Prompting
Prompt anatomy Context & role setting Iterative refinement Few-shot examples
Anatomy of a High-Quality Prompt
ContextI am a software developer at a company building a digital ID system for Bhutan.
TaskWrite a professional email to a client explaining a 2-week delay in the API delivery.
FormatUse a formal but empathetic tone. Keep it under 150 words. Include a subject line.
ExampleSubject line style: "[Project name] Update — [topic]"

The four elements: Context (who/what/where) · Task (what you want) · Format (how you want it) · Example (optional, but powerful)

1

Give it a Role

"You are a senior UX designer reviewing a mobile app..." — Role-setting dramatically shifts the quality and perspective of the response.

2

Be Specific About the Output

Vague: "Write something about APIs." Specific: "Write a 3-paragraph explanation of REST APIs for someone who has never coded before, using analogies."

3

Iterate — Don't Settle for the First Response

AI is a conversation. If the first response isn't right, say: "That's good, but make it shorter and less formal." You can keep refining in the same chat.

4

Give Examples When You Can

"Here's an example of the style I want: [paste example]. Now write something similar about [topic]." This is called few-shot prompting — very powerful.

Prompt Examples — Weak vs Strong
WEAK

Write about time management.

STRONG

You are a productivity coach speaking to a group of 20-year-old tech trainees in Bhutan who have no prior corporate work experience. Write a 5-tip guide to time management that is practical, specific, and uses examples relevant to software teams. Keep each tip under 50 words.

WEAK

Fix my code.

STRONG

I have a JavaScript function that should filter an array of user objects by age. It's not returning anything. Here is the code: [paste code]. Identify the bug, explain why it's wrong, and give me the fixed version.

✍ Exercise 2 · Pairs + Class

The Prompt Battle

⏱ 30 minutes 👥 Pairs (22 pairs) 📱 Phone / laptop with Claude or ChatGPT
1
Round 1 — Weak vs Strong (10 min): Each pair gets the same task. Person A writes a weak prompt. Person B writes a strong prompt using the anatomy framework. Both submit to AI. Compare the outputs side by side.
2
Round 2 — Refine (8 min): Take the weak prompt output. Improve it in 3 follow-up messages without rewriting the prompt from scratch. Use only instructions like: "Make it shorter", "Use bullet points", "Give a real-world example", "Change the tone to be more formal".
3
Share (8 min): 4 pairs share their best prompt + output on the projector. Class votes: which response was most useful?
4
Debrief (4 min): What made the strong prompts better? What surprised you about what the AI could do?
Tasks to use (rotate between pairs):
A. Write an email to a client explaining you need more information before you can proceed with their request. B. Explain what a database is to someone who has never used a computer. C. Create a 5-day study plan for learning HTML and CSS from scratch. D. Write a job posting for a junior software tester at a tech company in Bhutan.
13:30 – 13:45 · 15 min
⚖️ AI Dos & Don'ts
Ethical use Hallucinations Privacy & data AI in the workplace

✅ Do

  • Use AI to draft, then edit it yourself
  • Verify facts AI gives you — especially numbers & dates
  • Use AI to learn — ask it to explain, not just give answers
  • Treat it as a thinking partner, not a replacement for thinking
  • Tell your team when you've used AI to produce something
  • Use AI to explore options you wouldn't have thought of

❌ Don't

  • Paste confidential client data into public AI tools
  • Submit AI output as your own work without reviewing it
  • Blindly trust code AI writes — always read & test it
  • Use AI for decisions that need human judgment (hiring, firing)
  • Assume AI knows the latest news — it has a training cutoff
  • Share passwords, personal IDs, or sensitive info in prompts

🧠 Knowledge Check — Quick Fire Questions

1

You ask Claude to write code. It gives you something that looks correct. Should you copy-paste it directly into production? Why or why not?

2

Your manager asks you to summarize a 50-page NDA for a client. Is it okay to paste the full document into ChatGPT? What's the risk?

3

AI tells you the population of Bhutan is 3.2 million as of 2025. How would you verify this? What's the danger of trusting it directly?

🎯 Key message to land: AI is a power tool, not a cheat code. A carpenter with a nail gun is 10× more productive — but they still need to know where to put the nails. Your job is to know what to build. AI helps you build it faster.
