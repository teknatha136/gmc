Part 1

What is a flowchart (and who invented it)

A flowchart is a diagram that shows the steps of a process, in order, using a small vocabulary of standard shapes connected by arrows. Flowcharts were introduced by industrial engineers Frank and Lillian Gilbreth in 1921 to document factory operations, and were later standardized by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI X3.5, 1960s) and ISO (ISO 5807, 1985).

Flowcharts are the grand-parent of nearly every process diagram that came after — BPMN, activity diagrams, swimlanes. What sets them apart is their simplicity: a flowchart answers "what happens next?" without worrying about "who does it?" or "what data moves?".

The flowchart mindset

A flowchart describes an algorithm — a step-by-step procedure. It's the right tool when what happens matters more than who does it. It is also the right tool when you're at a whiteboard explaining a decision rule to a stakeholder who has never seen a BPMN diagram.

Part 2

The five core shapes (and a few extras)

A flowchart rarely needs more than five shapes. When you reach for shape number six, ask yourself whether a different diagram type would serve you better.

Terminator · oval / pill

Marks where the flow starts and where it ends. A flowchart has one Start and at least one End.

Process · rectangle

A single action or step. Label with a verb + object: "Check balance", "Print receipt".

Decision · diamond

A branch point. Label with a yes/no question: "Balance > 0?". Every outgoing arrow must be labeled (yes, no, or a specific condition).

Input / Output · parallelogram

Data coming in (user entering something, reading a file) or going out (displaying a message, printing a form).

Arrow · flow line

Direction of the flow. Always has an arrowhead. At decisions, always labeled.

Extras you'll occasionally see:

ShapeMeaningWhen to use
Subroutine · rectangle with double side-barsPredefined process / subprocedureTo hide detail — "Validate credentials" then draw that separately
Document · rectangle with wavy bottomA paper or digital documentWhen a physical or digital document is an output
Manual operation · trapezoidA step done by a human, not automatedTo distinguish "manual" from "automated" in hybrid processes
On-page / off-page connector · small circle / pentagonJump to another point in the diagram / on another pageTo avoid long crossing arrows on a large diagram

Part 5

When a flowchart is the right choice

✅ Reach for a flowchart

  • Pure algorithmic logic (calculations, validations, decisions)
  • Eligibility / qualification rules
  • Troubleshooting guides and help articles
  • Quick-reference sheets for a single role
  • Whiteboarding with non-technical stakeholders
  • Pseudocode-style developer handoff

⚠️ Pick a different diagram when…

  • Multiple actors or organizations are involved → use BPMN or swimlane
  • Parallel work needs to merge back together → use activity diagram
  • You're modeling data movement → use a DFD
  • You're scoping a system's features → use use case diagram
  • The process will drive a workflow engine → use BPMN

Common flowchart mistakes

MistakeWhy it hurtsFix
Unlabeled decision branchesReader can't tell which exit is "yes"Always label every outgoing arrow from a diamond
Arrows with no arrowheadsDirection is ambiguousEvery flow line ends in an arrowhead
No Start or no EndReader doesn't know where to begin / whether flow is completeOne Start. At least one End. Every path reaches an End.
Loops with no exitInfinite loop — even in the diagramEvery loop has a condition that lets you escape (retry count, timeout, "give up")
Mixing shape meaningsUsing a rectangle for a decision once, a diamond next timePick a convention and stick to it across the whole diagram
Trying to show "who does it"Flowchart has no concept of actorIf actor matters, upgrade to swimlane
50 shapes on one pageNobody will read itBreak into subroutines (connector shape) or split by phase
Takeaways

Key takeaways

What to carry forward

  • Flowcharts are the oldest and most universally understood process diagram.
  • Five shapes do most of the work: oval · rectangle · diamond · parallelogram · arrow.
  • Best for algorithms, decisions, eligibility, and troubleshooting — not multi-actor processes.
  • Every decision branch must be labeled. Every path must reach an End.
  • Don't try to show "who does it" — if that matters, upgrade to a swimlane diagram.
  • When in doubt, sketch a flowchart first — you can always upgrade it to BPMN later if the problem grows.

Recommended tools: paper and pen; then Lucidchart, draw.io, Miro, Excalidraw (for casual), Visio (for corporate).
