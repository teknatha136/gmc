Part 1

What is BPMN (and why it exists)

BPMN — Business Process Model and Notation — is an internationally recognized graphical language for describing business processes. It was created by the Business Process Management Initiative in 2004 and is now maintained by the OMG (Object Management Group). The current widely-used version is BPMN 2.0.

Before BPMN, every company drew processes differently. One team used flowcharts, another used "swimlane diagrams", another used proprietary BPM-tool notations. A BA who moved between projects had to relearn the shapes every time. BPMN fixed that.

Three audiences, one diagram

BPMN is designed to serve three audiences from the same drawing:

  • Business people — see the flow at a glance.
  • Analysts and managers — use it to spot bottlenecks and redesign.
  • Developers — read the same diagram and build automation (many BPMN 2.0 diagrams can be executed directly by workflow engines like Camunda, Flowable, or Bonita).

As a Business Analyst, BPMN is your default tool for documenting any multi-step, multi-actor process. It becomes part of your BRD's "as-is" and "to-be" sections, part of your requirements elicitation, and part of your hand-off to developers.

Part 2

The four element families

BPMN 2.0 has over 100 distinct shapes. You don't need all of them. Four families cover the vast majority of real-world BA work.

1 · Events (circles)

Start event

Thin single-line circle. Where the process begins. There is exactly one per pool, in most cases.

Intermediate event

Double-line circle. Something happens in the middle — a timer, a received message, a signal. Common: "wait 2 business days", "receive payment confirmation".

End event

Thick single-line circle. A path terminates here. A process can have multiple end events — one per distinct outcome.

2 · Activities (rounded rectangles)

Task

A single unit of work that cannot be broken down further in this diagram. Name it with a verb + object: "Review application", "Send confirmation email", "Print receipt".

Subprocess

A collapsed group of activities — hides complexity. The + marker means you can "drill into" it for a detailed sub-diagram. Essential for keeping big processes readable.

3 · Gateways (diamonds)

A diamond is a decision point. It doesn't do work itself — it routes work. A gateway always asks a question, never performs an action. See Part 4 for the three types.

4 · Flows (arrows)

Sequence flow (solid)

"Then do this next." Stays within one pool. Cannot cross a pool boundary.

Message flow (dashed)

"I am sending a message to another organization." Used between pools. This is how a customer "talks to" a company in the diagram.

Part 3

Pools & lanes — who does what

A pool is a container representing a single organization or participant. A lane is a subdivision of a pool representing a specific role, department, or system within that organization.

ConceptRepresentsRule
PoolAn independent organization (Tourist, Hospital, Bank)Sequence flow cannot cross pool boundaries. Use message flow.
LaneA role or system inside an organization (Nurse, Doctor, EMR)Sequence flow can cross lane boundaries freely.
Black-box poolAn external participant whose internal process you don't care about (e.g. "Payment Gateway")Drawn empty — only message-flow endpoints show.

The three-question test for pools and lanes

  1. Who starts this? → that's your first lane, probably.
  2. Does anyone else have a legal/organizational boundary? → separate pool.
  3. Are any steps done by a system, not a person? → dedicated "System" lane.
Part 4

Gateways in detail

Gateways are where beginners go wrong most often. A gateway represents a decision or convergence. It must not have a verb inside. It asks a question; it never does work.

Exclusive gateway · × (XOR)

Exactly one outgoing path is taken. All paths must be mutually exclusive. Every outgoing arrow must be labeled with its condition (yes / no / other).

Example: "Application complete?" → yes / no. Only one wins.

Parallel gateway · + (AND)

All outgoing paths are taken simultaneously. Used when independent work can happen in parallel and must all complete before moving on.

Example: Application received → run background check AND verify ID documents AND check financial status — all at the same time, then merge.

Inclusive gateway · ○ (OR)

One or more outgoing paths are taken, based on conditions. Think of it as multiple independent decisions happening at one point.

Example: Notify customer → by SMS (if they opted in) OR by email (if they opted in) OR both. At least one path must be taken.

Important — split and merge must match

If you split with a parallel gateway, you must merge with a parallel gateway. Same for exclusive and inclusive. Mixing them creates deadlocks where tokens (work items) get stuck.

Worked example · Bhutan e-visa application

Let's diagram a common Bhutan process: a foreign tourist applies for an electronic visa online. The Tourism Council receives the application, an officer reviews it, and a system either issues the visa or sends a rejection.

Two pools, three lanes in Council. Parallel split runs Officer review in parallel with Interpol background check, merges, then exclusive gateway routes to approve or reject. Dashed = message flow between pools.

Reading this diagram, anyone — a sponsor, a developer, an auditor — can answer in seconds: "what starts this? what runs in parallel? what's the decision? who sends what to whom?"

Part 6

When to use BPMN (and when not)

✅ Use BPMN when…

  • Multiple actors or departments are involved
  • There are clear hand-offs between humans and systems
  • You're documenting as-is / to-be for a BRD
  • The process will be automated by a workflow engine
  • Regulators or auditors need an unambiguous flow
  • The process will be maintained and updated over years

⚠️ Prefer something else when…

  • You're sketching an algorithm — use a flowchart
  • One actor, three steps — a simple bullet list wins
  • You're showing data movement — use a Data Flow Diagram
  • You're scoping features — use a use case diagram
  • You're modeling concurrent software behavior — use an activity diagram or sequence diagram

Takeaways

Key takeaways

What to carry forward

  • BPMN is an OMG-maintained industry standard. Learn it once, use it anywhere.
  • Four element families: events · activities · gateways · flows.
  • Pools = organizations, lanes = roles. Handoffs become visible and gaps become obvious.
  • Gateways: × exclusive, + parallel, ○ inclusive. Match splits to merges.
  • Use sequence flow inside a pool and message flow between pools.
  • BPMN shines on multi-actor processes, BRD as-is/to-be, and automation-ready flows.
  • Skip it for pure algorithms, single-actor steps, or pure data-movement diagrams.
  • Collapse repeatable blocks into subprocesses to keep diagrams readable.

Recommended tools to practice: Lucidchart, draw.io (free, has BPMN stencil), bpmn.io, Camunda Modeler (free desktop app — validates your diagram against BPMN 2.0).
