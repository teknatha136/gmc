Part 1

What is a swimlane diagram

A swimlane diagram (sometimes "cross-functional flowchart" or "Rummler-Brache diagram") is a process diagram divided into parallel lanes. Each lane belongs to one actor — a person, a role, a department, or a system. Every step sits in the lane of whoever does it. When the flow crosses lane boundaries, work has been handed off.

The technique was popularized by Geary Rummler and Alan Brache in their 1990 book Improving Performance: How to Manage the White Space on the Organization Chart. The "white space" is the space between org-chart boxes — where the company's processes actually live and fail.

Two orientations

  • Horizontal (most common) — lanes are horizontal stripes; process flows left to right.
  • Vertical — lanes are vertical stripes; process flows top to bottom.

Pick one based on which reads better for your content. Long processes usually work better horizontally; few-step processes with long activity labels sometimes read better vertically.

Swimlane diagrams use the same core shapes as flowcharts (rectangles for activities, diamonds for decisions, ovals for start/end) — they just add the lane structure on top. BPMN is a more formal cousin that adds additional shape semantics (events, gateways, message flow).

Part 2

Why handoffs matter

Every time work crosses a lane boundary, three things can go wrong:

  1. It waits. The receiving party might not be ready. Work sits in a queue.
  2. Context is lost. The sender knew things the receiver doesn't. Information has to be re-elicited.
  3. Ownership becomes fuzzy. "I thought you were doing that" — the phrase every project manager has heard.

A good rule of thumb

If a customer-facing process takes a long time, count the handoffs. Most of the elapsed time is in the gaps between lanes, not in the lanes themselves. Reducing handoffs usually improves cycle time more than optimizing any single step.

A well-drawn swimlane lets you see this without calculating anything. Long arrows crossing lanes = long delays. Many crossings = fragile process. One lane with twenty boxes = an overloaded role.

Worked example · loan application

A retail customer walks into a branch in Thimphu and applies for a small personal loan. Four actors are involved: the customer, a branch officer, a credit officer, and the loan system. Here's the as-is flow.

Loan application — red dashed lines are handoffs. Five handoffs in this flow.

Questions the diagram answers at a glance:

  • The customer does one thing and then waits — everything else is on the bank.
  • The branch officer and credit officer are sequential, not parallel — verification has to finish before risk assessment starts. Is that necessary?
  • The system lane only has disbursement — could earlier steps (document verification, credit bureau check) be partially automated?
  • The return to the customer lane is one long arrow — the customer hears nothing until disbursement.
Part 4

Swimlanes + timing = bottleneck-finder

A swimlane by itself tells you who does what. Add time annotations and it tells you where the time goes. Two formats work well:

  1. Annotate each box with its typical duration (e.g. "Verify documents · 25 min").
  2. Annotate each arrow between lanes with waiting time (e.g. "2–3 business days").

Typical pattern

When you add timings to a real-world process diagram, you almost always discover the same thing: active work is a small fraction of elapsed time. A loan that "takes 5 days" might involve 90 minutes of actual work and 4.5 days of waiting in queues between handoffs. Reducing those waits is the biggest lever.

Part 6

Common mistakes

MistakeFix
More than 5 lanesGroup related roles, or split into two diagrams
Activity in the wrong lane (the lane of the person who asked for it, not the person who does it)Always place the box in the lane of the doer
No dedicated "System" lane for automated stepsAdd one — otherwise automation gets attributed to humans
No timing annotations on a process-improvement swimlaneAdd per-step duration and per-handoff waiting time
Only one lane has activitiesUse a flowchart instead — you don't need lanes
Sequence flow crossing what should be a pool boundaryIf it's actually a different organization, upgrade to BPMN with pools and message flow
Takeaways

Key takeaways

What to carry forward

  • Swimlane diagrams are flowcharts with actor lanes — one lane per actor, role, or system.
  • Every lane crossing = a handoff, and handoffs are where processes usually fail.
  • Good for 2–5 actors and mostly linear flow. More than that, upgrade to BPMN.
  • Swimlanes answer at a glance: who's overloaded, where things wait, who's idle, what can be automated.
  • Add timing annotations to turn them into a bottleneck-finder.
  • They're the most useful diagram in the BA's toolkit for process-improvement work.

Recommended tools: Lucidchart, draw.io, Miro. All of them have swimlane templates out of the box.
