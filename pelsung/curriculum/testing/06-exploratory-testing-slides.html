03 Hook
🥬 A familiar moment
Imagine this
You walk into a brand-new market in Gelephu.

No shopping list. No map. You wander, you smell things, you ask sellers, you taste a fruit you've never seen, you notice prices, you spot the queue at one stall and wonder why. By the end you know that market far better than someone who ran in with a list and ran straight back out.

That walk is exploratory testing. You're learning the product, designing the next test, and running it — all at the same time.

Part One

Scripted vs
Exploratory

Two ways of testing. Both belong on a real team.

01
05 The kitchen analogy

Scripted = follow a recipe. Exploratory = chef's tasting menu.

📋 Scripted testing
Follow the recipe

Someone wrote the test cases ahead of time. You execute step by step. The expected result is already written. Pass or fail.

  • Predictable, repeatable
  • Good for regression — "did the old stuff still work?"
  • Easy to hand to a new tester
  • Can be automated later
🍽 Exploratory testing
Cook by taste

You learn the product, decide your next test based on what you just saw, and adjust as you go. The tester is the instrument.

  • Finds surprises and unknowns
  • Good for new features and unfamiliar areas
  • Needs an experienced curious tester
  • Hard to automate — done by humans

A real team uses both. Recipes for the known. Tasting for the unknown.

06 What exploratory testing IS — and is NOT

It's structured freedom — not random clicking

✅ It IS
  • Simultaneous learning, design, and execution — all in one head, in one session
  • Guided by a charter (a small mission)
  • Time-boxed — usually 60–90 min, today we'll do 30
  • Documented — you take notes as you go
  • A skill — gets sharper with practice
❌ It is NOT
  • Ad-hoc poking with no plan or notes
  • "Just play around for a while"
  • An excuse to skip writing things down
  • A replacement for scripted regression tests
  • Random — every action is chosen for a reason

The difference between exploratory and ad-hoc is whether you can describe afterwards what you did, what you found, and what you'd test next.

07
Part Two

The Charter

A small mission card · the structure inside the freedom.

02
08 Anatomy of a charter

Four lines. Write them before you touch the product.

MissionWhat are you investigating? (One sentence — start with "Explore…")
AreasWhat's in scope, what's out of scope. (Stops you from drifting into the whole app.)
Time-boxHow long will you spend? (60–90 min is normal. We'll do 30 today.)
Risks / OraclesWhat does "broken" look like here? (How will you recognise a problem when you see one?)

An "oracle" is just your way of telling right from wrong. It can be a spec, a similar product, common sense, or "that just feels off".

09 A real charter

Charter for an mBoB account-creation flow

Charter · mBoB sign-up · phone validation

Mission Explore the mBoB account creation flow with focus on phone-number validation.

Areas Sign-up screen · OTP screen · profile creation. Out of scope: pension flows, fund transfers, KYC document upload.

Time-box 60 minutes.

Risks / Oracles Phone number with country code, leading zero, spaces, +975 vs 0097517… should be handled the same way. Comparison oracle: how does WhatsApp handle the same number?

Notice how narrow this is. One area, one focus, one hour. That's the point.

10 Session-Based Test Management (SBTM)

Track where your time actually went · the T / S / B split

A tester rarely spends 60 minutes purely testing. SBTM logs the truth so a session can be planned and trusted.

T
Test

Time spent actually running test ideas against the product.

S
Setup

Logging in, creating data, finding the right page, getting unblocked.

B
Bug investigation

Time spent reproducing or characterising a bug you just found.

At the end of the session you write: "60 min total · T 35 min · S 15 min · B 10 min · 4 issues filed". That's an SBTM session report.

Part Three

Heuristics

Mnemonics that prompt good test ideas — your safety net when staring at a blank screen.

03
12 CRUD · the simplest one

For any data the user touches, test all four

C
Create

Can you add it? What about empty, very long, special characters?

R
Read

Does it show correctly? In the list, on detail, after refresh, on another device?

U
Update

Can you edit it? Does the change persist? What if you cancel halfway?

D
Delete

Can you remove it? Is it really gone? Does anything else break — favourites, history?

Worked example — a saved biller in mBoB: Create one. Read it on home and detail. Update its nickname. Delete it. If any of these four feels weak, you've found a test idea.

13 FEW HICCUPPS · consistency checks

"Is this consistent with…?" — eleven angles

Each letter is a thing the product should be consistent with. We'll walk through five today.

H · History

Consistent with how the product behaved before. "Last week the OTP arrived in 5 seconds. Today it's 40. Why?"

I · Image

Consistent with the company's image and brand. "This error message says 'oops something broke lol' — would Bank of Bhutan really say that?"

C · Claims

Consistent with what the product or marketing claims. "The home screen says 'Pay in 2 taps' — count the taps. Is it really 2?"

C · Comparable products

Consistent with similar products users already know. "WhatsApp accepts +975 with or without a leading zero. Does this app?"

U · User expectations

Consistent with what a regular user would expect. "Tapping the Bhutan flag logo on any screen — should it go home? Most apps work that way."

The full set: F·amiliar · E·xplainable · W·orld · H·istory · I·mage · C·laims · C·omparable products · U·ser expectations · P·urpose · P·roduct · S·tandards.

14 SFDIPOT · the coverage checklist

Have I looked at every kind of thing?

Run down the list at the start (or end) of a session. Anything you skipped is a test idea.

S
Structure

What it's made of — pages, modules, files

F
Function

What it does — features, actions

D
Data

What it holds — inputs, stored info

I
Interfaces

How it talks to other things

P
Platform

Where it runs — phone, browser, OS

O
Operations

How real users actually use it

T
Time

Sequence, timing, expiry, deadlines

Quick pass for mBoB sign-up: Structure (which screens?). Function (sign-up, OTP, profile). Data (name, phone, dob, password). Interfaces (SMS gateway). Platform (Android · iPhone · slow network). Operations (do users sign up at midnight? on a borrowed phone?). Time (OTP expiry, session timeout).

15 Note-taking during exploration

Capture as you go — memory will fail you in 20 minutes

📸 Time stamp + screenshot

The moment something looks off, take a screenshot and write the time. Future-you can't reproduce what past-you didn't capture.

"14:32 · OTP screen · screenshot · timer says -3s, still counting down"
💡 Test idea backlog

A running list of "things I want to try next". Don't drop the current thread — jot it down and keep going.

"What if I rotate the phone during OTP entry?"
❓ Questions for BA / Dev

When the product does something and you don't know if it's a bug or by design — write the question, don't guess.

"Is the 5-min OTP expiry a written requirement or a guess?"
🐛 Bug seeds

Things that smell like bugs but you couldn't reproduce yet. Mark them as "needs follow-up" — don't lose them.

"Sometimes Save spinner never stops. Saw it twice. Couldn't repeat."
16 The bug-hunting mindset

Three small shifts that change everything

🐛 Assume there are bugs

Don't try to "confirm it works". Try to break it on purpose. The product wasn't built by a perfect person on a perfect day.

🏃 Learn fast

The first 10 minutes is for understanding. Click everything once. Then, slow down and dig where it felt strange.

😯 Document surprises

Anything that made you go "huh?" — even if it's not clearly a bug. Surprises are the seeds of the bugs you'll find tomorrow.

The mantra: "The product is the suspect. I am the detective. My notes are the evidence."

17 Common pitfalls

Three traps every new explorer falls into

1. "No notes needed"

You feel you'll remember. You won't. By the next morning the steps are foggy and the bug can't be reproduced.

Fix: notes during, not after. Time + screenshot + one line.
2. Ignoring the time-box

One hour turns into three. Energy drops. Last hour finds nothing because you're tired, not because the product is clean.

Fix: set a real timer. When it rings, stop and write your session report.
3. Drifting from the charter

You started on phone validation and ended up testing transfer fees. The charter said "out of scope" — and you ignored it.

Fix: when you spot something out of scope, write it as a future charter — don't follow it now.

20 Step 2–4 · the session

35 min total · 30 explore · 5 pair share

1
Write your charter (3 min)

Mission · Areas (in / out of scope) · Time-box (30 min) · Risks / Oracles. One sheet. Hand it to your pair.

2
Run the session (30 min)

Set a timer. Use CRUD or FEW HICCUPPS to spark ideas. Take notes as you go — time stamp, screenshot, test idea, question, bug seed. Track T / S / B time.

3
Log at least 3 issues or observations

Each one: where (URL or screen), what (one-line description), why it matters, screenshot if possible. They don't all need to be confirmed bugs — observations count.

4
Pair share (5 min)

With your partner: what surprised you? Did you drift from the charter? What would your next charter be? Each pair: one volunteer shares one thing in the debrief.

21 Debrief · what good looked like

Signs your session was a good one

✅ Good signs

  • You can hand someone your notes and they can re-create what you did
  • You logged at least 3 issues / observations / questions
  • Your T / S / B numbers add up to your time-box
  • You can name what you didn't get to — your next charter
  • You found at least one thing that surprised you

⚠️ Warning signs

  • "I just clicked around" — no charter, no structure
  • Notes written from memory after the session ended
  • Drifted into something completely different from the mission
  • Found nothing — and didn't note any oracles or comparison
  • 30 min became 60 — the timer was ignored

The whole point: a one-page charter and one-page notes that someone else can read and trust.

  • ✅ SBTM tracks time as T (test) / S (setup) / B (bug investigation)
  • ✅ Heuristics are mnemonics that prompt ideas — CRUD, FEW HICCUPPS, SFDIPOT
  • ✅ Take notes during, not after — time stamps, screenshots, test ideas, questions, bug seeds
