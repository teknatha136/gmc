Why this matters

"We didn't know" is not a defense. Compliance laws have teeth.

GDPR fines: up to €20M or 4% of global revenue, whichever is higher.
Bhutan DPPA: up to BTN 5M and/or imprisonment.
PCI-DSS breach: $5,000–$100,000 per month + class-action lawsuits.

And those are just the financial penalties. Reputation damage is worse, license revocation can end the business, and individuals can be personally liable.

BAs spec what's collected · QAs verify it stays protected. Both must understand the law.

04 Bhutan DPPA · the local law

What every BA in a Bhutan-facing project must know

Bhutan Data Protection & Privacy Act, 2023

  • Scope: any person or organization processing Bhutanese personal data — domestic or foreign.
  • Lawful basis required for any processing: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interest, public task, or legitimate interest.
  • Data subject rights: access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), data portability, objection, withdrawal of consent.
  • Sensitive data (health, biometric, financial, religious, political) needs explicit consent and additional safeguards.
  • Cross-border transfer requires adequacy decision or contractual safeguards.
  • Breach notification within 72 hours to the regulator and affected data subjects.
  • Penalties: up to BTN 5 million and/or imprisonment for serious violations.
  • Regulator: National Data Protection Authority (under MoITT — Ministry of Information and Transport).

BA implication: for any feature that collects, stores, or shares Bhutanese personal data, you must spec the lawful basis, the retention period, the rights mechanism (how does a user request erasure?), and the breach response.

05 GDPR · the global benchmark

Strictest of the major laws · sets the bar globally

General Data Protection Regulation (EU, in force 2018)

  • Extra-territorial — applies if you process EU residents' data, even if your company isn't in the EU.
  • Eight rights for data subjects: information, access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, automated-decision rights.
  • Six lawful bases for processing — same set as Bhutan DPPA (the latter modeled on GDPR).
  • Privacy by design and by default — enshrined in law. Must be built in from the start.
  • DPIA required for high-risk processing (large-scale profiling, sensitive data, public spaces, etc.).
  • 72-hour breach notification to supervisory authority. Same as Bhutan DPPA.
  • Penalties: up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.
  • DPO (Data Protection Officer) required for many organizations.

SELISE relevance: SELISE Group AG is in Switzerland (which mirrors GDPR) and serves EU clients. Any Pelsung-built product touching EU users falls under GDPR.

06 PCI-DSS · for anything touching card data

Industry standard, not a law · but contractually required

Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

  • Scope: anyone storing, processing, or transmitting cardholder data.
  • Six goals · 12 requirements covering network security, data protection, vulnerability management, access control, monitoring, and policy.
  • Four levels based on transaction volume — most fintechs hit Level 1 (highest scrutiny).
  • Penalties: $5,000–$100,000 per month for non-compliance + lawsuit exposure on breach.
  • Best practice: tokenize cards via a PCI-compliant gateway (Stripe, Paddle, etc.) so you never touch raw PAN — transfers most of the burden.

What this means for BAs/QAs

  • Never store the full card number unless absolutely necessary (and you're certified).
  • Never log the CVV. Anywhere. Ever.
  • Use the payment gateway's token, not the card number, for everything downstream.
  • QA: verify card data is tokenized end-to-end. Test for accidental logging in dev tools, server logs, error reports.
07 PII classification · know what you're holding

Three sensitivity tiers · different handling per tier

Low sensitivity
Identifiers
  • Name
  • Email address
  • Phone number
  • Mailing address
  • Username

Standard handling. Encrypt in transit. Don't expose unnecessarily.

Medium sensitivity
Quasi-identifiers
  • Date of birth
  • National ID / CID
  • IP address (in many jurisdictions)
  • Device IDs
  • Geolocation
  • Browsing history

Encrypt at rest. Limit access. Log who reads.

High sensitivity (special category)
Sensitive PII
  • Health / medical data
  • Biometric data (fingerprint, face)
  • Financial data (card, account)
  • Religious / political views
  • Sexual orientation
  • Children's data

Explicit consent required. Strong encryption. Audit trail. DPIA mandatory.

08 The seven principles · GDPR, mirrored everywhere

Memorize these · all major laws share them

  • 1 · Lawfulness, fairness, transparency — must have a lawful basis · users know what's happening · no dark patterns
  • 2 · Purpose limitation — collect for a specific stated purpose · don't reuse for unrelated purposes later
  • 3 · Data minimization — only collect what you actually need · "we might want it later" is not a reason
  • 4 · Accuracy — data must be correct and up-to-date · provide a way to correct it
  • 5 · Storage limitation — keep only as long as needed · then delete or anonymize · spec retention periods explicitly
  • 6 · Integrity & confidentiality (security) — encrypt at rest and in transit · access control · audit logs
  • 7 · Accountability — be able to demonstrate compliance · documentation, audits, DPIAs

When BAs spec a feature, walk through these seven principles. If any is violated, the spec needs work before development.

09 Consent · what makes it valid

Four requirements · all must be true

Valid consent is…

  • Freely given — no coercion · no "agree or you can't use the app" for non-essential processing
  • Specific — about a particular purpose · not bundled · "I consent to marketing emails" separate from "I consent to processing my payment"
  • Informed — user knows what they're agreeing to · plain language · no buried legalese
  • Unambiguous — clear affirmative action · pre-ticked boxes are NOT consent

Plus three operational requirements

  • Withdrawable — must be as easy to withdraw as to give
  • Documented — when, what, version of policy, IP/device — auditable
  • Granular — separate consent for separate purposes (analytics ≠ marketing ≠ personalization)

Dark pattern alert: "Accept all" giant button + "Manage preferences" small grey link is increasingly being ruled invalid by EU regulators. The choices must be equally prominent.

10 Breach response · the 72-hour clock

When PII leaks · fast, structured response or huge fines

The clock starts when the team becomes aware of the breach

1
Contain · stop the bleeding. Disable the leak. Rotate credentials. Lock down access.
2
Assess · what data, how many people, how serious, who has it now? Engage legal + security + DPO.
3
Notify regulator · within 72 hours of awareness. GDPR: supervisory authority. Bhutan: NDPA. With facts you have, even if incomplete.
4
Notify affected individuals · without undue delay if high risk to their rights and freedoms. Plain language. What happened, what data, what they should do.
5
Investigate root cause · how did this happen, what allowed it, what process change prevents recurrence?
6
Document everything · timeline, decisions, communications. Regulator will ask. Auditors will ask.

Cost of slow notification: the British Airways breach (2018) drew a £20M fine partly because they took 8+ weeks to confirm scope. The 72-hour clock matters.

11 Accessibility · increasingly part of compliance

WCAG 2.1 AA is the de-facto legal target

Where accessibility law applies

  • EU — European Accessibility Act (EAA) became fully enforceable June 2025. Many products must comply.
  • US — Section 508 (federal procurement) requires WCAG 2.0 AA. ADA exposure via lawsuits — over 4,000 web accessibility lawsuits in 2023 alone.
  • UK — Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018.
  • Global trend — class actions, UN convention obligations.

What this means for BAs/QAs

  • BA: spec WCAG 2.1 AA explicitly. Don't just write "must be accessible" — meaningless. Spec the level.
  • QA: use axe / WAVE / Lighthouse + keyboard / screen reader checks. Track WCAG violations as a separate bug category for compliance reporting.
  • Both: retrofitting accessibility costs 5–10× more than building it in. Bake it into AC from day one.
12 BA spec · QA verifies · both must understand

The compliance work split

BA's compliance work

  • Identify what data the feature collects (PII inventory)
  • Define the lawful basis for each piece of data
  • Spec the consent flow (granular, withdrawable, documented)
  • Spec retention period and deletion process
  • Spec the data subject rights mechanism (access, erasure, portability)
  • Run a DPIA for high-risk processing
  • Spec WCAG 2.1 AA explicitly for any UI
  • Document compliance choices in the BRD

QA's compliance work

  • Verify PII isn't logged in plain text
  • Test consent flows: granular, withdrawable, documented
  • Test data deletion: erased everywhere, not just hidden
  • Test data export: complete, machine-readable
  • Verify access controls + audit logs
  • Run accessibility tests against WCAG 2.1 AA
  • Test breach scenarios (e.g. credential rotation)
  • Review error messages — don't leak data
13 Common compliance mistakes

Avoid these · every one has cost a real company a real fine

  • "We don't have EU users" — until you do. Build for GDPR by default; you'll grow into it.
  • Logging full request bodies — including passwords, MPINs, card numbers in plain text. Massive leak risk.
  • Pre-ticked consent boxes — invalid under GDPR, DPPA, and most modern laws.
  • "We'll add deletion later" — without a delete-flow, you can't honor data-subject rights. Non-compliant from day one.
  • Storing more data than needed — every extra field is extra liability if breached.
  • Treating accessibility as nice-to-have — it's increasingly law. Build it in.
  • Sharing PII with vendors without DPAs — Data Processing Agreements are required. Many SaaS tools (Slack, Notion, etc.) need a DPA in place.
  • "It's encrypted" without specifying when — encrypted in transit only? At rest only? End-to-end? Spec it.
  • Cross-border transfer without adequacy — moving Bhutanese data to a non-adequate country requires explicit safeguards (SCCs, BCRs).
