Part 1

Why compliance has teeth

Twenty years ago, "data protection" was a polite suggestion. Today it's enforceable law in most major jurisdictions, with fines that can be a meaningful percentage of a company's annual revenue. As a BA or QA touching any product that handles personal data, you cannot opt out of knowing this material.

Law / RegulationMaximum penalty
GDPR (EU)€20M or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher
Bhutan DPPA (2023)BTN 5 million and/or imprisonment for serious violations
PCI-DSS (industry)$5,000–$100,000 per month + class-action exposure on breach
HIPAA (US health)$100–$50,000 per violation, capped at $1.9M/year per type
EAA (EU accessibility)Varies by member state; €1M+ in some countries

Beyond fines

The financial penalties are the headline, but the real cost is often elsewhere:

  • Reputation — breach headlines destroy customer trust; recovery takes years
  • License revocation — financial regulators can pull your license to operate
  • Personal liability — directors and DPOs can be personally fined or imprisoned
  • Class actions — affected users sue collectively for damages
  • Customer attrition — major contracts have compliance clauses; one breach can lose the contract

Part 3

Bhutan DPPA · the local law

The Data Protection and Privacy Act 2023 is Bhutan's first comprehensive privacy law. Closely modeled on GDPR. Establishes the National Data Protection Authority (NDPA) under the Ministry of Information and Transport.

Scope

Applies to any person or organization, in Bhutan or abroad, processing personal data of Bhutanese residents. "Processing" is defined broadly — collecting, storing, using, sharing, deleting all count.

Lawful basis required

You can't process personal data on a whim. You need one of six lawful bases:

  1. Consent — freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous
  2. Contract — necessary to fulfill a contract with the data subject
  3. Legal obligation — required by law (e.g. tax records)
  4. Vital interest — to protect someone's life
  5. Public task — government / public authority duties
  6. Legitimate interest — business need that doesn't override the data subject's rights

Data subject rights

  • Right to access — "what data do you have on me?"
  • Right to rectification — "fix this incorrect data"
  • Right to erasure — "delete my data" (right to be forgotten)
  • Right to data portability — "give me my data in a usable format"
  • Right to object — to processing for marketing, profiling, etc.
  • Right to withdraw consent — must be as easy as giving it

Sensitive data

Health, biometric, financial, religious, political, sexual data require explicit consent (not just any lawful basis) and additional safeguards.

72-hour breach notification

If a breach occurs, you must notify the NDPA within 72 hours of becoming aware, and notify affected individuals without undue delay if there's high risk to their rights.

BA implication

For any feature that collects, stores, or shares Bhutanese personal data, you must spec:

  • The lawful basis for each piece of data
  • The retention period and deletion process
  • The rights mechanism (how does a user request erasure?)
  • The breach response (who's notified, how, by when?)
  • Cross-border transfer controls if data leaves Bhutan
Part 4

GDPR · the global benchmark

The General Data Protection Regulation came into force in 2018 and immediately became the global benchmark. Most modern privacy laws (including Bhutan DPPA) are modeled on it. If you build to GDPR, you're broadly compliant elsewhere.

Extra-territoriality

GDPR applies if you process EU residents' data, even if your company isn't in the EU. A Bhutanese fintech serving even one EU resident is subject to GDPR.

Eight rights for data subjects

Same six as DPPA, plus:

  • Right to information — to know what's happening with your data, in plain language
  • Right to restriction — pause processing while disputes are resolved
  • Right not to be subject to automated decision-making — a real human must be involved in significant decisions

Privacy by design and by default

Enshrined in Article 25. Privacy must be built into the product from day one, not bolted on later. Default settings must be the most privacy-protective option.

DPIA required for high-risk processing

A Data Protection Impact Assessment must be done before:

  • Large-scale profiling
  • Processing sensitive data at scale
  • Public-space monitoring
  • New technologies with privacy implications

BAs typically own the DPIA, with input from security and legal.

Enforcement

Maximum fine: €20M or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. British Airways was fined £20M in 2020. Meta was fined €1.2 billion in 2023. The fines are real.

Part 5

PCI-DSS · for card data

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard is not a law — it's a contractual standard imposed by the card networks (Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover, JCB). But violating it can mean fines, lost ability to process cards, and class-action lawsuits.

Scope

Applies to anyone storing, processing, or transmitting cardholder data — even if just briefly.

Six goals · 12 requirements

  1. Build and maintain a secure network
  2. Protect cardholder data
  3. Maintain a vulnerability management program
  4. Implement strong access control measures
  5. Regularly monitor and test networks
  6. Maintain an information security policy

The pragmatic strategy: tokenize

Use a PCI-compliant payment gateway (Stripe, Paddle, Adyen, Razorpay) that handles cards on your behalf. They give you a token instead of the card number; you store the token. Most of the PCI burden transfers to them.

What you must NEVER do

  • Never log the full card number (PAN), the CVV/CVC, or the magnetic stripe data. Anywhere. Ever.
  • Never store the CVV after authorization. Period.
  • Never email or message card data over insecure channels
  • Never display more than the last 4 digits of a card to anyone after entry
Part 6

PII classification · three sensitivity tiers

Not all personal data is equally sensitive. Classify what you hold so you can apply appropriate safeguards.

TierExamplesRequired handling
Identifiers (low)Name, email, phone, mailing address, usernameEncrypt in transit (TLS); standard access control; don't expose unnecessarily
Quasi-identifiers (medium)Date of birth, national ID/CID, IP, device IDs, geolocation, browsing historyEncrypt at rest; limit access by role; log who reads what
Sensitive PII (high)Health, biometric, financial, religious, political, sexual orientation, children's dataExplicit consent required; strong encryption; full audit trail; DPIA mandatory; minimum-necessary principle strictly applied

Quasi-identifiers can re-identify

Three quasi-identifiers can often re-identify someone in a "anonymized" dataset. Famous study: 87% of Americans can be identified from just ZIP code + birth date + sex. Treat the combination, not just individual fields.

The seven principles · GDPR-aligned, mirrored everywhere

These seven principles appear (in some form) in GDPR, Bhutan DPPA, nFADP, and most modern privacy laws. Memorize them and walk through each one for every feature you spec.

Part 8

Consent · what makes it valid

Consent is one of the six lawful bases — but it's the most fragile. To be valid, it must meet all four of these requirements:

The four requirements

  • Freely given — no coercion. "Agree or you can't use the app" for non-essential processing makes the consent invalid.
  • Specific — about a particular purpose. Not bundled. "I consent to marketing emails" is separate from "I consent to processing my payment."
  • Informed — user knows what they're agreeing to. Plain language, no buried legalese, no jargon-only privacy policies.
  • Unambiguous — clear affirmative action. Pre-ticked boxes are NOT consent. Silence is not consent. Continuing to use the site is not consent.

Plus three operational requirements

  • Withdrawable — must be as easy to withdraw as to give
  • Documented — when consent was given, what version of the policy, what the user was shown, IP/device for audit
  • Granular — separate consent for separate purposes (analytics ≠ marketing ≠ personalization ≠ third-party sharing)

Dark pattern alert

The classic "Accept all" giant green button next to a small grey "Manage preferences" link is increasingly being ruled invalid by EU regulators. The choices must be equally prominent. Reject and accept buttons should be the same size, color, and visual weight.

What good consent looks like

  • "We'd like to use cookies for: [ ] Required (always on) [ ] Analytics [ ] Marketing"
  • "Manage preferences" and "Accept selected" buttons equal in prominence
  • "Withdraw consent" link in account settings, one click away
  • Audit log: "User X consented to analytics on 2026-04-15, policy v3.2, IP 203.0.113.4"
Part 9

Breach response · the 72-hour clock

The 72-hour notification window appears in GDPR, DPPA, and most modern laws. It starts when the team becomes aware of the breach — not when they finish investigating. You're expected to notify the regulator with what you know, even if incomplete.

  1. Contain. Stop the bleeding. Disable the leak. Rotate credentials. Lock down access.
  2. Assess. What data, how many people, how serious, who has it now? Engage legal + security + DPO.
  3. Notify regulator. Within 72 hours of awareness. GDPR: supervisory authority. Bhutan: NDPA. With facts you have.
  4. Notify affected individuals. Without undue delay if high risk to their rights and freedoms. Plain language: what happened, what data, what they should do.
  5. Investigate root cause. How did this happen? What allowed it? What process change prevents recurrence?
  6. Document everything. Timeline, decisions, communications. The regulator will ask. Auditors will ask.

Cost of slow notification

British Airways' 2018 breach drew a £20M fine partly because they took 8+ weeks to confirm scope. The 72-hour clock matters. Better to notify with incomplete information and follow up than to wait for a complete picture.

What to spec in advance

Don't wait for a breach to figure out the response. Spec it as part of every product:

  • Who is the incident commander?
  • What's the chain of decisions (legal? CEO? regulator?)
  • What are the notification templates?
  • How do we know what data was affected? (Audit logs must answer this)
  • Who handles the press?
Part 10

Accessibility · WCAG, EAA, and lawsuits

Accessibility lives at the intersection of usability and compliance. WCAG 2.1 AA is the de-facto target for most accessibility laws.

JurisdictionRequirement
European UnionEuropean Accessibility Act (EAA) — fully enforceable June 2025. Covers many products serving EU consumers.
United StatesSection 508 (federal procurement) requires WCAG 2.0 AA. ADA exposure via private lawsuits — over 4,000 web accessibility lawsuits in 2023.
United KingdomPublic Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018 (post-Brexit, still aligned with EU)
BhutanPersons with Disabilities Bill in development; international agreements imply WCAG conformance
GlobalUN Convention on Rights of Persons with Disabilities — most countries are signatories

Spec the level explicitly

"The form should be accessible" is meaningless. Spec the level:

  • Required: WCAG 2.1 AA (or 2.2 AA, the latest)
  • Required where: every user-facing flow, including error states, modals, and emails
  • Required how: tested with axe / WAVE + manual keyboard + screen reader (NVDA/VoiceOver)

Cost of building it in vs retrofitting

Industry estimates: building accessibility in from day one adds ~5% to development cost. Retrofitting an existing product can cost 5–10× that — sometimes requiring a full rewrite. Bake it into AC from day one, every project.

Part 11

BA spec · QA verifies

BA's compliance work

  • Identify what data the feature collects (PII inventory)
  • Define the lawful basis for each piece of data
  • Spec the consent flow (granular, withdrawable, documented)
  • Spec retention period and deletion process
  • Spec the data subject rights mechanism (access, erasure, portability)
  • Run a DPIA for high-risk processing
  • Spec WCAG 2.1 AA explicitly for any UI
  • Document compliance choices in the BRD

QA's compliance work

  • Verify PII isn't logged in plain text (search server logs, error reports, dev tools)
  • Test consent flows: granular, withdrawable, documented
  • Test data deletion: erased everywhere, not just hidden — including backups, caches, search indexes
  • Test data export: complete, machine-readable
  • Verify access controls + audit logs
  • Run accessibility tests against WCAG 2.1 AA
  • Test breach scenarios (e.g. credential rotation, audit-log integrity)
  • Review error messages — don't leak PII or system internals

Takeaways

Key takeaways

What to carry forward

  • Six laws to know: Bhutan DPPA · GDPR · PCI-DSS · EAA · HIPAA · nFADP. The strictest applicable one wins.
  • 72-hour breach notification is the global default. Clock starts on awareness, not on full investigation.
  • Three PII tiers: identifiers · quasi-identifiers · sensitive. Different handling per tier.
  • Seven principles: lawful · specific · minimal · accurate · time-limited · secure · accountable.
  • Valid consent: freely given · specific · informed · unambiguous — and withdrawable, documented, granular.
  • PCI-DSS: tokenize via a gateway. Never log card data. Never store CVV.
  • Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA) is increasingly compliance, not just nice-to-have. Spec the level.
  • BA specs · QA verifies. Both must understand the law.
  • Privacy by design and by default: build it in, don't bolt it on.
  • "We didn't know" is not a defense.
