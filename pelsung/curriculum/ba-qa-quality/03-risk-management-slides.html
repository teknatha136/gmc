02
Why risk thinking

"We can't test everything. So what should we test?"

Test budget is finite. Time is finite. Attention is finite.

Risk thinking gives you a framework: spend more on the things where being wrong hurts most. Spend less where it doesn't.

Risk = likelihood × impact. Two numbers. Most decisions fall out from there.

03 The risk equation · two numbers, one decision

Risk = Likelihood × Impact

  • Likelihood — how probable is this bad thing? (0–100% or Low/Med/High)
  • Impact — if it happens, how bad? (BTN, hours, customers affected, reputation, lives)
  • Risk score — multiply them. Or plot on a 3×3 / 5×5 grid. Higher = scarier.

Examples in different units

  • Server crash on Black Friday: Likelihood 30%, Impact $2M lost sales = expected loss $600k
  • SMS gateway typo in confirmation: Likelihood 90% (typo exists), Impact minor confusion = low risk
  • Wrong tax calculation: Likelihood 5%, Impact regulatory fine + reputation = high risk despite low likelihood

You won't always have numbers. High/Med/Low works for most BA/QA decisions.

05 The four risk responses

Avoid · Mitigate · Transfer · Accept

Avoid

Change the plan so the risk doesn't apply. "Don't store credit cards" eliminates the credit-card-breach risk.

Mitigate

Reduce likelihood or impact. "Add monitoring + auto-failover" reduces likelihood and impact of outages.

Transfer

Push the risk to someone else. "Use Stripe for payments" transfers PCI-DSS compliance burden to Stripe.

Accept

Live with the risk. Document the decision. "We accept the 0.1% chance of brief downtime during deploys" — done.

Mantra: not every risk needs a fix. Sometimes the right answer is to accept and move on. The wrong answer is to ignore.

07 Risk categories · don't miss any

Six families of risk — sweep through them all

  • Technical — outages, latency, capacity, integration failures, deprecations
  • Security — breaches, attacks, leaks, broken auth, vulnerable dependencies
  • Compliance / Regulatory — Bhutan DPPA, GDPR, PCI-DSS, accessibility law (WCAG/EAA), industry-specific rules
  • Operational — vendor failures, deployment problems, runbook gaps, on-call coverage
  • People — key-person dependency, attrition, knowledge silos, ramp-up time
  • Business — competitor moves, market change, funding, scope creep, schedule slip

When building a risk register, walk through these six explicitly. Most teams over-index on technical and miss compliance / people / operational.

08 Risk-based testing · QA's lens

Same risk register · different question · "where do I test most?"

The mapping

  • High-risk feature → deep functional + non-functional tests, edge cases, automation, regression every build
  • Medium-risk feature → solid functional, basic non-functional, regression on release
  • Low-risk feature → smoke tests, sanity checks, regression occasionally
  • Trivial → exploratory if time permits

Concrete example · mBoB

  • Money transfer = critical risk · test every level · automate every scenario · every build
  • View transaction history = medium · functional + perf at scale · regression each release
  • Update profile photo = low · happy-path + 1 edge case · smoke test in CI
  • Customer service phone number page = trivial · check it loads in smoke test

Without risk-based prioritization, QA tests "everything equally" and runs out of time before testing what really matters. With it, the highest-risk features get the deepest coverage.

09 Same artifact · two roles · two lenses

BA owns the register · QA reads it for test priority

BA's risk work

  • Identify risks during requirements elicitation
  • Score them with stakeholders (likelihood × impact)
  • Maintain the risk register through the project
  • Choose mitigation responses with stakeholders
  • Surface risks at every milestone review
  • Trigger the §12 (Risks) section of the BRD

QA's risk work

  • Read the risk register before designing tests
  • Map test depth to risk score
  • Identify quality risks the BA might miss (perf, a11y, data integrity)
  • Push back if a high-risk feature has thin AC
  • Track which risks are still uncovered by tests
  • Surface "we found this in testing" risks back to BA

Sync point: a quick risk review at sprint planning. BA presents the register, QA confirms test coverage matches the priorities, dev confirms mitigation work is in scope.

11 The process · how to actually do this

Five steps · run it as a workshop, not a solo exercise

  • 1 · Identify — gather the team, sweep the six categories, brainstorm risks. Use BRD assumptions, past incidents, OWASP, ISO 25010 as prompts.
  • 2 · Assess — score each one (likelihood × impact). Doesn't need to be precise — "Low/Med/High" works.
  • 3 · Prioritize — plot on the matrix. The top-right corner gets attention first.
  • 4 · Respond — pick one of the four (avoid · mitigate · transfer · accept). Document the decision.
  • 5 · Monitor — review the register at sprint planning, milestone reviews, and after every incident. Update scores. Close mitigated risks.

Cadence: create at project start. Update weekly during active development. Quarterly review for mature systems.

12 Common risk-management mistakes

Avoid these · most teams fall into at least three

  • Risk register as compliance theater — created at project start, never updated. Useless. Update it.
  • Only technical risks — missing compliance, people, business risks. Sweep all six categories.
  • Vague risks — "things could go wrong with security." Specific: "MPIN brute-force attack succeeds via missing rate limit."
  • No owner per risk — orphaned risks die unmitigated. Every risk needs a name beside it.
  • "Mitigated" without verification — mitigation is a claim until QA / audit confirms. Don't close on the dev's word.
  • QA tests without reading the register — same priorities should drive both BA's plan and QA's tests. Walk through together.
  • Ignoring low-likelihood / high-impact risks — the rare-but-catastrophic ones (data loss, security breach). They need a plan even if rare.
  • Treating risks as static — likelihoods and impacts change as the project evolves. Re-score regularly.
