Part 1

Why risk thinking matters

Software projects can never test everything, build everything, or guard against every possible failure. Time, budget, and attention are finite. Risk management is the discipline that helps you decide where to spend each of those — and where to deliberately not spend.

Without risk thinking, teams default to one of two failure modes:

  • Test everything equally — and run out of time before the most important features get covered. The truly critical bugs ship.
  • Test by gut feel — coverage depends on whoever's loudest in the room. The same bugs surface again and again because no one tracked the risks.

Risk thinking replaces both with a simple discipline: name the risks, score them, decide a response, monitor.

Part 2

The risk equation

The simplest, most useful framework in software project work:

Risk = Likelihood × Impact

Likelihood: how probable is this bad thing? (0–100%, or Low / Med / High)

Impact: if it happens, how bad? (BTN, hours, customers affected, reputation, lives, regulatory fines)

Risk score: multiply them. Or plot on a 3×3 / 5×5 grid. Higher score = scarier.

You won't always have precise numbers. High / Medium / Low is enough for most BA/QA decisions. The point is to be explicit rather than to be precise.

Examples in different units

  • Server crash on Black Friday — likelihood 30%, impact $2M lost sales = expected loss $600k. High risk.
  • Typo in SMS confirmation — likelihood 90% (typo exists), impact minor confusion = low risk despite high likelihood.
  • Wrong tax calculation — likelihood 5%, impact regulatory fine + reputation damage = high risk despite low likelihood.

Notice how the third example shows why low likelihood doesn't mean low risk. A rare event with catastrophic consequences is still scary.

Part 3

The risk matrix

The most common visualization is a 3×3 (sometimes 5×5) grid plotting likelihood against impact.

Impact: LowImpact: MediumImpact: High
Likelihood: HighMedium Watch & mitigate cheaplyHigh Mitigate or avoidCritical Stop · plan around
Likelihood: MediumLow Accept, monitorMedium Plan mitigationHigh Mitigate or transfer
Likelihood: LowTrivial Accept · move onLow AcceptMedium Plan response · don't ignore

Reading rule: top-right = drop everything, bottom-left = ignore, the diagonal middle band is where most decisions need judgement and planning.

Why a matrix beats a flat list

A flat list of "100 risks" all looks equally scary. The matrix instantly shows you which 5 to focus on. Visual ranking creates better discussions than spreadsheets do.

Part 4

The four risk responses

For every risk, you must pick one of four responses. Document the choice. The worst response is "no decision".

ResponseWhat it meansExample
AvoidChange the plan so the risk doesn't apply"Don't store credit cards" eliminates the credit-card-breach risk entirely
MitigateReduce likelihood or impact (or both)"Add monitoring + auto-failover" reduces likelihood and impact of outages
TransferPush the risk to someone else"Use Stripe" transfers PCI-DSS compliance burden from us to Stripe
AcceptLive with the risk; document the decision"We accept the 0.1% chance of brief downtime during deploys"

Mantra

Not every risk needs a fix. Sometimes the right answer is to accept and move on. The wrong answer is to ignore. The difference: an accepted risk is documented, named, owned, and visible. An ignored risk is a surprise waiting to happen.

Part 5

The risk register

The risk register is the artifact — a living spreadsheet of every known risk on the project. It becomes part of the BRD (§12 Risks). It's reviewed weekly during active development and at every milestone.

Required columns

  • ID — short reference (R-01, R-02…) so you can talk about it in meetings
  • Risk description — specific, not vague. "MPIN brute-force attack via missing rate limit" not "security risk"
  • Category — see Part 6
  • Likelihood · Impact · Score
  • Response — avoid / mitigate / transfer / accept + the action
  • Owner — a person, not a team. Single point of accountability.
  • Status — Open · In Progress · Mitigated · Accepted · Closed
Part 6

The six risk categories

When building a register from scratch, walk through these six explicitly. Most teams over-index on technical risks and miss the rest.

CategoryWhat's in itCommon omissions
TechnicalOutages, latency, capacity, integration failures, deprecations, tech debtOften the only category teams remember
SecurityBreaches, attacks, leaks, broken auth, vulnerable dependenciesSometimes lumped under "technical" — keep it separate, the response patterns differ
Compliance / RegulatoryBhutan DPPA, GDPR, PCI-DSS, accessibility law (WCAG/EAA), industry-specific (HIPAA, etc.)Often missed entirely until an audit
OperationalVendor failures, deployment problems, runbook gaps, on-call coverage"It worked in dev" — operational risks bite at deploy
PeopleKey-person dependency, attrition, knowledge silos, ramp-up timeAlmost always forgotten — until someone resigns mid-sprint
BusinessCompetitor moves, market change, funding, scope creep, schedule slipTreated as separate from "project risk" — but it isn't
Part 7

Risk-based testing · QA's lens on the same register

QAs read the BA's risk register from a different angle: "where do I test most? where do I test least?"

The mapping from risk score to test depth

  • Critical risk → deep functional + non-functional tests · all edge cases · automation · regression every build
  • High risk → solid functional + most relevant non-functional · key edge cases · automation · regression each release
  • Medium risk → standard functional · basic non-functional · regression on release
  • Low risk → smoke tests + 1-2 happy-path scenarios · regression occasionally
  • Trivial → exploratory if time permits · skip otherwise

Concrete example · mBoB

  • Money transfer = critical · test every level · automate every scenario · run every build
  • View transaction history = medium · functional + perf at scale · regression each release
  • Update profile photo = low · happy path + 1 edge case · smoke test in CI
  • Customer service phone-number page = trivial · check it loads in smoke test

The payoff

Without risk-based prioritization, QA tests "everything equally" and runs out of time before testing what really matters. With it, the highest-risk features get the deepest coverage and the lowest-risk get just enough.

Part 8

BA and QA roles in risk

BA's risk work

  • Identify risks during requirements elicitation
  • Score them with stakeholders (likelihood × impact)
  • Maintain the risk register through the project
  • Choose mitigation responses with stakeholders
  • Surface risks at every milestone review
  • Populate §12 (Risks) of the BRD

QA's risk work

  • Read the risk register before designing tests
  • Map test depth to risk score
  • Identify quality risks the BA might miss (perf, a11y, data integrity)
  • Push back if a high-risk feature has thin AC
  • Track which risks are still uncovered by tests
  • Surface "we found this in testing" risks back to BA

Sync point

Run a quick risk review at sprint planning. BA presents the register, QA confirms test coverage matches the priorities, dev confirms mitigation work is in scope. 15 minutes saves weeks.

Part 9

Worked example · mBoB v4 release

Take the seven risks from the table above and walk through them as a team would in a planning session.

R-02 · MPIN brute-force attack — Critical

Likelihood: High (mobile banking is a known target). Impact: High (account compromise + reputation damage + regulatory).

Response: Mitigate. Implement 3-attempt lockout, rate limiting, and exponential backoff. Add audit logging.

QA implication: Test all attack scenarios (10 wrong attempts, race conditions on the lockout, rapid retries). Automate as part of CI.

R-01 · Payment gateway outage — High

Likelihood: Medium (third-party dependency). Impact: High (no payments = service down).

Response: Mitigate. Add fallback gateway. Add circuit breaker. Add user-visible "try again" with retry counter.

QA implication: Chaos test the gateway. Verify graceful degradation. Confirm no double-charges on retry.

R-05 · Bhutan DPPA non-compliance — Medium (low likelihood, high impact)

Likelihood: Low (we're not actively non-compliant). Impact: High (fines, license risk).

Response: Mitigate. Run a Data Protection Impact Assessment. Implement consent flows. Document the data lifecycle.

QA implication: Test consent UX. Verify data deletion requests work. Verify access logs.

R-06 · Build server runs out of disk — Low

Likelihood: Medium. Impact: Low (CI fails, we get alerted, we add disk).

Response: Accept + monitor. Configure disk-space alerts at 80%.

QA implication: None. Not a feature to test.

Part 10

The five-step process

  1. Identify. Gather the team, sweep the six categories, brainstorm risks. Use BRD assumptions, past incidents, OWASP, ISO 25010 as prompts.
  2. Assess. Score each one (likelihood × impact). Doesn't need to be precise — Low/Med/High works.
  3. Prioritize. Plot on the matrix. The top-right corner gets attention first.
  4. Respond. Pick one of the four (avoid · mitigate · transfer · accept). Document the decision.
  5. Monitor. Review the register at sprint planning, milestone reviews, and after every incident. Update scores. Close mitigated risks. Add new ones.

Cadence

  • Create at project start (workshop with BA + Eng Lead + key stakeholders)
  • Update weekly during active development
  • Review at every milestone / release
  • Quarterly review for mature systems
  • After every incident: did we have this in the register? If not, add it.
Part 11

Common mistakes

MistakeFix
Risk register as compliance theater — created at start, never updatedMake it a weekly artifact. Review in standup or sprint planning.
Only technical risks — missing compliance, people, businessSweep all six categories every time
Vague risks ("things could go wrong with security")Be specific: "MPIN brute-force succeeds via missing rate limit"
No owner per risk — orphans die unmitigatedEvery risk has a named person beside it
"Mitigated" without verificationMitigation is a claim until QA / audit confirms; don't close on dev's word
QA tests without reading the registerSame priorities should drive both BA's plan and QA's tests
Ignoring low-likelihood / high-impact risksThe rare-but-catastrophic ones (data loss, breach) need a plan even if rare
Treating risks as staticLikelihoods and impacts change as the project evolves; re-score regularly
Confusing risk register with bug trackerRisks are potential problems. Bugs are actual problems. Keep them separate.
Takeaways

Key takeaways

What to carry forward

  • Risk = Likelihood × Impact. Two numbers drive most decisions.
  • Use the 3×3 matrix. Top-right = critical. Bottom-left = trivial. Diagonal = judgement.
  • Four responses: avoid · mitigate · transfer · accept. Document the choice.
  • The risk register is the artifact: ID, risk, category, scores, response, owner, status.
  • Six categories: technical · security · compliance · operational · people · business. Sweep all six.
  • BA owns the register · QA reads it for test priority. Same artifact, two views.
  • Process: identify → assess → prioritize → respond → monitor.
  • You can't test everything. Test what matters most.
